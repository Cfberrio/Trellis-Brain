## Purpose
Brand context layer for running the `copy-editing` skill on DR scripts (social video, reels, carousels, voiceover). Not a new skill. Not a generic brand summary. This file exists so future Claude runs can diagnose and revise a DR script with the brand's voice, hook logic, CTA logic, and recurring correction patterns already loaded — before the editing engine touches a single line.

## How Claude should use this note
- Read this note first, before touching the script.
- Then apply the `copy-editing` skill as the evaluation framework (clarity → voice → so what → proof/specificity → emotional resonance → low-friction CTA).
- Diagnose before revising. Name what is weak and why.
- Preserve DR voice. Do not rewrite into a generic coach/youth-sports tone.
- Apply the historical correction patterns below instead of inventing new ones.
- Avoid blind rewrites. If a script is already on voice, fix the weakest element, not the whole thing.

## What this brand is trying to do through scripts
Sell parents on a method, not a vibe. DR scripts exist to show that development, identity, and confidence are the outputs of a structured coaching system — and that parents can trust this system because it is intentional, taught, and supervised. Scripts should make parents want to stay close for the season, not just like the post.

## Audience orientation
- Primary: parents of kids 6–12, especially those who are logistics-overwhelmed, new to sports, or skeptical of youth sports quality.
- Secondary: school gatekeepers and coaches (rarely the addressee of public scripts, but voice must not violate how they are treated elsewhere).
- Kids are never the direct audience of scripts. They are the subject.

## Voice summary
- Parent-facing: warm, organized, trustworthy. Confidence through clarity, not hype.
- Coach-truth cadence: direct, grounded, specific. Tactical + reflective in the same beat.
- Never motivational fluff. Never "unleash potential." Never elite-athlete positioning.
- Short lines. Mobile-scan rhythm. Fragments and line breaks are part of the voice.
- Spanish micro-cues (e.g. "Sin conexión, no hay compromiso.") are acceptable when used as reinforcement, not novelty.

## Script evaluation priorities
Evaluate DR scripts in this order:
1. Is the hook parent-relevant or coach-truth? If generic, it fails before anything else.
2. Does the script teach something specific (method, drill logic, developmental pattern)? "So what" must land.
3. Is the voice developmental, not motivational? Teaching over yelling, method over luck.
4. Is there a clear moment of proof (observation, quote, small specific example)?
5. Does the CTA give the parent one low-friction next step that fits DR's season-long relationship?
6. Would the parent feel the coach is intentional, present, and aligned with them?

## Hook rules
DR hooks work when they create parent-facing tension or reveal a coaching truth parents normally do not see. Patterns proven to work:
- Overheard or witnessed micro-moment: "A kid said something last practice that hit me way harder than I expected."
- Parent-facing tension: "Most parents assume practice is practice."
- Method reveal: "Week 1 decides the season."
- Counter-intuitive frame: "Hard doesn't mean wrong. Hard often means growth is happening."
- Naming a hidden risk: "Your child's development shouldn't depend on chance."
- Coach confession/truth: "Not gonna lie… this was NOT the plan."

Hook rules:
- Lead with specificity (a player, a drill, a quote, a week).
- Never lead with generic "As a parent…" or "As a coach…" openers without a specific beat attached.
- Do not open with motivational quotes. Do not open with brand-about-brand statements.
- First 2 lines must pass the "would a parent keep scrolling?" test.

## CTA rules
DR CTAs are relationship-building, not conversion-pushing. The season is the product. The CTA is the invitation to stay close to the system.

Approved CTA patterns:
- "Stay close this season."
- "Follow this team through the rest of the season."
- "Keep watching."
- "Save this for Week 1." / "Save this."
- "Share with a parent starting a season." / "Share with a parent."
- "Comment [keyword]" — only when there is a clear payoff on the other side (PDF, parent guide, dashboard access). Do not use it for no reason.

CTA rules:
- One CTA per script. Primary + optional secondary share CTA is allowed, but never stacked asks.
- Never close with a generic "Learn more" or "DM us" without a concrete reason.
- Never use hard-sell registration pressure inside a developmental script. Re-enrollment asks live in end-of-season communications, not top-of-funnel video.
- CTA must match the emotional altitude of the script. A quiet observation script does not end on a loud "SIGN UP NOW."

## Common script mistakes to catch
- Hook is too generic ("As parents, we all want the best for our kids").
- Script sounds motivational instead of developmental ("Believe in your child!") — DR is teaching, not cheerleading.
- Coaching differentiation is unclear — the script could have been written by any youth sports brand.
- Parent relevance missing — script talks about the kid's experience but never gives the parent something to do, notice, or ask.
- No proof beat — claims that DR coaches are "intentional" with no observation, quote, or example to back it.
- CTA is soft or absent — ends on a feeling instead of an invitation.
- Elite-athlete framing leaks in — "championship mindset," "next-level athlete." DR is beginner-safe and fun-first.
- "Keeping kids busy" language — never. DR is explicitly the opposite.
- Over-indexing on yelling, intensity, or grind. DR coaches teach; they don't bark.
- Yelling-coded verbs ("dominate," "crush it," "grind"). Replace with teaching-coded verbs ("teach," "repeat," "explain," "watch for," "lock in").
- Over-long setup before the payoff — parents are on mobile.
- Stacked CTAs (save + share + comment + follow) — dilutes action.

## What strong scripts usually do
- Open on a specific observed moment (a practice, a quote, a uniform handoff, a week label).
- Name the method or principle the moment reveals.
- Reframe a parent assumption ("Most parents assume… but actually…").
- Land on a short coach-truth line with line breaks.
- Close with a season-long invitation ("Stay close this season").
- Keep sentences short and scan-friendly. Fragments are welcome.
- Treat parents as partners in the system, not spectators.

## What weak scripts usually do
- Open on brand statements or generic motivation.
- List values instead of showing them in a moment.
- Use hype language ("unleash," "transform," "dominate," "unlock potential").
- Talk to the kid instead of the parent.
- End without a next step or with a next step that does not fit the season relationship.
- Sound like a generic youth sports ad.
- Confuse teaching with yelling, or discipline with strictness.
- Position DR as elite/club when the real positioning is beginner-safe, fun-first, method-led.

## Historical correction patterns
Reusable corrections observed across DR content inventories, brand core, and communication rules:

- Method over luck. Whenever a script implies "good coaches just care more," correct to structured process: blocked / serial / retrieval practice, supervised coaching, repeatable system.
- Teaching over yelling. Replace intensity-coded language with teaching-coded language ("we explain," "we slow down," "we repeat").
- Development over hype. If the script ends on an emotional promise with no mechanism, add the mechanism.
- Parent alignment is the product. If a script only talks about what happens on the field, add the parent-side beat ("ask them what their coach's name is," "don't pull them out — ask one question").
- Identity before performance. Moments of belonging (uniform day, team name, chant) are DR's strongest emotional beats. Keep them. Do not bury them in stats.
- Slow down early, speed up later. When a script rushes to a payoff, re-sequence so the "quiet shift" arrives before the big claim.
- Intentionality beats volume. Rewrite loud, generic lines into specific, quiet ones ("we probably say 'go' 500+ times," not "we motivate kids every day").
- Reasoning, not just repetition. Advanced-player beats must include the "why this skill / why this movement" layer.
- Coach-as-teacher framing. When a coach appears in a script, show them explaining, supervising, or observing — not just cheering.
- Keep the season frame. Most CTAs should tie back to a season-long relationship, not a single transaction.

## Brand-specific keyword anchors
Use these as retrieval anchors when scanning or tagging scripts. No fluff.

- development, method, structure, system, supervised
- teaching, explain, repetition, reasoning, attention
- discipline, confidence, resilience, patience, belief
- identity, belonging, uniform, team, culture
- alignment, parent dashboard, communicate, together
- beginner-safe, fun-first, on-campus, after-school, season
- slow down, lock in, stay close, save this, share with a parent
- avoid: unleash, transform, dominate, elite, next-level, just busy, luck

## Output contract for future script evaluation
When Claude evaluates a DR script using this note plus the `copy-editing` skill, the response must return:

1. **Diagnosis** — what is weak and why, mapped to the script evaluation priorities above.
2. **Historical rule applied** — which correction pattern(s) from this note are being used.
3. **Revised script** — full rewrite or targeted rewrite, on voice, mobile-scan rhythm preserved.
4. **CTA** — exactly one primary CTA (plus optional share CTA), chosen from the approved patterns.
5. **Editor notes** — short bullets on what was cut, what was kept, and why.
6. **B-roll notes (optional)** — only if the script implies visual beats (uniform moment, drill, quiet shift, parent observation).
