TENNIS SIMON SAYS
Setup:
Players stand on one side of the court along the baseline.
Feet are shoulder-width apart.
Dominant hand on the grip, secondary hand on the neck
of the racket.
Players start upright and relaxed, holding their rackets.
Game Rules:
The coach (acting as "Simon") gives commands, but
players should only follow if the command starts with 
"Simon says...".
If the coach gives a command without saying "Simon says"
and a player follows it, that player is eliminated.
Commands:
"Simon says, Ready!": Players quickly move into the ready
position (feet wide, knees bent, racket prepared).
"Simon says, Back!": Players return to their upright,
relaxed position.
"Simon says, Left!" or "Right!": Players shuffle laterally to
the left or right while maintaining the ready position.
"Simon says, Ball!": Coach simulates hitting a ball to one
side, and players move in that direction as if preparing to
hit it.
"Simon says, Split Step!": Players perform a small jump or 
split step, preparing for a shot.
"Simon says, Forehand!": Players move into forehand
position:
Turn the body toward the dominant hand side.

PLAYER CUES
What is a Serve?
A serve is the first shot used to start a point. It's performed
from behind the baseline and is crucial for gaining control
of the rally. A well-executed serve can disrupt the
opponent's rhythm or even win the point outright.
Grip
Use the Continental Grip: This grip is essential for serving,
as it allows for versatility and control, enabling you to apply
spin and power. The racket should be held as if you're
holding a hammer, with the face slightly angled to impart
spin when needed.
Footwork
For the Serve:
Preparation: Start with your feet shoulder-width apart, with
your non-dominant foot pointing toward the net.
Step Forward: As you swing to serve, step forward with
your back foot, transferring your weight to generate power.
Common Mistakes to Watch For:
Not stepping into the serve with your back foot, which
results in a loss of power.
Failing to shift weight forward when serving, reducing the
serve's effectiveness.
Over-rotating or swinging too hard, leading to loss of
control and accuracy.
Incorrect grip adjustments, causing inconsistent serves.
Confusing hand or arm movements, which can affect the
serve's spin and direction.

Understanding the Serve:
The serve in tennis is one of the most technically challenging shots
because the player has full control over the shot from start to finish.
Mastering the serve allows players to set the tone for the point.
Foot Positioning:
Stance:
For right-handed players: Place your left foot diagonally facing the
left net post, while your right foot should be parallel to the baseline,
facing sideways.
For left-handed players: Place your right foot facing the right net
post, with your left foot positioned sideways.
Alignment: Ensure the back foot's toe is aligned with the front foot's
heel for stability.
Racquet and Ball Positioning:
Hold the racquet as if you were about to scratch your back, with the
elbow pointing up and racquet behind your head.
Ball Toss: Lift the ball with your non-dominant hand and toss it slightly
above your head. Swing the racquet up to meet the ball at its peak.

Teaching the Overhead Smash
Focus on Technique: The overhead smash requires precise
technique, as players often mistake it for a regular forehand.
Grip: Use the Continental Grip. Place the 8th pebble of the
grip against the hard part of your index finger.
Racket Position: Bring the racket behind your head, as if
scratching your back.
Ball Tracking: Point your non-dominant hand at the ball to
maintain focus and accuracy.
Contact Point: Decide whether to hit the ball in the air or let it
bounce before smashing.
Overhead Smash Drill
Player Line-Up:
Have players line up at the baseline.
Execution:
The coach feeds a lob to each player, one at a time.
Depending on the height of the lob, instruct players to
either hit the ball in the air or let it bounce first.
Target Practice:
Set targets on the opposite side of the court for players to
aim at when hitting their overhead smash.
Objective: This drill helps players develop accuracy, power, and
confidence in executing the overhead smash.

GAME OF THE DAY (10 MIN)
In the Game of the Day for forehands, players will compete in a fun, competitive
format that emphasizes backhand + forehand stroke development.
KING / QUEEN OF THE COURT WITH SERVES
1. Set Up:
Choose one or two players to be the king/queen on one side
of the net.
The rest of the players line up on the opposite side of the
court.
2. Challenger Rotation:
Players from the opposite side challenge the king/queen one at
a time by playing a point against them.
3. Winning Conditions:
First Point: If the challenger wins the first point, they earn the
right to play a second point against the king/queen.
Second Point: If the challenger wins two consecutive points,
they become the new king/queen.
4. Automatic Victory:
If a challenger hits a winner (a shot that the king/queen cannot
reach or return) during the first point, they immediately
become the new king/queen, skipping the need for a second
point.
5. Repeat:
Continue the drill, with challengers rotating through the line to
take on the reigning king/queen.
The game keeps going until a new king/queen is crowned or
the drill time ends.

HANGMAN
Setup:
Line players up behind the baseline on one side of the
court.
Players will take turns hitting the skill of the day (e.g.,
backhand) or previously learned skills.
Game Rules:
Objective: Players must hit the ball over the net and within
the court boundaries.
Body Parts: If a player succeeds, they "lose" a body part
(start with one arm, then a leg, etc.). The progression
continues until they are hitting from the ground.
If a player misses their last attempt (after all body parts are
"lost"), they go to the other side of the net, also known as 
jail (or become a shark or go into detention).
Escaping Jail:
To escape from jail, players must catch a ball, either after 
one bounce or in the air.
Winning Condition:
Players continue until only one player remains, or the coach
decides to end the game. Those who avoid missing shots
and "losing body parts" will keep going.
