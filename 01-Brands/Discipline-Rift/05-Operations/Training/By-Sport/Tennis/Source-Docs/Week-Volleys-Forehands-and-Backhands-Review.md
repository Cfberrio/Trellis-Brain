TENNIS SIMON SAYS
Setup:
Players stand on one side of the court along the baseline.
Feet are shoulder-width apart.
Dominant hand on the grip, secondary hand on the neck
of the racket.
Players start upright and relaxed, holding their rackets.
Game Rules:
The coach (acting as "Simon") gives commands, but
players should only follow if the command starts with 
"Simon says...".
If the coach gives a command without saying "Simon says"
and a player follows it, that player is eliminated.
Commands:
"Simon says, Ready!": Players quickly move into the ready
position (feet wide, knees bent, racket prepared).
"Simon says, Back!": Players return to their upright,
relaxed position.
"Simon says, Left!" or "Right!": Players shuffle laterally to
the left or right while maintaining the ready position.
"Simon says, Ball!": Coach simulates hitting a ball to one
side, and players move in that direction as if preparing to
hit it.
"Simon says, Split Step!": Players perform a small jump or 
split step, preparing for a shot.
"Simon says, Forehand!": Players move into forehand
position:
Turn the body toward the dominant hand side.

TENNIS TAG
Explain:
Define the boundaries on the court within which the game
will be played.
Select one player to start as "it," holding a tennis ball.
Gameplay:
The player who is "it" must tag other players by touching
them with the volleyball (no throwing, just a soft touch).
Players must stay within the boundaries; if anyone steps
out, they become "it."
The tagged player then becomes "it" and continues the
game.
Team Variation:
If there are many players, divide them into two teams:
Chasers: A team of players with volleyballs who try to
tag the runners.
Runners: The other team, without volleyballs, must
avoid being tagged.
The chasers have a set time (e.g., 30 seconds) to tag as
many runners as possible. After time is up, teams switch
roles.
Purpose: 
Develop agility, quickness, and spatial awareness while
adding an element of fun and teamwork.

PLAYER CUES
What is a Volley?
A volley is a shot hit before the ball bounces, typically
performed at the service line or closer to the net. It's an
aggressive move designed to disrupt the opponentʼs
rhythm or win the point quickly.
Grip
Use the Continental Grip: The racket should be held
perpendicular to the ground, with the face slightly pointing
to the sky. This allows for control and the ability to impart
backspin.
Footwork
Forehand Volley: Step forward with your opposite foot (left
foot for right-handed players).
Backhand Volley: Step forward with the same foot (right
foot for right-handed players).
Common Mistakes to Watch For:
Not stepping forward with the opposite foot during a
forehand volley.
Failing to move forward towards the ball while volleying.
Bringing the racket back and swinging instead of using a
short, controlled punch.
Overhitting or smashing the ball instead of maintaining
control.
Confusing hand movements during the volley, leading to
improper execution.

OVERHAND SMASH ADVANCED
Teaching the Overhead Smash
Focus on Technique: The overhead smash requires precise
technique, as players often mistake it for a regular forehand.
Grip: Use the Continental Grip. Place the 8th pebble of the
grip against the hard part of your index finger.
Racket Position: Bring the racket behind your head, as if
scratching your back.
Ball Tracking: Point your non-dominant hand at the ball to
maintain focus and accuracy.
Contact Point: Decide whether to hit the ball in the air or let it
bounce before smashing.
Overhead Smash Drill
Player Line-Up:
Have players line up at the baseline.
Execution:
The coach feeds a lob to each player, one at a time.
Depending on the height of the lob, instruct players to
either hit the ball in the air or let it bounce first.
Target Practice:
Set targets on the opposite side of the court for players to
aim at when hitting their overhead smash.
Objective: This drill helps players develop accuracy, power, and
confidence in executing the overhead smash.

HANGMAN
Setup:
Line players up behind the baseline on one side of the
court.
Players will take turns hitting the skill of the day (e.g.,
backhand) or previously learned skills.
Game Rules:
Objective: Players must hit the ball over the net and within
the court boundaries.
Body Parts: If a player succeeds, they "lose" a body part
(start with one arm, then a leg, etc.). The progression
continues until they are hitting from the ground.
If a player misses their last attempt (after all body parts are
"lost"), they go to the other side of the net, also known as 
jail (or become a shark or go into detention).
Escaping Jail:
To escape from jail, players must catch a ball, either after 
one bounce or in the air.
Winning Condition:
Players continue until only one player remains, or the coach
decides to end the game. Those who avoid missing shots
and "losing body parts" will keep going.

KING / QUEEN OF THE COURT ADVANCED
1. Set Up:
Choose one or two players to be the king/queen on one side
of the net.
The rest of the players line up on the opposite side of the
court.
2. Challenger Rotation:
Players from the opposite side challenge the king/queen one at
a time by playing a point against them.
3. Winning Conditions:
First Point: If the challenger wins the first point, they earn the
right to play a second point against the king/queen.
Second Point: If the challenger wins two consecutive points,
they become the new king/queen.
4. Automatic Victory:
If a challenger hits a winner (a shot that the king/queen cannot
reach or return) during the first point, they immediately
become the new king/queen, skipping the need for a second
point.
5. Repeat:
Continue the drill, with challengers rotating through the line to
take on the reigning king/queen.
The game keeps going until a new king/queen is crowned or
the drill time ends.

RALLY ADVANCED
Set Up:
Choose one player to be on one side of the net.
The rest of the players line up on the opposite side.
Challenger Rotation:
Players take turns rallying with the player on the other side
of the net, one at a time.
Objective:
Each player attempts to keep the rally going for as long as
possible with the player on the opposite side.
Winning Condition:
The player who manages to sustain the rally for the longest
number of hits wins the round.
Repeat:
Continue the drill, rotating through the line of challengers.
Players can switch sides after each rally.

players understand and apply these lessons both on and off the field.
Introduce the Word of the Week:
Begin by introducing a key life lesson or value as the "Word of the
Week." Choose a word that is relevant to sports and personal
development, such as "Integrity," "Resilience," or "Responsibility."

Provide a brief explanation of what the word means and how it applies
to both sports and life. For example:
Word: Integrity
Explanation: "Integrity means doing the right thing, even when no
one is watching. In sports, itʼs about being honest, playing by the
rules, and respecting your opponents. In life, integrity is about being
true to your values and making good choices, even when it's hard."
Discuss the Life Lesson:
Engage the players in a brief discussion to help them connect with the
concept:
Ask: "Why is integrity important in sports? Can anyone give an
example of a situation where they had to show integrity?"
Share a personal or famous example that demonstrates the
importance of the word.
Assign a Task:
Ask the players to think about the word during the week and find
examples in their own lives or in the stories they read or hear about.
They should come back next week ready to:
Share their understanding of the word.
Give an example of how they or someone they know demonstrated
this life lesson in sports or everyday life.
Wrap-Up:
Encourage players to practice the life lesson throughout the week, both
in their sports activities and in their personal lives. Remind them that
these lessons help them become better athletes and better people.
This activity emphasizes the importance of applying sports values to real-life
situations, fostering personal growth and character development in young
athletes.
