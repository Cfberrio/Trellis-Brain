WHY DR PRACTICE?

What DR Practice Is (and Is Not)
DR Practice is not:
A specific drill
A "game-only" practice
Letting players just scrimmage and "figure it out"
DR Practice is:
A sequenced learning structure designed to move athletes from knowing
what to do →  recognizing when to do it →  executing it under pressure
Built around the cognitive cycle: See →  Decide →  Act
The reason practices transfer to matches instead of stalling at execution-only
reps
The Core Structural Idea

### Page 2

DR Practice works because decision-making is layered on top of skill, not
substituted for it.
The structure respects three truths of learning:
Players canʼt decide well about skills they donʼt yet control
Execution breaks down when decisions are introduced too early
Game performance requires decision-making under constraint
Thatʼs why DR Practice is not random—it is progressive.
Where DR Practice Lives Inside a Session
DR Practice is intentionally placed after skill stabilization but before full chaos.
In the Discipline Rift Universal Practice Structure DRUPS, DR learning only
occurs in two blocks:
Block 5  DR Teaching Guided Decisions)
Block 6  Application Constraint Game)
Everything before that exists to prepare the nervous system and skill base for
decisions.

### Page 5

Block 1  Culture + Belonging
Creates psychological safety →  players take risks later
Block 2  Retrieval Warm-Up
Reactivates prior learning →  prevents "cold starts"
Block 3  Teaching Block
Clarifies what right looks like →  shared mental model
Block 4  Skill Acquisition Reps Engine
Stabilizes mechanics →  frees attention for decisions later
Without these, DR becomes guessing instead of learning.
Block 5: Guided Decision–Response (The Teaching
Bridge)
Purpose:
Teach how to decide, not just what happened.
Structural features:
Decisions are limited
Cues are named and coached
Feedback happens between reps
Environment is manipulated, not chaotic
This is where players learn things like:
"If the pass is off the net, what changes?"
"When the blocker commits, what option opens?"
They are deciding, but inside a protected framework.

### Page 6

Test whether decisions survive speed, pressure, and emotion.
Structural features:
Fewer stoppages
Real scoring consequences
Constraints replace verbal reminders
Players self-regulate decisions
This answers the real question:
"Can they still choose the right action when it feels like a match?"
Why DR Practice Fails Without Structure
Common mistakes:
Jumping to games too early
Calling everything "game-like"
Adding decisions before skills are stable
Coaching outcomes instead of cues
The structure prevents these by sequencing cognitive load.
The Structural Payoff
When DR Practice is structured correctly:
Skills transfer
Players adapt instead of freeze
Teams improve decision quality without over-coaching
Coaches talk less, players solve more
