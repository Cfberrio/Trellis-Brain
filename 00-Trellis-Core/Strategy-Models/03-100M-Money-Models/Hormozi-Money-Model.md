# Hormozi — Money Model

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Defines the central object of the book and its scaling threshold. A Money Model is a deliberate sequence of offers, not a single offer. The book's pricing of the threshold — *make more profit from one customer in 30 days than it costs to acquire and service them* — is what removes cash as a constraint on growth. Frames every other note in this folder.

## Core Idea
Most businesses fail not because they can't get customers but because **getting a customer costs more than the customer pays in a useful timeframe**. Cash dribbles in over years; advertising bills come in monthly. The fix is not better products or cheaper ads — it is engineering the customer's first 30 days to return more cash than the acquisition + delivery cost. That requires more than one offer at more than one moment, sequenced. Hormozi calls that sequence a Money Model.

The implicit principle (later branded by Hormozi as Client Financed Acquisition / CFA): your existing customers should pay for your next customers. When they do, you can scale advertising as fast as you can operationally absorb it.

## Framework / Structure

### The threshold (three tiers)
1. **Bare-minimum Money Model** — first-30-day profit from one customer covers the cost to acquire + service that one customer. Business survives.
2. **Good Money Model** — first-30-day profit from one customer pays for that customer plus at least one more. Business compounds.
3. **$100M Money Model** — first-30-day profit from one customer pays for that customer plus *many* more. Cash stops being a scaling constraint. Advertising spend becomes the only ceiling.

### The three stages
Money Models grow alongside the business. Each stage must work reliably before the next is added.
- **Stage I — Get Cash.** Attraction Offers. Turn strangers into customers; cover acquisition cost.
- **Stage II — Get More Cash.** Upsell + Downsell Offers. Maximize 30-day cash from people already in the door (yeses and nos alike).
- **Stage III — Get The Most Cash.** Continuity Offers. Lock in recurring revenue and one final 30-day cash bump (prepay upsells on continuity).

### The four offer types
Each type has a distinct job. They compose; they do not substitute for each other.
1. **Attraction Offers** — turn strangers into customers. Five mechanisms documented at [[Hormozi-Attraction-Offers|Attraction Offers]].
2. **Upsell Offers** — get yeses to spend more. Four mechanisms at [[Hormozi-Upsell-Offers|Upsell Offers]].
3. **Downsell Offers** — get nos to become yeses. Three mechanisms at [[Hormozi-Downsell-Offers|Downsell Offers]].
4. **Continuity Offers** — keep them buying. Three mechanisms at [[Hormozi-Continuity-Offers|Continuity Offers]].

The taxonomy is exhaustive in Hormozi's framing for the first 30 days: every offer in a Money Model belongs to one of these four roles by where it sits in the customer journey.

## Strategic Implications

- **Speed of cashback is a structural variable, not a tactic.** A business that recovers acquisition cost in 30 days and a business that recovers it in 24 months can be equally "profitable per customer" yet have radically different growth ceilings. The slow one needs cash on hand to grow; the fast one finances itself. The whole book is engineering for the fast one.
- **One offer is not a business — it's a front end.** If a company has only one thing to sell, it has not yet decided how it makes money. Profit usually lives in the second, third, fourth offers (the McDonald's fries-and-Coke case). The first offer's job is often to *qualify* a buyer; the rest of the model monetizes them.
- **The four types are role-based, not product-based.** The same product can sit at different slots in different models. A continuity subscription can be the Attraction Offer in one model and the Continuity Offer in another. What classifies an offer is *when in the sequence it appears and what job it does there*, not what it contains.
- **Stages are sequential because operations break before models do.** A "finished" Money Model installed in a fragile business will collapse the business. Each stage stresses different parts of operations (delivery, sales, support, retention). Reliability at each stage gates the next.
- **The 30-day window is deliberate.** It is short enough to expose acquisition-economic problems immediately and long enough to fit a meaningful sequence of offers. It also matches monthly ad billing cycles — i.e., the cash math has to close inside the same window the cash leaves.

## Practical Use

Diagnostic for any existing business:
1. **What is the 30-day profit per acquired customer?** If unknown, that's the first thing to measure. If known and negative, you have an Attraction Offer + Upsell problem (Stage I/II), not a marketing problem.
2. **Which of the four offer slots is empty?** Most cash-constrained businesses have an Attraction Offer and nothing else.
3. **Which slot is leaking?** A full Money Model that misses 30-day target usually has a weak Stage II (no upsell) or no downsell capture of nos.
4. **Build order, not parallel build.** Pick the next empty or weakest slot. Make it work reliably before touching the next. Patience measured in quarters, not weeks.

## Notes / Limits

- Hormozi's seven "important rules" gating ethical use of the playbook (book pp. 23–25): all businesses have Money Models; some Money Model offers fit some businesses better than others; refund on request, no haggling; never hard-sell; comply with advertising law (especially around "free"); be transparent (don't lie about facts); any offer can be used standalone in any order. These constrain the playbook — they do not change the architecture.
- The 30-day window is a target, not an iron rule. Some industries (e.g., subscription software with long sales cycles) cannot literally hit it. The principle — *recover acquisition cost faster than you spend it* — translates; the calendar does not.
- The book deliberately does not address pricing strategy from first principles or value construction. Those live in `$100M Offers`. The Money Model assumes you already have something worth buying; it engineers how you sell it.
