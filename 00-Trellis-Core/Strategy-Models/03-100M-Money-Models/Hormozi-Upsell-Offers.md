# Hormozi — Upsell Offers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Stage II of a Money Model. Whatever you offer next. Upsells extract the bulk of 30-day profit from the customers Stage I delivered. The first sale rarely makes the money; upsells do.

## Core Idea
Solving a problem reveals the next problem. A customer who just bought your thing is in a measurably higher-buying state — they have committed; their buying machinery is on; the next offer faces dramatically less resistance than the first. The McDonald's principle: $0.25 burger profit becomes $3.00 with three sequenced upsells, an 11.6× multiplier. Without upsells, the burger business doesn't exist.

Upsells take three forms by what they sell: *more* of the same (quantity), *better* version (quality), or *complementary / different* (new). And four forms by *how* they're presented — the four mechanisms below. Upsells fail when timed wrong (before the customer has felt the problem), targeted wrong (solves a problem they don't have), or framed wrong (don't believe you).

## Framework / Structure

### 1. The Classic Upsell — "You can't have X without Y"
Solve the next problem the moment the customer becomes aware of it. The previous offer manufactures awareness; the upsell answers it.

- Examples are mechanical: car wash → sealant; bicycle → helmet, lights, puncture-resistant tires; exercise course → nutrition course; rental car → insurance, late return, prepaid gas.
- Sequencing rule: offer **higher-margin upsells first**. If you can only get one yes, take the bigger one.
- "Say no to say yes" mechanic: ask "you don't want anything else, do you?" People are conditioned to answer "no" to that — which actually means "no, I don't want *more*; what you offered is fine." Converts trained-no into structural-yes.
- Hyper-buying-cycle rule: customers in life transitions (weddings, new hobbies, babies, relocations, new businesses) buy in concentrated bursts. If you serve those problems, run the upsell stack aggressively during the window.
- Bonuses-as-problem-creators: free bonuses introduce new objects that need protection / storage / accessories — which become the next upsell. The fur coat earmuff example: free earmuffs → $30/year storage for them.
- **BAMFAM**: Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Never end an interaction without scheduling the next. Each meeting is an upsell window; missing one is missing a sale.
- Upsell guarantees, warranties, insurance: instead of giving them away, charge 5–50% extra. Many customers prefer paying for protection over getting it free.

### 2. Menu Upsell — "You don't need that, you need this"
Combines up to four micro-tactics, in order: Unsell → Prescribe → A/B → Card On File. Used when you have multiple offers and want to avoid yes/no questions entirely.

- **Unsell.** Tell them what they *don't* need ("you can skip this, you're not pregnant," "you don't need this size, you've got a big dog"). Builds trust by sacrificing a sale. Employees especially love unselling — let them; it converts.
- **Prescribe.** Tell them what they *do* need, with detailed personalized instructions, integrated into how they'll use the things they already bought. No "do you want…?" — only "here's how you'll use it." Removes the not-buy option from the conversation.
- **A/B Upsell.** When multiple options solve the same problem, ask preference between two — never yes/no. "Chocolate or vanilla?" "Morning or afternoon?" "One bottle or two?" Anything can be A/B-ified: quantity, start date, payment, flavor, time slot, format, size, color, material, personnel, channel.
- **Add a nudge** to A/B for inexperienced customers: "this is my favorite," "X is usually a safe bet," "Tuesday's sessions are smaller."
- **Card On File.** Final move — "want to use the card on file?" Removes the buying-friction reflex (taking out the card, choosing which one, recalling past regret).
- Run-out hack: if sold out, take payment and delay delivery. Reframe as "we'll order it for you" — works far better than declining the upsell.

### 3. Anchor Upsell — premium first, then the real offer
Present a premium offer at 5–10× the main offer's price. Get "the gasp." Come to the rescue with the main offer. Anchored buyers spend more than they planned, and a meaningful tail still buys the premium.

- Five steps: present anchor → gasp → ask "do you care about [premium feature]?" → present main offer → ask which card.
- Required: the premium offer must be *real*. If you treat it as a fake, the customer will too. Hormozi's failure-case example: a friend kept losing the play until he reworked the premium into something he genuinely wanted to deliver. Then he tripled profit.
- Same primary features in both tiers, different secondary features. The anchor's secondary features carry the price premium (designer, exclusivity, response time, white-glove). Core function is identical.
- "The Gasp" is the signal the anchor worked. No gasp = anchor priced too low or feels reasonable. Bigger gasps correlate with bigger main-offer take rates.
- This is the same psychological mechanism as price anchoring in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]] — but operationalized at the upsell slot with a sales script and a sequencing rule.

### 4. Rollover Upsell — credit prior purchases toward the next offer
Apply some or all of what the customer (or a competitor's customer) paid before as credit toward your next, more-expensive offer. Most flexible upsell; works on four cohorts.

- **Who** to roll over to:
  1. Old customers (6+ months since last purchase) — winback campaign.
  2. Your own upset customers — alternative to refund; "do-over" credit.
  3. Competitors' upset customers — find via negative reviews, roll over the cost of breaking their old agreement.
  4. Current customers — apply first purchase price as discount over a longer-term agreement.
- **What** to roll over to: more, better, or new — but *something with higher margin than the credit eats*.
- **How** to roll the credit:
  - Up front (entire credit applied immediately) — best for winbacks where the goal is reactivation.
  - At the end (credit pushes the term out) — preserves cash flow.
  - Spread over time ($600 credit → $50/mo discount over 12 months) — the most cash-positive variant; keeps customer paying continuously.
- Pricing rule: next offer must be **at least 4× the credit applied**. So even a full rollover discounts only 25%.
- Add urgency: rollover offer is one-time-only at the moment it's presented. Surprise + non-repeatable raises take rate.
- Personalized winback example: Hormozi made 200 personalized videos offering $4,000 credit each — 20% conversion → ~$1.9M annual recurring revenue from one day of recording.

## Strategic Implications

- **The thing you sell most is rarely the thing you make most on.** The Attraction Offer's job is to manufacture buying state; the upsells monetize it. Businesses that don't grasp this build front-end traffic and starve.
- **Upsells timed to the problem outperform upsells timed to a calendar.** Hormozi's failure modes (wrong time, wrong fit, wrong frame) all reduce to one cause: ignoring the problem-revealing moment. Calendar-based "30-day upgrade emails" miss this; in-meeting / in-checkout upsells hit it.
- **Removing the not-buy option is the meta-pattern across all four mechanisms.** Classic uses "say no to say yes." Menu uses prescribe + A/B. Anchor uses comparative framing. Rollover uses one-time-only urgency. None of them ask "do you want this?" — every one of them changes the question.
- **Anchor and Rollover are the two highest-leverage upsells when correctly built.** Anchor lifts AOV by re-framing perception of the main offer; Rollover converts dead customers, complaint-lost customers, and *competitors'* customers into cash. The latter is rare to find documented at this granularity.
- **BAMFAM is a structural rule, not a sales tactic.** Without a scheduled next interaction, the upsell window closes. Operationally, this means every customer-facing process needs a built-in next-meeting trigger.

## Practical Use

Choosing a mechanism by situation:

| Situation | Default mechanism |
|---|---|
| Customer just bought; immediate adjacent need exists | Classic Upsell |
| Multiple variants/options/SKUs in the catalog | Menu Upsell |
| Single product line; customer price-anchored too low | Anchor Upsell |
| Returning customer / lost customer / complaint customer | Rollover Upsell |
| Want to lift commitment length on existing customer | Rollover Upsell (spread variant) |
| Want to convert a competitor's customer | Rollover Upsell (cost-of-breaking-contract credit) |

For new businesses: start with the Classic Upsell. It is the lowest-skill, highest-impact mechanic. Ship it first. Add Menu when you have more than one SKU. Add Anchor when you have a credible premium tier. Add Rollover when you have a customer base big enough to mine.

Combine freely with downsells — a no to an upsell triggers a downsell, and the conversation continues. See [[Hormozi-Downsell-Offers|Downsell Offers]].

## Notes / Limits

- Hormozi flags the Upsell section as "brutally effective" with an explicit ethics warning: these mechanics get yeses from people who would have said no, which means the underlying product has to be worth the price. If it isn't, upsells convert bad businesses into worse ones faster.
- The "say no to say yes" mechanic depends on a casual, almost bored disposition. Delivered with hype it reads as manipulation and breaks trust.
- A/B Upsell breaks down for high-stakes purchases where the customer wants the option to walk away to think about it. In those contexts, Anchor + Classic perform better.
- Rollover Upsell on competitors' customers should be checked against industry-specific contract law and false-claim regulations.
