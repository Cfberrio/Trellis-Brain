# Hormozi — Money Model Assembly

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Combine the four offer types into a working Money Model. Defines the build order, the staging discipline, the pricing-evolution rule, and the operational shape of a complete sequence. Where the four mechanism notes describe *parts*, this note describes the *machine*.

## Core Idea
A Money Model develops in lockstep with the business — never installed all at once. Hormozi's evolution: get customers reliably → make them pay for themselves reliably → make them pay for *other* customers reliably → maximize each customer's long-term value → pour cash into ads. Trying to skip stages collapses the business; "when your Money Model starts working, your business starts breaking" because each stage stresses different operational systems.

The build is **one offer at a time, each made reliable before the next is added**. Patience measured in quarters, not weeks. "You either build it right or you build it again. And again. And again."

## Framework / Structure

### Build order (four steps)
1. **Step 1 — Attraction Offer (Stage I).** Goal: turn strangers into customers and cover acquisition + delivery cost. Pick from the five mechanisms in [[Hormozi-Attraction-Offers|Attraction Offers]]. Advertise. Tune until lead → customer conversion is reliable. May take up to a year to find what works in your market.
2. **Step 2 — Upsell Offer (Stage II).** Goal: lift 30-day profit well above acquisition + delivery. Pick from the four mechanisms in [[Hormozi-Upsell-Offers|Upsell Offers]]. Trigger at the moment of greatest customer need (the problem the Attraction Offer revealed).
3. **Step 3 — Downsell Offer (Stage II).** Goal: convert nos into yeses. Pick from the three mechanisms in [[Hormozi-Downsell-Offers|Downsell Offers]]. Alternate Payment Plan and Feature Downsells in the same conversation.
4. **Step 4 — Continuity Offer (Stage III).** Goal: stack recurring revenue + extract one final 30-day cash bump (bulk prepay on continuity sign-up). Pick from the three mechanisms in [[Hormozi-Continuity-Offers|Continuity Offers]]. May land outside the 30-day window — that's acceptable when timing requires it.

### The evolution rule
Each stage must pay for the next stage's installation. In Hormozi's words:
- First, get customers reliably.
- Then make sure they pay for themselves reliably.
- Then make sure they pay for *other customers* reliably.
- Then start maximizing each customer's long-term value.
- Then spend as many advertising dollars as possible.

Operational implication: the business gets harder before it gets bigger. Plan for breakage at each transition (delivery scale, sales process, support load, retention systems).

### Reference Money Models from the book

#### Gym Launch (Service / B2B licensing)
- **Stage I — Attraction:** Decoy Offer. Free DIY content vs. $16,000 done-with-you licensing.
- **Stage II — Upsell:** Classic Upsell. $42,000/yr "Gym Lords" advanced playbooks + community ($36,000 prepaid).
- **Stage II — Downsell:** Payment Plan Downsell (seesaw). $10,000 down + 52 weekly → $800/wk for 52 weeks → free start with continuity-discount frontload.
- **Stage III — Continuity:** Menu Close + Feature Downsell. Full package $800/wk through to a $100/wk Minimum Package (original licensing materials + tech support).
- Result: $476K/mo at 3 months → $1.5M/mo with Stage II → $2.3M/mo at 14 months → $4.4M/mo at 20 months (with Prestige Labs added).

#### Micro Gyms (local service)
- Attraction: Win Your Money Back (pay-to-enter fitness challenge with refund-on-results).
- Stage I Downsell: Payment Plan (split → three-pay → Trial With Penalty).
- Stage II Upsell: Menu Upsell on supplement bundles.
- Stage II Downsell: Feature Downsell (Big Bundle → Small Bundle → monthly subscription).
- Stage III Continuity: Rollover Upsell + Lifetime Discount ($50/mo off for life with 12-month commitment).

#### Newsletter (digital product)
- Attraction: Free Trial ($0 then $399/mo after 30 days).
- Stage II + III combined: Pay Less Now or Pay More Later + Lifetime Discount ($297 now, lock that rate forever).
- Hormozi calls this "a six-headed money-making monster" — single offer plays Attraction, Upsell, and Continuity slots simultaneously.

#### Dog Food (physical product)
- Attraction: Buy X Get Y Free (Buy 4 months, get 2 free).
- Stage II Upsell: Classic Upsell (toys → vitamins).
- Stage II Downsell: Feature Downsell ("just the premium food then?").
- Stage III Continuity: Automatic renewal after the 6-month bulk runs out.

### Cross-stage operating rules
Drawn from the book's "Important Notes" in Section VI plus Section IV's Rules of Downselling:

- **Perfect one offer at a time.** Don't ship a full Money Model at once.
- **Raise price in stages.** New offers cheap at first to generate volume + feedback. Once reliable, raise price until you stop making more money — which is the only test that matters.
- **Simple scales. Fancy fails.** "Fewer products, more offers" — design 100 ways to sell one product before designing 100 products.
- **Affiliate offers fill Money Model gaps.** Sell other people's stuff for commissions to plug missing slots without building the operations. Works at every scale.
- **Attraction → Continuity convertible.** "Buy 6 months get 6 months free" can auto-roll into month-to-month subscription at the end. Same offer plays Attraction and Continuity slots; small tweak, big implication.
- **Mix and match freely.** The four-type taxonomy is for thinking, not for fences. Upsell tactics inside an Attraction Offer; Continuity Offer used to attract; etc. Hormozi's instruction: start with the canonical placement, then experiment as your reps build.

## Strategic Implications

- **The build sequence is the differentiator.** Most businesses with money problems have a working Attraction Offer and nothing else. The book is largely a build-order argument: Stage I → II → III, never parallel, each gated on reliability. Skipping stages is the most common failure mode.
- **A Money Model is one thing, sold many ways.** This is the book's deepest claim. The catalog of features (response time, service ratio, payment cadence, term length, guarantees, etc.) becomes the inventory used by both upsells (added) and downsells (removed). One product, many offers; one cohort, many trades.
- **Reliability is the gate, not profitability.** A profitable Stage I that breaks under volume cannot fund Stage II. Operations come before model expansion. The book's repeated framing: "your Money Model evolves alongside your business" — meaning the business has to keep up with what the model demands.
- **The 30-day window is enforced by ad billing, not customer behavior.** Cash leaves on monthly cycles; cash should arrive on the same cycle. This is why Stage III continuity, while important, comes last — it stacks recurring on top of a system that is already cash-positive in 30 days, rather than betting on multi-month payback.
- **Affiliates are the cheap way to plug missing slots.** Especially for early-stage businesses with one offer: an affiliate Upsell or affiliate Continuity can fill the model long before product is built. Same logic applies in reverse at scale (affiliate revenue with no operational cost).

## Practical Use

For an existing business:
1. **Audit which slots are filled.** Map current revenue to Attraction / Upsell / Downsell / Continuity. Most businesses fill only 1–2 slots.
2. **Pick the next empty slot.** Don't optimize a slot that's reliable; build the missing one. Order: Attraction → Upsell → Downsell → Continuity.
3. **Pick a mechanism within that slot.** Use the decision matrices in the four mechanism notes.
4. **Ship a low-priced version first.** Generate yeses, collect feedback, raise price.
5. **Make it reliable before adding the next slot.** Reliability = predictable conversion + delivery without breaking other operations.
6. **Plug remaining gaps with affiliate offers** if building the next slot is operationally infeasible right now.

For a new business: Stage I only. Don't think about the rest until customers and cash are flowing reliably.

## Notes / Limits

- The build-order discipline is a constraint, not a guarantee. A bad product still loses money no matter how perfect the Money Model is. The book assumes the offer-construction work from `$100M Offers` and the lead-generation work from `$100M Leads` are already in place; this book engineers what happens between them.
- The 30-day window and the "pay for itself + pay for one more" threshold are operational targets, not universal laws. Some industries (long enterprise sales cycles, regulated finance) cannot literally hit them; the *direction* of the principle (recover cash faster than you spend it) translates.
- Mixing offer types creatively is encouraged late, not early. Hormozi explicitly says: "start with the way I suggest it. Then, as you get better, experiment." The mechanism notes assume canonical placement; the assembly note opens the door to recombination once each part is reliable.
- The example Money Models (Gym Launch, Micro Gyms, Newsletter, Dog Food) are illustrative compositions, not templates to copy. The mechanism choices reflect industry shape; the build-order discipline is what generalizes.
