# Hormozi — Attraction Offers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Stage I of a Money Model. Attraction Offers turn strangers into customers and — when constructed properly — pay back acquisition + delivery cost on the first transaction. They are the engine of cash recovery; nothing downstream matters if Stage I leaks.

## Core Idea
Strangers cannot judge your value, but they can judge price. A discount or a free component is the only universal language for "this is a great deal" before trust exists. So every Attraction Offer leans on free or discounted framing — but it does so in ways that *still produce cash*. The trick is: free and discount are not opposites of profit; they are levers that can be combined with anchors, contingencies, prepayments, and bundles such that the buyer perceives "free" while the business collects more than it spent. Hormozi documents five mechanisms, each engineering "free" differently.

## Framework / Structure

The five mechanisms below all generate leads using free/discount framing, but each captures cash through a different structural device.

### 1. Win Your Money Back
Customer pays up front. They get the money back (cash or store credit) only if they hit defined results, perform defined actions, or both.

- Three variants: results-only (bet on themselves), actions-only (bet on following directions), or both (bet on the system).
- Cash dynamics: ~10% of customers actually qualify and request the refund (Hormozi's data across thousands of gyms). The math closes because (a) most don't qualify and (b) those who qualify are perfect upsell targets — they followed the program and got results.
- Refund criteria must be (a) easy to track, (b) genuinely conducive to results, (c) doubling as advertising for the business (posts, testimonials, referrals, reviews).
- Critical move: *make everyone a winner privately* mid-program. Tell each customer their next offer applies their winnings as credit, regardless of whether they hit the goal. Removes anxiety, increases retention, opens upsell window.
- Apply credit *over time* on the next offer, not up front. A $600 win becomes $50/mo off a 12-month $200/mo product, not three months free. Keeps skin in the game.
- Required guardrail: only run this if existing refund rate is below 5%. Otherwise the product is the problem.

### 2. Giveaways
Advertise a chance to win a Grand Prize in exchange for contact info + qualifying actions. After picking a winner, offer everyone else a discounted version of the same prize (a "partial scholarship" or equivalent).

- Six steps: pick Grand Prize → pick promotional offer → ask for contact info + eligibility → pick qualifying actions → set deadline → announce winner + contact everyone else.
- Grand Prize must equal the thing you actually want everyone to buy, with a credible monetary anchor. If the giveaway flops, the prize wasn't grand enough.
- Promotional offer (the "everyone else" offer) is enhanced with a 10–30%-of-gross-margin discount. A 10% retail discount becomes a *64% cost-to-value* shift when expressed against the Grand Prize value anchor.
- Two prizes for twice the leads: anyone whose referral wins also wins. Creates referral loop with quality control (referrers protect their own win).
- Three layers of urgency: time to enter (3–7 days), time to claim (7 days), time to use (hours to a few days).
- Always have a downsell ready — present same-percent discount on a different product if the Grand Prize doesn't fit.

### 3. Decoy Offer
Advertise a free or low-cost (decoy) version. When leads engage, present a much more valuable premium offer side-by-side. Most pick premium; the rest still become decoy customers worth upselling later.

- Steps: advertise a smaller / older / less-personalized version → on engagement, show both side by side, emphasize premium → contrast benefits.
- Decoy is intentionally stripped (no guarantees, fewer features, less personalization). Make the contrast huge.
- Permission script: "Are you here for free stuff or lasting results?" → if results, skip to premium. If free stuff, present decoy then immediately contrast: "Which do you think will get you to your goal faster?"
- Discount Offers (vs. Free Offers) attract fewer leads but higher show-up rates — important when a no-show has high cost (medical, legal, etc.).
- Optional: surprise decoy buyers with one or two zero-cost premium features. Builds goodwill, raises later-upsell take rate.

### 4. Buy X Get Y Free
Customer buys one thing; gets other things free. The free items can match or differ from the paid item.

- Reframes the same total price as a "free" offer instead of a discount. "Buy 1 pair of boots for $600, get 2 pairs free" sells more than "3 pairs for $200 each" at the same total revenue.
- Two design rules: (a) more free than paid (Buy 1 Get 2 outperforms Buy 2 Get 1); (b) raise base prices *first* to absorb the giveaway — actually raise them, don't fake it.
- Free items can be different (cheaper) products of equivalent perceived value (e.g., 1 shirt + 3 pairs of socks beats 1 shirt + 1 shirt for the same cost).
- Free framing beats equivalent-percentage discount framing in most markets — test both.
- For services, this becomes time: "Buy 6 months get 12 months free" gets one prepay covering 18 months of attention. Prepay customers are the highest-spending customers; keep selling them.
- Cash discipline: budget delivery for the full term you sold. Spending prepaid cash on overhead breaks the model.
- Use on existing recurring customers (cap at ~10%) for emergency cash without killing baseline recurring revenue.

### 5. Pay Less Now or Pay More Later
Customer chooses: pay $0 now and get billed full price later (with conditional satisfaction guarantee), or pay a 20–50% discounted price now plus bonuses.

- Pay Later combines delayed payment + conditional guarantee. They can cancel before the charge if the result wasn't delivered — but only if they met the qualifying conditions (attendance, completion, etc.). Conditions must align with what produces value.
- Pay Now offers immediate discount + bonuses + immediate delivery. Pay Later gets the card on file regardless. Then pitch Pay Now after they choose Pay Later.
- The promised result must be a clear yes/no, measurable, deliverable inside the timeframe. Vague promises generate cancellations.
- Tuning: too many Pay Later → make Pay Now sweeter. Too many Pay Now → reverse. >10% Pay Later cancellation = overpromising or underdelivering.
- Recurring-revenue variant: pay a higher ongoing rate later, or a lower rate locked for life if they pay now.
- See [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Guarantees|Guarantees]] for the underlying guarantee mechanics — this offer is a conditional satisfaction guarantee applied at the Attraction slot.

## Strategic Implications

- **Free and discount are continuum, not categories.** Hormozi treats "free," "$1," and "discount" as interchangeable framings — the right choice depends on lead-cost economics and show-up rates, not on which "feels" right. Run both as A/B in any new market.
- **The Attraction Offer must be designed to fund downstream offers, not just to acquire.** Win Your Money Back and Buy X Get Y Free both bake in reasons to make a *next* offer (apply credit, deliver bonuses, sell complements). If your Attraction Offer doesn't create an upsell handoff, you've designed a one-shot front end.
- **Prepay is the highest-leverage cash mechanism in the book.** Buy X Get Y Free + Pay Less Now both pull months of revenue forward. With operational discipline, that cash funds the next wave of acquisition before the current cohort ages out — the engine of CFA.
- **Free leads ≠ profitable leads.** Discount offers attract fewer but better-qualified leads. In high no-show-cost businesses, default to a small price tag, not free.
- **Win Your Money Back is the highest-margin Attraction Offer in the book** because most "winners" don't claim, and those who do are the cleanest upsell pool. Hormozi credits it with $1B+ industry-wide and uses it as the base for the Trial With Penalty downsell logic too.

## Practical Use

Choosing a mechanism by business type:

| Business shape | Default mechanism | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Outcome-driven (fitness, coaching, B2B results, weight loss) | Win Your Money Back | Customer effort produces the result; refund condition aligns incentives |
| High-ticket / consideration-heavy | Giveaways | Generates engaged leads who already raised their hand for the premium item |
| Already running ads, lead cost climbing | Decoy Offer | Premium-vs-decoy contrast lifts AOV without raising lead price |
| Consumable / repeatable / time-based service | Buy X Get Y Free | Reframes price as free, pulls prepay forward |
| Service with measurable yes/no result | Pay Less Now or Pay More Later | Card on file regardless of choice, cancellations gated by completion criteria |

Choosing by cash priority:
- Need cash *now*: Buy X Get Y Free, Pay Now option of Pay Less Now or Pay More Later.
- Need *leads* now: Win Your Money Back, Giveaways, Decoy.
- Need card on file regardless: Pay Less Now or Pay More Later.

Recurring-revenue note: every mechanism in this section can be adapted to flow customers into a continuity offer — the conversion happens at the *next* slot, not here. See [[Hormozi-Continuity-Offers|Continuity Offers]] for how Attraction Offers feed continuity, and [[Hormozi-Money-Model-Assembly|Money Model Assembly]] for how the slots compose.

## Notes / Limits

- Advertising law around "free" is jurisdictional and tightening. Specifically: store-credit-as-refund framing, conditional guarantees, "free trial with penalty," giveaway eligibility rules. Hormozi explicitly defers to legal counsel; the playbook is inspiration, not compliance.
- Hard selling breaks Attraction Offers because the offer's premise is that demand exists and the offer reveals it. Pushing converts current customers but poisons referrals. The book repeatedly recommends *making the offer available* and walking away if they decline.
- Free Goodwill Offer (the chapter on p. 62) is a review request, not an Attraction mechanism — same slot Hormozi uses in `$100M Offers`. Documented as source-only in the Source Map.
