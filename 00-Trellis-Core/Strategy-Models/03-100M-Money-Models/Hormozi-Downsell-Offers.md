# Hormozi — Downsell Offers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Stage II of a Money Model. What you offer after a no. Downsells convert the lost half of every sales conversation into additional cash without dropping price as a freebie. They are the difference between "X% close rate" and "Y% close rate where Y is much larger."

## Core Idea
A no is a no to *this offer*, not to *all offers*. The standard reaction — drop the price to make the sale — is not downselling, it's discounting, and it costs you customers who would have paid full price. Real downselling changes one of two things: **how they pay** (payment plan, trial) or **what they get** (fewer features, lower quantity, lesser quality). Price stays defended; the trade changes.

Hormozi's frame: think of downselling as having "100 ways to offer the same product," not "100 products." Same inventory; many trades.

## Framework / Structure

### The Rules of Downselling (apply across all three mechanisms)
1. **No means no for this thing, not for all things.** Make another offer.
2. **Downsells are trades.** If you give something, get something. Never one-sided concession.
3. **Personalize, don't pressure.** Find what they like, offer more of that, less of what they don't, with price to match.
4. **Same product, new framings.** Don't invent new SKUs to downsell — recombine what exists.
5. **Don't drop your price just to get them to buy.** That's discounting and breaks ethics + economics.
6. **Customers talk about price.** Ad-hoc discounts leak; word gets around. Plan price tests; don't improvise.

### 1. Payment Plan Downsell — change *how* they pay
Same product, same total price, spread payment across time. Up to seven graduated steps, presented in this order. Stop when they buy.

1. **Reward paying in full** (don't punish paying over time). Frame: "It's $15, but $10 if you prepay — most people prepay." Same math; better feel; built-in price anchor.
2. **3rd-party financing → credit card → layaway.** Push the payment-plan risk onto another party first. Layaway = paid in installments before delivery; lowest risk to you.
3. **Half now, half later** — schedule the second payment to a paycheck date. Biweekly billing aligned with paychecks beats monthly for 30-day cash and reduces declines.
4. **Temperature check.** "On a 1–10, how badly do you want this?" 8+ → keep going. 7 or below → switch to Feature Downsell.
5. **Split into three.** ⅓ now, ⅓ next two paychecks (or months).
6. **Even spread across the term.** 16-week program → 16 weekly payments.
7. **Free Trial.** Last resort — and not a regular free trial; see Trial With Penalty below.

Built-in upsell on payment plans: at any point during the plan, offer the original prepay-discount price for paying off the balance. Some customers take it.

Decline-rate hack: align charge dates to paycheck dates, run failed cards multiple times that day. Recovers ~⅓ of declines.

Critical guardrail: payment plans only grow the business if **the percentage of paid-in-full sales stays the same** while overall close rate rises. If paid-in-fulls drop because PIF buyers chose the plan, you've cannibalized your own cash. Watch this metric.

Cross-data: ProfitWell churn data Hormozi cites — monthly billing = 10.7% monthly cancellation; quarterly = 5%; annual = 2%. Larger-payment cadences reduce churn, not just boost upfront cash.

### 2. Trial With Penalty — free trial with teeth
Customer gets the product or service free *if* they meet the terms. They only pay if they don't. The terms mirror Win Your Money Back criteria — actions and results that make great long-term customers.

- Five-step downsell flow: agree they'll stay if it works → get the credit card → present trial → explain fees *after* card → require check-in meetings (which double as upsell windows).
- Card is non-negotiable: "What card do you want to use? It's just how we've always done it." If they refuse, end the conversation politely.
- "Sell staying and paying" before billing starts: confirm "if this gets you the result, will you stay long-term?" If no, no point starting the trial.
- Fees can be one lump (one mess-up = full charge) or per-criterion (small charge per missed item). Per-criterion preserves goodwill; lump enforces compliance hard.
- Mid-trial check-ins are the upsell windows. Three scenarios:
  - Loved it → upsell longer term or premium tier.
  - Hated it → take the blame, ask what would have been better, offer a higher-tier alternative ("based on what you needed, this fits better"). ~50% conversion.
  - Didn't use it → reach out repeatedly; offer fee waiver in exchange for a meeting. Don't bill non-starters — small fee not worth a 1-star review.
- **When to use Trial With Penalty vs. Pay Less Now or Pay More Later:** Pay Less Now/More Later is for **physical products and one-time services**; Trial With Penalty is for **recurring products and services where the customer must do work to get results**.
- Discount-instead-of-free trick: $1 first month instead of free month. Same effect, justifies card requirement, validates payment method ahead of recurring billing.

### 3. Feature Downsell — change *what* they get
Lower price by removing or downgrading features. Removing things they value little while lowering price meaningfully gets them to take the downsell; removing things they value highly while lowering price slightly gets them to take the original.

- Levers (most-to-least common):
  - **Quantity** — fewer sessions, fewer units, shorter duration.
  - **Quality** — older versions, less personalized, lower-grade materials.
  - **Service quality features** — time availability, location availability, response speed, delivery speed, service ratio (1:1 → 1:many), communication channel, provider qualifications, live vs. recorded, in-person vs. remote, DIY vs. DWY vs. DFY, expirations, personalization, insurance/guarantee depth.
  - **Entire feature removal** — drop priority chat, drop calls, drop guarantee.
  - **Done-For-You → Do-It-Yourself product downsell** — chiropractor → at-home tools; painter → paint + lease the spray gun; consultant → workshop.
- Standardized process: cut something high-value first and lower price slightly → if reject, keep removing and dropping price until they buy. The first cut is for re-upselling them on the original; the rest is to find the best fit.
- **Name the combinations.** "Whale Package," "Total Transformation," "Minimum Package." Premium names trigger aspiration; "Minimum" triggers floor-effect ("I should at least get the minimum").
- After every two downsells, temperature check (1–10 scale). 8+ → switch to Payment Plan Downsell. 7 or below → recombine features.
- "Deal? Fair enough?" close after each downsell — small linguistic move; meaningful lift.
- Free orientation + DIY product: when all DFY downsells refused, offer a free orientation. Half show. Almost all who show buy a DIY product (e.g., supplements after a refused fitness offer).
- Feature-downsell *current customers* before they cancel — second-highest-value cohort, per Hormozi.
- **Guarantee removal** as a downsell lever: see [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Guarantees|Guarantees]] for the underlying mechanic. Removing the guarantee makes its value visible and re-upsells the original price.
- Bartering trick: discount in exchange for review + testimonial + social posts + 2 referrals. "$100 off if you do these four things." Advertising is worth more than the discount.

## Strategic Implications

- **Downsells are the single biggest swing factor in 30-day profit at fixed lead volume.** Stage I gets the lead in the door. Stage II Upsells extract from yeses. Downsells extract from nos — and nos outnumber yeses in nearly every business. Skipping them means leaving the largest cohort uncaptured.
- **Don't cut price; cut payment shape or feature shape.** This is the central discipline. A business that only knows how to discount has one downsell move; this section gives 11 (7 payment-plan steps + 1 trial + 3 feature levers).
- **Trial With Penalty is the inverse of Win Your Money Back.** Same criteria; opposite incentive. WYMB rewards adherence with refund eligibility; TWP penalizes non-adherence with a fee. Same mechanism, deployed at opposite ends of the buying conversation.
- **Pricing/feature symmetry across stages:** the same Feature Downsell levers (response time, service ratio, etc.) can be used in reverse to construct Anchor Upsells (Stage II). The catalog of features-and-prices is one inventory used in both directions.
- **Active retention via downsell.** The "downsell current customers before cancellation" mechanic moves downsell out of new-sales into retention. It is operationally cheaper to feature-downsell a churning customer than to acquire a new one — and Hormozi reports the cohort lands with second-highest LTV.

## Practical Use

Decision tree on receiving a no to the original offer:
1. **Do they want it at all?** Temperature check. <8 → Feature Downsell to find a different fit. ≥8 → Payment Plan Downsell.
2. **Recurring or one-time?** Recurring + work-based → Trial With Penalty as floor. One-time / physical → Pay Less Now or Pay More Later (which lives in [[Hormozi-Attraction-Offers|Attraction Offers]] but pulls double duty here).
3. **Has the customer already bought once and is canceling?** Feature Downsell into the lower package they actually use. Don't refund; reshape.

Combine Payment Plan and Feature Downsells in the same conversation. They alternate naturally — drop a feature, change the payment, drop another, etc. — and the buyer feels collaborated-with instead of pressured.

## Notes / Limits

- Hormozi explicitly bans negotiating price. "Customers who demand to pay less for the same thing are business terrorists. I don't negotiate with terrorists." The whole architecture rests on holding price firm and changing payment or features instead.
- Trial With Penalty has only been validated in businesses where the customer does work to get the result (fitness, B2B, software with onboarding). Adapt before applying to passive consumption (entertainment, content) — the no-show penalty has unclear legitimacy when adherence isn't required.
- The "cancellation fee" + "exit interview" pairings border on retention-coercion in unfavorable framings. The book consistently says: if someone wants out, let them out. Don't use fees to trap unhappy customers; use them to surface fixable complaints.
- Free orientations as DIY-downsell catchers depend on having a credible self-service product — otherwise you're delivering free education to leads who never buy.
