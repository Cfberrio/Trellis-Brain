# Hormozi — Continuity Offers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Money Models — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Stage III of a Money Model. Continuity Offers provide ongoing value for ongoing payments — recurring revenue. The book's contrarian point: *do not use continuity as your Attraction Offer*. Use it as the last offer in the sequence, after Attraction + Upsell + Downsell have already extracted up-front cash. Done last, continuity stacks recurring revenue on top of a model that's already cash-positive on day 30.

## Core Idea
Continuity reverses the trade-off: more buyers at the cost of less cash-now. A $1,000 thing might convert 10 of 100 leads ($10,000 today, $0 tomorrow). The same thing at $50/mo might convert 40 of 100, kept 20 months ($2,000 today, $40,000 over time, **and** 4× the upsell-able customer base). As an Attraction Offer it starves; as a Stage-III lock-in it compounds.

The mechanism rule: get them to start, then get them to stick. Hormozi's three offers attack each side — Bonuses get them to start; Discounts and Waived Fees get them to stick. A useful rough heuristic: rewards (bonuses, discounts) outperform punishment (fees), but for long-commitment B2B contexts a Waived Fee mechanism keeps people committed when emotion would push them out.

## Framework / Structure

### 1. Continuity Bonus Offer — get them to start
Give an awesome thing at signup. The bonus's value typically exceeds the first month's payment — meaning the first payment effectively buys the bonus, with the membership as "free."

- Anatomy: bonus (added value) + discount (lowered cost) + urgency (today only). All three together; not one or two.
- Bonus types:
  - **More of the same** (years of past newsletters, archive access).
  - **Complementary** (free nutrition with fitness membership).
  - **Upgrade** (free gold tier when buying bronze, with limited availability).
- Pair with [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Bonuses|Bonuses]] — the underlying construction logic (problem-targeting, value anchoring, stacking) is canonical there. The continuity-specific moves are below.
- **Sell the bonus, then explain how to get it free.** Don't lead with "join the membership." Lead with the bonus, anchor its value, then: "Want to know how you can get this for free? Become a member today."
- Stack: after presenting one bonus, layer 2–3 more, each with its own dollar anchor.
- **Make bonuses things you already have and do.** Past content. Onboarding you'd do anyway. T-shirts on a digital product. Cost-marginal to you, high-value to them.
- **Make bonuses available only to members** — forces continuity if maximizing recurring is the goal. Or sell the bonus standalone for more upfront cash. Choice depends on which side of the model you need to feed.

#### Continuity-vs-Standalone pricing data
Hormozi's tested ratios (book p. 132) — pricing standalone offer relative to first month of continuity+bonus determines split:
- 1.33× standalone price → 50% choose continuity.
- 1.66× → 60% continuity.
- 2× → 70% continuity.
- 2.33× → 80% continuity.
- 2.66× → 90% continuity.

The principle generalizes even where the specific numbers don't: smaller standalone-vs-continuity gap → more standalone buyers (more upfront cash, less recurring). Larger gap → more continuity (less upfront, more recurring).

#### Bulk prepay continuity upsell
After someone agrees to continuity, immediately upsell bulk prepay at a discount. "Buy 5 months get 1 free." If even 1-in-8 of new continuity sign-ups takes the bulk upsell, 30-day profits jump ~50%.

### 2. Continuity Discount Offer — get them to start; some lock-in
Give free time (now, later, spread, or earned) in exchange for a longer commitment. Four variants:

- **Up front** — apply discount immediately, push the term out. Best in industries with strong contract enforcement (cell phones, storage, real estate, equipment). Bad fit for high-churn industries — you front-load the giveaway and they leave.
- **At the end** — discount applied at term-end as bonus time, *if* every payment was on time. They earn the free time. Lower-risk to you.
- **Spread over time** — $600 of free time over a 12-month commitment becomes $50/mo discount across the term. Most cash-positive variant: continuous payments with full discount value delivered.
- **After first 1–2 payments** — "first and last month," "last month up front," activation fee. Validates payment method before delivering discount.

#### Highest value-per-word in the book
Bill weekly, every 2 weeks, every 4 weeks — **not monthly**. 12 monthly cycles vs. 13 four-week cycles per year = 8.3% more annual revenue at identical effective price. On 20% margins, that lifts annual profit by ~41%. Pure language change; no product change.

#### Other small high-leverage moves
- Add a 3% processing fee. "It's $X plus a 3% processing fee." Almost no one objects. On a 10%-margin business, that's +30% profit.
- Get a second form of payment by offering to waive the processing fee in exchange. Cuts declined-payment loss.
- Prefer ACH over card where available — cheapest transaction type.
- **Lifetime discount earned at the average churn point.** If average customer leaves at month 4, advertise "lifetime discount unlocks at month 5." Designed to push past the churn cliff. The rice-company example: 5%-off subscription, 15%-off if they stay 5 months — locks lifetime rate just past the typical drop-off.
- **Gift cards** for unused continuity time — they can self-redeem later or gift to a friend. Some forget; you keep the full-price sign-up.
- Don't eat into the term with discounts — *extend* the term. "12 months + 3 free" stays 15 months total; not "9 months paid and 3 free totaling 12." Then Feature-Downsell to a shorter term if needed.

#### Cancellation policy
Hormozi's preferred policy: cancellation fee = the discount they took. Took $600 in discounts? Pay $600 to cancel. Self-explaining, fair, makes the math a wash.

- **Make cancellation easy and visible.** Hidden cancel paths convert customers into 1-star reviews. Visible cancel paths convert them into save opportunities.
- **Exit interview** in exchange for waiving cancellation fee. Saves ~⅓ of would-be-cancels. The interview either fixes the complaint, surfaces a Rollover Upsell into a higher tier, or generates feedback that improves the next sale.

### 3. Waived Fee Offer — get them to start *and* stick
"Pay a setup fee and go month-to-month, OR commit 12 months and I waive the fee. Cancel inside the term and you owe the waived fee."

- Setup fee typically 3–5× monthly rate.
- Mechanic: the fee is what gets them to start (waived = real value). The fee is also what gets them to stick (canceling early triggers it).
- Best fit: services with long time-to-result (SEO, weight loss, investing, accelerator-style B2B) where customers will get emotional mid-program and consider quitting.
- Smaller fee → more month-to-month sign-ups → more upfront cash. Larger fee → more commitments → more recurring. Tune by what the model needs.
- Drop the fee fully after the commitment period — fee shouldn't outlast the commitment.
- **>5% wanting to cancel early = product problem, not pricing problem.** Don't use the fee to handcuff a bad product; that converts angry customers into vocal angry customers.
- Optional intensifier: cancellation fee donated to a cause the customer hates. Two reasons to stay (avoid the fee + avoid the cause). Use cautiously; reputational risk.

## Strategic Implications

- **Continuity-as-Stage-III is the book's structural recommendation.** Most businesses install continuity as the front door (trial → subscription) and then can't afford to advertise. Hormozi's model puts it last so cash-now offers fund the acquisition cost; continuity then stacks on top.
- **Bonuses get them in; structure keeps them in.** Continuity Bonus Offers maximize sign-up rate; Continuity Discounts and Waived Fees maximize stickiness. Different jobs. Combine — Continuity Bonus on the front, Lifetime Discount earned at the churn cliff, Waived Fee for committed B2B clients.
- **Billing cadence is a profit lever, not an admin choice.** Weekly / 4-week billing produces materially more annual revenue than monthly billing at identical effective price. The 8.3% number multiplied by industry margin compounds into outsized profit lifts. Default to non-monthly cadence in any new continuity build.
- **Continuity standalone-vs-recurring pricing ratio is a tunable dial.** The 1.33×–2.66× table lets you target the cash-now / recurring mix explicitly. Cash-strapped phase → smaller ratio (more standalone takers). Stable phase → larger ratio (more recurring stack).
- **Cancellation is a sales conversation, not an admin transaction.** The exit-interview-saves-⅓ data point is the meta-rule. Make cancellation visible, take the conversation, run the rollover upsell or feature downsell against the actual complaint. Hidden cancels guarantee lost saves.

## Practical Use

Choosing among the three:

| Goal | Default mechanism |
|---|---|
| Maximize sign-up rate to continuity | Continuity Bonus Offer (bonus + discount + urgency) |
| Lock in long commitment with reward | Continuity Discount Offer (spread variant or earned-lifetime variant) |
| Lock in long commitment with structural friction | Waived Fee Offer |
| B2C consumer subscription | Continuity Bonus + Continuity Discount stacked |
| B2B service with multi-month delivery | Waived Fee Offer |
| Recovering cash on a continuity-only business | Bulk prepay upsell after continuity sign-up |

Always pair continuity with one mechanism from each upstream stage. Hormozi's repeat pattern: Attraction Offer (cash) → Upsell (more cash) → Downsell (capture nos) → Continuity Offer at first month-end with bulk prepay upsell on top.

## Notes / Limits

- Front-loaded discounts attract the most sign-ups but also produce the highest churn — use them only where contract enforcement is strong (telecom, storage). Otherwise, default to spread or earned-lifetime variants.
- The 8.3% billing-cadence trick assumes customers don't notice or care about the cadence reframe. Not a fraud, but transparency-wise should still be presented honestly ("billed every 4 weeks") rather than implied to be monthly.
- Waived Fee Offers should not be deployed against products with high early-churn rates — the fee then functions as a customer trap rather than a commitment device, and the >5% guardrail will signal the misuse.
- Continuity Discount Offer's "lifetime discount" framing must mean lifetime in practice. Promising lifetime then re-pricing later is the fastest way to torch trust.
