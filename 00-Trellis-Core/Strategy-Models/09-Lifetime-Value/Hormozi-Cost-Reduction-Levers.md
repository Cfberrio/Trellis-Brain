# Hormozi — Cost Reduction Levers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Lifetime Value — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Operational palette for **Lever 2 of the [[Hormozi-Crazy-Eight|Crazy Eight]]** — decrease cost-of-delivery for the same output, raising gross profit without touching price. Nine concrete tactics, mostly service-business-flavored but several apply to physical and digital products. This palette is not given anywhere else in the existing Hormozi corpus in the vault — Pricing-Power addresses price posture, CFA-Three-Levers addresses CAC math, but cost-of-delivery reduction is its own lever and lives here.

## Core Idea
Price has no ceiling; cost has a floor at zero. That asymmetry is structural. But the act of repeatedly hunting cost in the operating model surfaces design changes that compound — the business becomes more profitable *and* more scalable, because most cost-reduction moves are also leverage-creation moves (productizing, offshoring, ratio improvements, capping unbounded service).

The mental model is not "cut costs to survive." It is: **cost reduction is a delivery-design lever**. Each tactic is a re-architecture, not a budget cut.

## Framework / Structure

### The Nine Levers

| # | Lever | Mechanism | Typical Lift |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Improve employee:customer ratio | Same staff serves more customers per head | 50%+ cost cut on direct labor |
| 2 | Offshore talent | Same skill, lower cost-of-living geography | Up to 80% cost cut |
| 3 | Productize delivery | Sell more *similar* customers so templates+automation replace personalization | Step-change in margin and consistency |
| 4 | DFY → DWY | Shift "I do it for you" to "I help you do it" | Ratio improves dramatically; small price decrease tolerated |
| 5 | Cap usage | Bound unlimited service (revisions, calls); charge per unit beyond cap | Eliminates resource drain on heavy users |
| 6 | Lifetime → Annual access | Replace one-time-buys-forever with annual renewal | Caps support cost on stale customers |
| 7 | In-person → Remote | Drop travel + geographic talent constraints | Cuts travel cost both sides; widens hiring pool |
| 8 | Cut meeting time | Delete recurring meetings quarterly, halve survivor durations | Recovers team and customer time |
| 9 | Bulk + prepay | Lock 10–20% vendor discounts via prepay or bulk purchase | Risk-free 10–20% return on capital |

### Lever-by-lever detail

**1. Employee:customer ratio.** The default account-rep model puts one rep on five clients. Doubling the ratio to 1:10 cuts direct delivery cost in half. The ceiling is set by what the rep can sustain at the quality bar — not by what's "comfortable."

**2. Offshore talent.** Find the same skill set in lower-cost-of-living countries. Hormozi flags up to 80% labor-cost reduction as available *for free, to anyone*. The constraint is finding the talent, not paying for it.

**3. Productize via similar customers.** The cost-driver in custom service work is variance. Sell more customers with more similar problems, and the same templates, scripts, automations, and SOPs deliver to all of them. Result: faster delivery, more consistent outcomes, higher margin. This is also the prerequisite for moving up the leverage curve via SOPs and software.

**4. DFY → DWY.** Functionally a ratio improvement, but architecturally distinct enough to call out. "I'll do it for you" → "I'll help you do it." Customer ratio jumps; price drops slightly; margin jumps a lot. Works whenever the customer can absorb the doing if you provide the system.

**5. Cap usage.** Unlimited revisions, unlimited calls, unlimited support — endlessly drain delivery capacity for the customers least worth keeping. Cap the included quantity, charge per unit beyond. Worked example: video-editing agency caps revisions at N, charges for the N+1th. Restores per-customer profitability and self-selects out the unbounded customer.

**6. Lifetime → Annual.** Lifetime access on a one-time payment commits the business to indefinite support cost on a transaction whose revenue ended on day one. Annual renewal puts the support cost back in proportion to the revenue. Eliminates the long tail of dead-weight users.

**7. In-person → Remote.** Cuts travel cost both sides (your team, the customer). Also expands the talent pool — you're no longer hiring within commute distance. Double cost win: lower delivery cost *and* lower talent cost.

**8. Cut meeting time.** Hormozi names recurring internal/customer meetings as one of the largest invisible cost drivers. Quarterly ritual: **delete every recurring meeting; reinstate only the ones that proved necessary; on reinstatement, halve the duration**. Tightens cycles for team, customers, and operator simultaneously.

**9. Bulk and prepay.** With cash on hand, lock 10–20% discounts from vendors via prepayment or bulk purchase. Hormozi's framing: "if someone offered you a guaranteed 20% return on your money, you'd take it." Prepay discounts *are* that return, with the same risk profile as the underlying business. Same logic on bulk physical goods.

## Strategic Implications

- **Cost reduction is design work, not finance work.** Every tactic in the palette restructures *how the business delivers*, not what it pays. The CFO frame ("cut 5% across the board") doesn't surface any of these. The operator frame ("redesign delivery") surfaces all nine.
- **Ratio levers (1, 3, 4) compound.** Going from 1:5 to 1:10 (Lever 1) is a 2× ratio. Then productizing the customer base (Lever 3) lets the same rep handle 1:20. Then DFY→DWY (Lever 4) takes it to 1:40. The compounding moves a service business toward software-like margins without becoming software.
- **Cap + Annual + Lifetime→Annual together cure the "dead weight customer" problem.** The customer who pays once and consumes forever is a structural cost loss. These three levers, used together, return cost-to-revenue alignment.
- **Bulk/prepay is a balance-sheet lever disguised as an operational one.** It converts cash into margin at a guaranteed rate. Operators who treat cash as an emergency reserve miss this; the cash that earns 20% via prepay is doing more work than the cash sitting idle.
- **Cost levers feed price levers.** Lower cost expands the conversion-x-LTGP optimum that drives [[Hormozi-Price-Testing-Method|Price Testing]] — at a lower delivery cost, the price-conversion sweet spot shifts (more headroom to lower price *or* more profit at the same price).

## Practical Use

**Action step (Hormozi's own framing, p. 11):** *Pick the top two from this list you can implement in your business this quarter.* Don't try all nine; pick the two highest-impact, lowest-friction.

**Selection heuristic.**
- Service business with custom delivery → start with **3 (productize)** and **4 (DFY→DWY)**.
- Service business with churned customer book → start with **5 (cap)** and **6 (lifetime→annual)**.
- Anywhere with high travel cost → **7 (remote)**.
- Anywhere with packed calendars → **8 (cut meetings)** as a quarterly ritual.
- Strong cash position → **9 (bulk/prepay)** is free margin.
- High direct labor cost → **2 (offshore)** before **1 (ratio)**, because offshoring is cheaper per attempt than ratio expansion.

**Implementation cadence.** Pick two; set implementation dates; revisit the full list quarterly to pick the next two. Over a year, an operator can move through 6–8 of the nine, leaving the business architecturally different.
