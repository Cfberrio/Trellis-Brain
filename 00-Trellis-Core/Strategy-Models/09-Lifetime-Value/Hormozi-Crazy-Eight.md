# Hormozi — The Crazy Eight

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Lifetime Value — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Umbrella framework of the *Lifetime Value* playbook. Decomposes "make customers spend more" into eight discrete, mutually exclusive levers that any operator can run against any product or service. Replaces the broader two-axis frame ("raise AOV or raise frequency") with eight operational moves, each with its own action step. Designed as a fluency drill: once internalized, the operator can think through all eight on any business in seconds.

## Core Idea
> "He who can make his customer more valuable than his competition, wins."

Advertising is the machine; **LTV is the fuel**. Cost-per-attention only goes up over time — every auction-based platform inflates as it matures. The only durable moat is making each customer worth more to your business than to your competitors, so you can outbid them on every channel that auctions attention. CAC has a floor at $0; LTV has no ceiling. The Crazy Eight is the lever set for moving LTV.

The eight are organized along two axes:
- **Margin levers** — raise the spread on a given transaction (price, cost).
- **Volume / mix levers** — change how often, how much, how good, or how complementary the buying is (frequency, cross-sell, quantity up/down, quality up/down).

## Framework / Structure

### The Eight Levers

| # | Lever | Mechanism | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Raise Prices | Charge more for the same thing | Margin ↑ |
| 2 | Lower Costs | Deliver the same thing for less | Margin ↑ |
| 3 | Increase # of Purchases | Same customer buys more times over their life | Frequency ↑ |
| 4 | Cross-Sell | Customer buys a different complementary thing on top | Mix ↑ |
| 5 | Upsell Quantity | Same product, more of it, same transaction | AOV ↑ |
| 6 | Upsell Quality | Same product, premium tier | AOV ↑ |
| 7 | Downsell Quantity | Smaller version offered to non-buyers | Recovery |
| 8 | Downsell Quality | Lesser version offered to non-buyers | Recovery |

### Lever 1 — Raise Prices
You charge more for the same thing. Pricing affects gross profit more than any of the other seven. A 10%-margin business that raises price 20% (sales held flat) does not grow 20% — it triples. Operational test method is its own canonical note: see [[Hormozi-Price-Testing-Method|Hormozi — Price Testing Method]]. Strategic posture in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Hormozi — Pricing Power]].

### Lever 2 — Lower Costs
You decrease cost-of-delivery for the same output. Costs only go down to zero, but the discipline makes the business more profitable and more scalable. Hormozi gives a nine-tactic palette specific to service businesses (employee:client ratio, offshoring, productizing similar customers, DFY→DWY, capping usage, lifetime→annual access, in-person→remote, killing recurring meetings, prepay/bulk discounts). Full palette: [[Hormozi-Cost-Reduction-Levers|Hormozi — Cost Reduction Levers]].

### Lever 3 — Increase # of Purchases
Three (and only three) sub-mechanics:

1. **Add recurring.** Convert a one-time purchase into a weekly/monthly/quarterly/annual subscription or membership. Even at high churn, going from 1× to 3× purchases triples LTV. Canonical recurring offer architecture: [[../03-100M-Money-Models/Hormozi-Continuity-Offers|Hormozi — Continuity Offers]].
2. **Decrease churn.** Already-recurring revenue: get them to stay longer. Halving churn doubles LTV (`Price/Churn = lifetime revenue`). Hormozi punts the depth to the *Churn Checklist Playbook*; in this book it remains a pointer, not a framework.
3. **Follow-up / reactivation.** Run a **quarterly promotion** to former customers and engaged leads; provide value the rest of the time (give:ask ratio). Top-of-mind capture for buyers who weren't ready yet. The 90-day cadence is operationalized in [[../06-Fast-Cash/Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Cadence|Hormozi — Fast Cash Cadence]].

Worked examples per channel: lawn care upsells a maintenance membership; mug seller launches "mug of the month"; course creator adds accountability calls; meat membership adds text-controlled delivery to reduce churn; supplement brand runs a quarterly weight-loss challenge; course creator runs a quarterly promo cohort.

### Lever 4 — Cross-Sell
Sell a different but complementary product on top of the first. LTV math:
```
New LTV = Old LTV + (cross-sell conversion rate × cross-sell gross profit)
```
Example: $100 LTV + 20% × $100 cross-sell = $120 LTV. Examples: lawn care → snow-blowing; burger → fries+soda; course → community.

**Constraint.** The cross-sell must not "break the business" — it should plug into existing infrastructure, audience, and operations. Founders who sell adjacent things they're personally passionate about (vs. things their customers' next problem demands) lose months and millions. Cited example: Hormozi's first deal with the company that wanted to sell what *they* loved; an ask-the-customer survey settled the disagreement, the customer-aligned upsell won, the implementation 2.2× LTV and let the company 5× ad spend profitably (revenue moved from few-millions/month to few-millions/week).

### Lever 5 — Upsell Quantity ("Sell More")
Three sub-shapes:
- **Bulk** — same item, more units, same transaction (1 burger → 2; 1 month service → 12-month prepay).
- **More often** — increase service frequency (monthly pest control → every three weeks). Not applicable to physical products.
- **Bigger** — same time/transaction, larger size or longer duration (1-hour visit → 3-hour visit; normal burger → big burger).

Sales sequence: **offer the upsell first, then downsell to the standard offer if declined.** Documented lift: 20%+ in cash collected.

Mechanism architecture (Classic / Menu / Anchor / Rollover): [[../03-100M-Money-Models/Hormozi-Upsell-Offers|Hormozi — Upsell Offers]]. The Crazy Eight specifies *which dimension* to lift (quantity); the Upsell Offers note specifies *how to present* the lift.

### Lever 6 — Upsell Quality ("Sell Better")
Premium version at higher price. Two flavors: **newer** (newer process, new recipe, updated model) or **premium** (better ingredients, materials, people, or service-feature tier). For services, the "better" axis is unusually rich — Hormozi enumerates ~17 dials:

- Response speed, time availability, days/week, hours/day, call duration, location count, cancellation/reschedule policy, delivery speed, service ratio (1:1 vs 1:many), comm channel (text/chat/video), provider seniority, live vs recorded, in-person vs remote, DFY/DWY/DIY, expirations, personalization (generic / 3–5 avatars / made-for-you), and material/ingredient grade for products.

Sales sequence: **offer premium first, then downsell**. Combine with quantity upsell where useful.

Mechanism architecture: same reuse to [[../03-100M-Money-Models/Hormozi-Upsell-Offers|Upsell Offers]].

### Lever 7 — Downsell Quantity ("Sell Fewer")
Reverse of Lever 5: smaller, less-frequent, or fewer-unit version, offered to prospects who would otherwise not buy at all.

Math intuition: if the standard $5 offer converts 1 of 4 visitors ($1.25/visitor), and a $2.50 downsell converts 2 more of the remaining 3 ($5 / 4 = $1.25 + $1.25 = ~50% lift per visitor), revenue per visitor rises even though average ticket dropped.

**Hard rule:** **never sell a downsell to a qualified prospect**. Downsells are reserved for prospects who otherwise would walk. Cannibalizing your main offer with a self-served discount destroys the math.

Examples: home service goes from 12-month prepay to 3-month; once-monthly visit becomes once-every-other-month; 1-hour visit becomes 30-min; 2-burger order becomes 1-burger.

Mechanism architecture (Payment Plan / Trial-with-Penalty / Feature Downsell): [[../03-100M-Money-Models/Hormozi-Downsell-Offers|Hormozi — Downsell Offers]].

### Lever 8 — Downsell Quality ("Sell Worse")
Same result, lower-quality experience. The Lever-6 list, reversed:
- Slower response, less availability, fewer locations / days / hours, fewer reschedule allowances, junior staff, higher customer:staff ratio, less convenient comm channels, more recorded / less live, more remote / less in-person, DFY→DWY→DIY shift, less personalization, weaker or absent guarantee.
- Physical: lower-grade ingredients/materials, weaker or no warranty, longer wait times.

Same hard rule as Lever 7: **only to unqualified prospects**.

## Strategic Implications

- **The eight are mutually exclusive levers, not a sequence.** They can be applied in any order, in any combination, to any business. The framework is a fluency drill — run all eight against any product mentally to find the next-best move. The compounding effect is that two levers stacked tend to multiply, not add.
- **Pricing is the single highest-leverage lever.** Margin compounds bottom-line faster than any volume lever, because every dollar of price drops to gross profit. This is why Hormozi tests price more frequently than anything else and starts every diagnostic on price.
- **Downsells are recovery margin, not main offer.** Treating downsells as a default move (selling them to qualified prospects) destroys the upsell math. Treating them as the salvage of otherwise-lost prospects is pure additive cash. The discipline boundary between the two is the entire economic difference.
- **The "different thing" trap kills cross-sells.** Founders default to "sell what I love next" instead of "sell what solves the customer's next problem." Customers buying product A do not want a B that lives in a different universe; they want *more of A* or *more help with A* — faster, easier, less risk, less hassle, for more money. Hormozi calls this his favorite upsell of all time.
- **LTV is the arms race.** Two operators with identical traffic costs differ in growth potential by LTV. The operator who can monetize each customer further outbids on every auction. Inflating CPM is not an enemy if your LTV inflates faster.
- **Cap-and-Cadence levers compound through Fast Cash mechanics.** Lever 3's "quarterly promotion" sub-mechanic is the same operational beat as the [[../06-Fast-Cash/Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]] — Crazy Eight #3 is *why* Fast Cash runs every 90 days.

## Practical Use

**Diagnostic loop** (run on any business, in this order):

1. **Establish baseline LTV / LTGP.** Use the formulas in [[../04-100M-Lost-Chapters/Hormozi-CFA-Three-Levers|CFA Three Levers]] (gross profit, churn, transaction count). The LTV book reuses the same Lost-Chapters math step-for-step — that is the canonical home for the calculation.
2. **Pass 1 — Margin levers.** Can price be tested up? Can delivery cost be cut by one of the nine [[Hormozi-Cost-Reduction-Levers|cost-reduction tactics]]? Pick one of each.
3. **Pass 2 — Frequency lever (#3).** Is there a recurring offer? Is churn measured and worked? Is there a quarterly reactivation promo on the calendar?
4. **Pass 3 — Mix levers (#4–#6).** Is there a cross-sell that "doesn't break the business"? Is there a quantity upsell offered first on every sales call? Is there a quality upsell offered first on every sales call?
5. **Pass 4 — Recovery levers (#7–#8).** Are downsells armed for unqualified prospects only? Sales-team rule explicit?

**Tear-sheet (operator action steps, paraphrased pp. 23–24):**
- #1 Set a date for first price test.
- #2 Pick one cost-reduction tactic, set an implementation date.
- #3 Identify a same-day cross-sell that fits existing operations.
- #4 Run the Churn Checklist (separate book).
- #5 Begin offering the quantity upsell *first* on sales calls; downsell to standard.
- #6 Begin offering the quality upsell *first* on sales calls; downsell to standard. Combine with #5 where possible.
- #7 Build a quantity downsell, deploy to unqualified prospects only.
- #8 Build a quality downsell, deploy to unqualified prospects only.

## Notes / Limits

- The Crazy Eight is a **lever inventory**, not a stack-rank. The right next move is always business-specific; the framework's value is preventing blind spots.
- Lever 3's "decrease churn" sub-mechanic is intentionally underspecified in this book — Hormozi defers to the *Churn Checklist Playbook* (separate Acquisition.com title, not yet in this vault) for the full mechanism.
- Levers 5–8 (the four upsell/downsell directions) overlap with the mechanism architecture in `03-100M-Money-Models`. The Crazy Eight specifies *which dimension to move*; Money Models specifies *how to present the move*. They compose; they do not duplicate.
- "Don't break the business" is the one universal constraint — every lever must integrate with existing infrastructure, expertise, and audience. A lever that requires a parallel business is a different business, not an LTV move.
