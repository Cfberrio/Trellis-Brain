# Hormozi — Attraction: Free Pick Your Price

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A specific attraction-stage mechanic: market the offer as fully free, then present a sliding-scale donation/contribution at checkout with bonus tiers. Combines high goodwill with collected cash. Used in low-trust environments and consumer-facing services where pure free attracts but pure paid repels.

## Core Idea
The offer is genuinely free if the prospect chooses $0. At checkout, present a "pick your price" framing — typically positioned as supporting employees / a cause / the artist — with bonus tiers that incentivize paying more. Goodwill is loaded into the structure: paying is voluntary, framed as generosity, and tier bonuses make it feel like a deal at any price. Conversion is high; ticket average is lower than premium but materially above $0.

Origin: Hormozi observed it during COVID at a "Free Car Wash" accepting donations on behalf of staff. Adapted the structure to gym sales, achieved $99 average ticket "more than the average low-barrier offer."

## Framework / Structure

### Mechanic
1. Market the front-end as **free**. No bait-and-switch — the prospect can take it at $0.
2. At checkout (in person or live conversation), present **pick-your-price** framing tied to a believable reason (support employees, cause, artist, etc.).
3. Present **three tiers of bonuses** (small / medium / large) that scale with payment.
4. Make bonuses operationally cheap so giving them away at lower tiers doesn't strain the business.
5. **Pre-frame** the structure at sale opening so the donation ask isn't awkward at close.
6. At close, list tiers and ask: *"We accept Visa, Mastercard, or [payment]. Which would you prefer to use?"* Then shut up.

### Examples (in-source)

**Lemonade Stand.**
- Offer: free lemonade cup.
- Tiers: $5+ → hand-squeezed cup; $25+ → take-home pitcher; $99+ → three months of shipments.

**Car Wash.**
- Offer: free machine wash + soap.
- Tiers: $30+ → wax; $67+ → hand buff; $99+ → full interior.

**Weight Loss.**
- Offer: free 21-day program.
- Tiers: $99 (most pay) → extra 1-1 call; $199+ → full supplement handbook; $499+ → 10lb-loss guarantee or credit toward any service.

**Dentist.**
- Offer: free dental cleaning.
- Tiers: $99 → extra service; $299 → larger extra.

**Coaching.**
- Offer: free coaching/mentorship (group access only).
- Tiers: $299 → course; $997 → 6 group calls.

### Pre-frame Script
At the start of the sale, explain that pick-your-price is the structure, that staff are offering bonuses at different levels, and that prospects are not obligated to pay anything. Frame the payment as going to *staff* (people respond better than to "the business" — Hormozi's observation, source-acknowledged as illogical but real).

### Confrontational Qualifying
With pre-frame established, hit the prospect with **interview-style questions** to gauge long-term fit. The pick-your-price structure attracts goodwill seekers; the qualifying weeds out non-committers. Examples:
- *Are you willing to change the way you do X?*
- *Are you willing to stop doing Y?*
- *What if life gets busy — will you stop showing up?*
- *Will you attend all of these appointments?*

### Constraint: Free Only
Does not work with a discount wrapper. Free is structurally required for the goodwill mechanic — the donation framing breaks if there's a baseline price.

### Paid Variant — Range Pricing
Hormozi observed an art gallery using a paid version: every piece priced as a range ("$149–$299"), framed as letting the buyer choose how much to support the artist. Most pay above the midpoint to avoid seeming cheap. Half goodwill, half capitalism. Different mechanic, same psychological lever.

## Strategic Implications

- **Goodwill is the asset.** This mechanic creates more goodwill than any other free offer because the prospect feels in control of their own destiny and is helping (not being sold to). Sets up long-term relationship value beyond the transaction.
- **High conversion, lower ticket.** Compared to premium or moderate-discount offers, conversion is high (hard to say no to free + voluntary donation). Average ticket lands lower than mid-priced offers but well above $0.
- **Works in low-trust environments.** When the brand has no reputation or the market is skeptical, the pick-your-price framing breaks the trust deficit by giving the prospect full control.
- **Operational design constraint.** The free thing must have low operational cost — you're going to give it away to some prospects. Save the higher-cost bonuses for paying tiers.
- **Two-step is mandatory.** This is structurally a two-step sale: the goodwill-loaded front end converts the prospect into a customer; the next offer (upsell, continuity) is where the real LTGP lives. Treat the donation as optional cash, the upsell as the actual money.

## Practical Use

Decision: use this mechanic when —
- Brand reputation is low / market is cold / regulation discourages "free" without something attached.
- Operations can absorb giving the front-end thing away for $0.
- Sales conversation is one-on-one (in person or phone). Doesn't work on a static landing page.
- A meaningful upsell exists downstream (otherwise the donation is the whole revenue, which under-monetizes).

Build sequence:
1. **Define the free thing** — low operational cost, deliverable to anyone, genuinely valuable.
2. **Define three bonus tiers** — small/medium/large, with bonuses that scale in cost-to-deliver alongside price.
3. **Write the pre-frame** — pick-your-price, beneficiary framing (staff/cause/artist), no obligation.
4. **Write the qualifying interview questions** — fit-focused, not price-focused.
5. **Train the close** — list tiers, ask which payment method, shut up.
6. **Connect to next offer** — confirm the prospect is in a stack that captures upsells/continuity downstream.

## Notes / Limits
- Conversion rate is high; ticket is low. Net revenue depends on stack design downstream, not on this mechanic alone.
- "People feel better paying staff than the business" — Hormozi acknowledges this is illogical but observed it. Frame accordingly; don't argue with the psychology.
- Author note: cut from `$100M Money Models` because Hormozi worried businesses unable to close the upsell would lose money. The mechanic *requires* an upsell capability to be profitable — without it, this is just a free giveaway with sometimes-tips.
- Does not pair with a discount wrapper.
- Range-pricing variant is a related-but-distinct mechanic for paid contexts.
