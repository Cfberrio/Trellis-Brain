# Hormozi — Maker vs Manager

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A work-mode taxonomy for organizing time, calendars, and meetings around two structurally different types of work. Resolves a common organizational productivity loss: managers and makers have opposite calendar economics, and treating them the same destroys maker output. Hormozi: *"If you spend time, or manage people who do, this may be one of the most important things you ever read."* Borrows the title from Paul Graham's 2009 essay; expands with operational guidance.

## Core Idea
There are two ways to manage time, for two different kinds of work:

- **Managers** divide their day into 30–60 minute blocks. 10–20 chunks per day. Empty time = lost opportunity. Goal: fill the calendar.
- **Makers** need half-day or full-day blocks. 1–2 chunks per day, 10–14 per week. Empty time = full productivity. Goal: maximize blank space.

A short meeting from a manager costs the manager one work unit. The same meeting costs the maker an entire work unit (because it splits a 4-hour block into two ~2-hour fragments, neither big enough to do maker's work). **Same meeting, 10× cost asymmetry.** Most workplaces ignore this asymmetry by default.

## Framework / Structure

### Manager Mode

- **Block size**: 30–60 minutes. 10–20 chunks/day.
- **Work content**: meetings, persuading, leading, training, encouraging, decision-making. Each chunk often differs from the last.
- **Day shape**: clear start, clear end. Work until last calendar slot.
- **Empty time**: lost opportunity → fill it.
- **Time as currency**: empty slot is "free" to coordinate. Cost of using it is matching another person's empty slot.
- **Manager's productivity = full calendar.**

### Maker Mode

- **Block size**: half-day or full-day. 1–2 chunks/day, 10–14/week.
- **Work content**: producing things. Known inputs (keystrokes), variable outputs (something different each time). Best makers only switch tasks when a project finishes.
- **Day shape**: similar start time, variable end. Work *open-to-goal* — until quality output per unit time drops. Performance, not the clock, decides done.
- **Most of the work isn't visible.** *"They don't solve problems so they can work, solving is the work."* A maker can be stuck for 45 minutes, then bang — the answer comes. Dry spells last days. Hormozi's case: six months to figure out a table of contents.
- **Empty time = full productivity.**
- **Cost of task switching is monumental.** A 10am 30-minute meeting splits the morning into 2hr + 1.5hr — neither sufficient for immersive maker's work. *"A whole work unit — poof!"*

### The Zeigarnik Effect Tax
Open loops occupy attention more than closed ones. Useful for managers (remembers what's pending). Catastrophic for makers — pending meetings chew through pre-meeting work blocks via clock-watching, intrusive thoughts about what to cover, mental rehearsal of the meeting itself. The maker is "removed" from the work hours before the meeting begins.

### When They Collide
The default productivity disaster. Manager assumes "30 minutes, you've got the free time, what's the big deal?" — true for the manager, false for the maker by 10×. Two failure modes:
1. Maker accepts → loses entire work unit + Zeigarnik tax on adjacent work blocks.
2. Maker declines → offends manager, damages relationship, decreases future collaboration odds.

Result: managers prevent the very work they check in on. Worse, when maker output drops, managers tend to *increase* check-ins, compounding the problem. Lose-lose feedback loop.

### The Solution — Three Audiences

**To managers:**
1. **Understand both costs you put on a maker.** Coordination during their work destroys productivity; the meeting itself eats an entire work block. When you ask for a meeting, it costs the maker 10× what it costs you. Be sure the meeting is worth it.
2. **Understand the value of the maker's no.** It's not rejection — it's them keeping a larger commitment to you and the team to *finish the project*.

**To makers:**
1. **Good managers want to help. Let them.** Send them this framework. *"You will have to take meetings that eat your entire day sometimes."* When that happens, **fight fire with fire — swap to manager calendar style** for that day. Stack admin + feedback + wins + meetings into the lost chunk so other blocks survive.
2. **Make meeting blocks. Defend them.** Standardize "meeting hours" (Hormozi: after 1pm). Schedule meetings back-to-front so any addition lands as late as possible, preserving morning maker time.
3. **Set expectations for slow responses during maker's block.** People adapt if told ahead. They get upset only when expectations aren't met — change them up front.
4. **Work when you say you work.** *"You only get to claim the title of 'maker' if you make stuff."* Wasting protected time confirms managers' worst suspicions and damages the credibility of every maker who needs the same protection.

**Freelancer/solopreneur variant:** No team to block for you. Hormozi's earlier-career routine: 4am–10am maker work, then tend business fires the rest of the day. Nine work blocks/week (one weekday + two each weekend day). With real work happening before/after the managers, interruptions cease.

**To organizations:**
1. **Mandated quiet-time chunks.** No-message, no-meeting periods. Daily hours or full days. Apply to roles that contain makers — engineers, developers, copywriters, builders, presenters, media editors.
2. **Spread the language across the org** so everyone has the same vocabulary for the cost of *"got a min?"*

## Strategic Implications

- **The cost asymmetry is structural, not preferential.** Maker output requires uninterrupted blocks; manager output requires coordination. Treating them as preference issues ("just work like me") destroys output by design.
- **Calendar geography is a leadership decision.** Where meetings sit in the day is upstream of how much maker work the org produces. Default of "fill the morning with sync" inverts the right structure.
- **Maker's "no" is information, not insubordination.** Managers who interpret "I can't make this" as a signal of priority discipline get more output than managers who interpret it as availability.
- **The Zeigarnik tax is invisible but enormous.** A 1pm meeting affects 10am work via mental anticipation. Eliminating mid-day meetings is often higher leverage than shortening them.
- **Roles in growing orgs drift toward manager mode by default.** Writers become editors who become VPs of editorial; the maker layer thins. The org loses its production engine if the drift isn't actively countered.
- **The freelancer schedule is a forced solution.** Working before/after managers exist is the fallback when no one will protect your blocks. Effective; also exhausting and antisocial. Org-level fixes are better.

## Practical Use

For makers:
1. **Block your maker hours.** Default: morning (Hormozi's default: full first half of day) or before/after standard work hours.
2. **Designate meeting hours.** Default: afternoon. Schedule incoming requests back-to-front.
3. **Write the boundary.** Tell collaborators when you're slow-to-respond. Once. Then keep the boundary.
4. **Stack lost days.** When a meeting eats a block, intentionally fill the rest with admin, feedback, and wins. Save other blocks.
5. **Earn the title.** During protected blocks, produce. Otherwise the protection erodes.

For managers of makers:
1. **Default to async.** Slack/email/doc reviews don't fragment maker time. Use them.
2. **Bundle requests.** Aggregate questions into one weekly 1:1, not five 15-minute pings.
3. **Check the maker's calendar before scheduling.** Their gaps are not invitations.
4. **Take "no" as discipline, not absence.**
5. **Spread the framework.** Send the source essay or this note. Common vocabulary reduces friction.

For orgs:
1. **Pick a quiet window** and enforce it (e.g., no meetings before noon, or one full no-meeting day weekly).
2. **Audit roles** — who is actually a maker? They need the protection.
3. **Measure output by maker metric** — features shipped, content produced, deals built — not meeting count.

## Notes / Limits
- Source borrows the maker/manager distinction from Paul Graham's 2009 essay "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule." Hormozi's contribution is operational depth (specific scheduling rules, freelancer variant, org-level interventions) and the Zeigarnik tax framing.
- "Maker work has highest importance, lowest urgency" — true for many makers but not all. Bug fixes and customer-incident response are urgent maker work; same task-switching cost applies.
- Group sizes matter. A 5-person team's mandated quiet hours scale differently than a 500-person organization's. The mechanism is the same; rollout differs.
- Title-of-maker discipline: the framework requires the maker to actually make during protected time. If they don't, the protection collapses for everyone using the same vocabulary in the org.
- Manager mode isn't lesser — it's different. Both are necessary; the failure mode is treating either as the default for the other's work.
