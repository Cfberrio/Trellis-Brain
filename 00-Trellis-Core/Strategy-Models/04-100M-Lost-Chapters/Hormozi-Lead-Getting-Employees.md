# Hormozi — Lead-Getting Employees

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The expanded employees framework cut from `$100M Leads` for "creeping into operations." Defines how to get, train, manage, and diagnose performance of employees whose job is generating leads. Centers on a single insight — **getting employees is the same problem as getting customers** — and uses the same Core Four to solve it. Adds the 3Ds training method, the Performance Diamond diagnostic, and the lead-getter unit-economics math.

## Core Idea
Lead-getting is a system that scales with people, not effort. To advertise more, you hire more. The trade ratio Hormozi observes: every 40 hours of doing-work converts to ~4 hours of managing-work — so 200 hours/week of work becomes 20 hours of management, then 4 hours of managing the manager. This compounds. *"Employees make a fully functioning enterprise that grows without you."*

A second insight: **owning a business that needs you isn't ownership, it's a high-paying job.** A business that runs without the owner becomes a sellable asset, not just a paycheck. *"You get rich from what you make. You become wealthy from what you own."* Replacing yourself with employees is the operational path from rich to wealthy.

## Framework / Structure

### Belief Reset
Hormozi's stated wrong beliefs:
- *"If you want it done right, you gotta do it yourself."*
- *"No one can do it like I do it."*
- *"Nobody can replace me."*

Replaced with:
- *"If you want it done right, get someone to spend all their time doing it."*
- *"If I can do it, someone else can do it better."*
- *"Everyone is replaceable, especially me."*

The "I can do it better than them" trap: maybe true on any one task, but false across all tasks. The owner is one person; the workforce is a workforce. Outcompeting employees distracts from running the business. The owner trained them; if they're bad, the owner is the variable.

### The Five Lead Stages
Standard lead lifecycle, used in this chapter as a parallel to the hiring pipeline:
```
Uncontacted → Contacted → Engaged → Qualified → Sold
```
1. **Uncontacted.** Action: advertise to them.
2. **Contacted.** Action: incentivize engagement with lead magnet/offer.
3. **Engaged.** Action: qualify on problems + money.
4. **Qualified.** Action: sell — make an offer, fulfill in exchange for money.
5. **Sold.** They exchanged money for what you offered.

### Hiring = Customer Acquisition (the central insight)
The process of getting attention is the same. The process of helping make decisions is the same. Different vocabulary, identical mechanism. *"Humans are humans."*

#### The Internal Core Four (mapping to $100M Leads' Core Four)

| Customers | Employees |
|---|---|
| Warm Outreach | Asking your network |
| Cold Outreach | Recruiting |
| Post Content | Posting job openings |
| Paid Ads | Promoting job postings |
| Customer Referrals | Employee referrals |
| Affiliates | Associations, guilds, listservs |
| Agencies | Staffing firms |
| Employees | Employees (unchanged) |

When you need talent, you advertise for it. When you need more, you do more. Same reliable-process logic as customer acquisition.

### Training — The 3Ds Model

Used to train any new hire on any lead-getting activity (or any operational task).

**Document.**
Write down the steps exactly as you do them. Bonus: record yourself across multiple shifts and review as observer. Test: can you do the job A+ following only your own checklist? If yes, you have draft 1.

**Demonstrate.**
Do it in front of the trainee, walking through the checklist. If they slow you down or ask a clarifying question, update the checklist. That's draft 2.

**Duplicate.**
They do it in front of you, following the same checklist. You observe. If the outcome differs from yours, fix the checklist. *"If they get it wrong or get confused, then we got it wrong or made it confusing."*

#### Training principles
- If a step needs repeated explanation, the step is too complicated or compounds multiple steps.
- *"Competence vs performance"* — they can know what to do and not be good at it yet. Practice, not retraining. *"Slow then smooth then fast."*
- **Focus on direction-following, not outcomes.** If they follow directions and get the wrong result, the directions are wrong (controllable). Easier than re-engineering the human.
- Praise correct steps as they happen.
- If they follow exactly and get the wrong result, praise the following + fix the checklist on the spot.
- No punishment during training. Reward what you want more of.
- Fix one step at a time. Multiple corrections at once block learning.

### Keeping Performance — The Performance Diamond

Once trained, maintain performance with frequent check-ins (Hormozi: **6–11 meetings per week per lead-getter**, mostly short).

**Cadence:**
- One 30–45 minute weekly 1:1 — coaching, feedback, praise.
- Daily huddles at start + end of each shift — expectations + reporting. A few minutes each. Fast feedback cycles for skill and morale.
- Recurring questions/issues feed back into the training checklist for the next hire.
- Once stable, manager can take over huddles, then 1:1s. If performance holds, integration is complete.

#### Four Causes of Performance Drop

When performance drops, the issue is one of four:

**1. Communication — they don't know THAT we want it.**
Sounds like: *"I didn't know you wanted me to do that,"* *"I didn't know you wanted me to do that first,"* *"I've got too much on my plate."*
Fix: have them summarize where, when, how much they're supposed to perform. Compare to your version. Mismatch → you didn't communicate clearly.

**2. Training — they don't know HOW.**
Sounds like: *"I don't know how to do that,"* *"I didn't know you wanted me to do it that way."*
Fix: don't ask "do you understand?" — they're conditioned to say yes. Have them *show* you. Treat as first-time training, with patience and fast feedback.

**3. Motivation — they don't WANT to.**
Three sub-causes:
- Reward at wrong time / frequency / intensity / source.
- Aversion to work / environment / leadership / coworkers / consumers.
- Lifestyle/event factors: positive (newborn, wedding), negative (death in family, divorce), or chronic (poor sleep, alcohol, illness).
Fix: ask open-ended. Adjust reward, environment, or acknowledge external life cost.

**Best diamond-hard feedback question** (from Leila Hormozi):
> *"Over the last [length of time] your [task performance] has changed from the norm. What do you think has gotten in the way and how can I help?"*
Sets up collaborative problem-solving, not blame.

**4. Circumstances — something is stopping them.**
Examples: out of beef → can't grill burgers. Slow internet → can't edit video. Broken phone → can't call. The owner expected the employee to solve a problem the owner should have prevented.
Fix: ask. Don't shoot the messenger. These are the easiest fixes when you spot them.

### Lead-Getter Returns Math

Compute return on lead-getting payroll (excluding paid ad spend, which is computed separately):

```
Total Payroll ÷ Total Engaged Leads = Cost per Engaged Lead
Cost per Engaged Lead × Engaged-leads-per-customer = CAC
LTGP ÷ CAC = LTGP:CAC ratio (target ≥ 3:1)
```

Worked example: $100k payroll / 1,000 leads = $100 per engaged lead. 10 leads per customer → $1,000 CAC. $4,000 LTGP → 4:1 ratio.

Acquisition.com benchmark (at time of writing): ~30,000 engaged leads/month. No paid ads. No outreach. Content team payroll ~$100k/mo. Cost per engaged lead = $3.33. Profitable at any reasonable LTGP.

### Diagnostic — Which Employees to Focus On

If `CAC ≤ 3× industry average`: focus on raising LTGP. You're acquiring fine.

If `CAC > 3× industry average`: you have either an advertising or sales problem. Diagnose with one question:

> *Do my engaged leads have the problem I solve and the money to spend?*

- **No** → not qualified → **advertising problem**. Wrong audience.
- **Yes** + not enough volume → **advertising problem**. Right audience, not enough.
- **Yes** + qualified but not buying → **sales problem**. Don't fire the sales rep for a marketing miss; don't fire marketing for a sales miss.

Costs of getting a customer should sum to ≤ 1/3 of LTGP for a healthy business.

## Strategic Implications

- **Hiring is acquisition, not HR.** Treating recruiting like a customer-acquisition problem (with funnel discipline, multiple channels, conversion metrics) outperforms treating it like a procurement problem.
- **Replaceability is the goal, including yours.** Owner-as-bottleneck is the structural cap on every business with one. Designing the business such that the owner is the most replaceable role is the strategic move.
- **Training quality is upstream of performance management.** Most "performance problems" are actually training failures or communication failures. The Performance Diamond exists because owners blame the wrong cause by default.
- **Skill > talent at the lead-getting level.** *"Anyone can be taught to do 'ground level' jobs for any business."* Iron will is a learned skill, not innate. Pick anyone willing; train well; treat the winners well.
- **Daily huddles are the real management unit.** The 30-min weekly 1:1 is for direction; the daily 5-minute huddles are where skills compound. Most teams have the inverse cadence and underperform because of it.
- **Costs as 1/3 of LTGP is the operating budget.** Above that, scale brittleness; well below, leaving acquisition money on the table.

## Practical Use

To build a lead-getting team from scratch:

1. **Document your own lead-getting process** in checklist form. Record yourself.
2. **Cast wide for hires.** Use the Internal Core Four. Group-screen if 1-on-1 screening is the bottleneck (Hormozi's case study: 40 interviews → 1 hire fixed by group screens).
3. **Hire willing > skilled.** For ground-level lead-getting, willingness + work ethic beats prior experience.
4. **Train via 3Ds.** Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate. Iterate the checklist with each trainee.
5. **Install the Performance Diamond cadence.** Daily huddles + weekly 1:1s. ~6–11 touchpoints per week.
6. **Monitor returns weekly.** Cost per engaged lead, CAC, LTGP:CAC. Cut channels and roles that under-deliver.
7. **Diagnose performance drops with the four-cause framework** before discipline. Most are communication/training/circumstance, not motivation.
8. **Promote from within when winners emerge.** Treat them well, don't burn them out, give them what they deserve.

To diagnose a stalled acquisition function:
1. Compute CAC. Compare to industry × 3.
2. If above, ask the qualified-and-money question. Route to advertising fix or sales fix accordingly.
3. If within, focus on raising LTGP via [[Hormozi-Offer-Stacking|Offer Stacking]] and continuity mechanics.

## Notes / Limits
- Author note: chapter cut from `$100M Leads` for being too long and creeping into general management. The framework leans operational; expect to need other knowledge for senior hires (no playbook here for "fancy employees" / six-figure C-suite).
- The 6–11 meetings/week cadence is for **lead-getting frontline employees**, not knowledge workers. Knowledge work has different rhythms — see [[Hormozi-Maker-vs-Manager|Maker vs Manager]].
- Group-screening worked for low-skill, high-volume frontline hiring. Different roles need different screening.
- The "iron will is a skill, not innate" claim is a source position, not a peer-reviewed result. Treat as operational guidance.
- Acquisition.com cost-per-engaged-lead figure is benchmark for *content-driven* acquisition. Outbound or paid will run materially higher.
