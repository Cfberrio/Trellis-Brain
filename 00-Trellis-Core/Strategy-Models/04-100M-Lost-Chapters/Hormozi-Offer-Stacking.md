# Hormozi — Offer Stacking

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The choreography rule for sequencing multiple Grand Slam Offers across a customer's first 30–90 days. Distinct from `Hormozi-Offer-Creation` in `01-100M-Offers` (which handles construction of *one* offer). This note handles the **multi-offer sequence**: which offer at which stage, and how to chain them so each prospect generates as much 30D Cash and LTGP as possible without operational drag.

## Core Idea
Money models are built from a series of offers, not one. Each stage of the model has different offer types that fit it. Stacking those offers — Attraction → Up Front Cash → Upsell/Downsell → Continuity, repeated — multiplies LTV and lets you outspend any competitor on any platform. Hormozi: *"In many ways, I wrote all of `$100M Money Models` to get to this part."*

## Framework / Structure

### The Stacking Sequence
Standard order Hormozi uses across nearly every business he advises:

```
1. Attract → 2. Up Front Cash → 3. Upsell/Downsell → 4. Continuity
```

Then often repeated:
```
→ 5. Continuity #2 → 6. Up Front Cash #2
```

Each step has a distinct economic role:
- **Attract** (free or discount wrapper) — generate flow into the business cheaply.
- **Up Front Cash** — sell the high-ticket primary offer to liquidate acquisition cost in 30 days.
- **Upsell / Downsell** — capture whales who'll buy big and minnows who'll buy something. Downsells exist so "no" never ends the conversation.
- **Continuity** — convert one-shot revenue into recurring cash flow.
- **Repeat** — additional continuity, prepayment-for-discount cycles, second up-front cash injection.

### Identify Adjacent Customer Needs
Before adding offers, list every revenue stream a customer is buying that's adjacent to the core desire you serve (power, money, beauty, weight loss, etc.). Then either:
- **Affiliate** — partner with another business that pays you to send them customers. Hormozi's affiliate-commission take to date: $3M+, near-pure-profit.
- **Add directly** — if it costs little to add operationally (e.g., a sales consult to sell physical products is "one meeting" of added complexity).
- **Acquire** — if the referred-out revenue is large enough, buy or build that business.

**Onboarding hack — Free Onboarding via Affiliate.** Add an extra onboarding call for new clients during which you ensure they click each affiliate link they need. Affiliate commissions cover the entire cost of the onboarding/support team after month one. Customers get more support; you get a self-funding service team.

### The Sample Weight-Loss Flow (worked example)
Four-sale sequence over 6–12 weeks:

**Sale #1 — Up Front Cash (Service Sale).**
- Pitch high-ticket primary first. Cascade of downsells: half down → quarter down with payment plan → quarter down no payment plan → free trial with card on file → complementary nutrition orientation. The "no" route still funnels into Sale #2.

**Sale #2 — Upsell (Physical Products).**
- At nutrition orientation 24–72 hours later: 3-month full-stack supplement bundle. Downsells: 1-month + subscription discount → essentials only → 1–2 must-have products → cross-sell to a partnered meal-prep company.
- *Pro tip:* Cash from products at orientation covered Hormozi's onboarding payroll and trainers earned more per hour than personal-training rates.

**Sale #3 — Continuity (Feedback Meeting).**
- Frame as feedback gathering 2–3 weeks in. Use to (a) collect product feedback, (b) save unhappy customers, (c) upsell long-haul. Happy → high-ticket prepay, downsell to monthly continuity. Unhappy → upsell into higher-support tier ("VIP program," credit prior payment toward upgrade). *Every problem is an upsell opportunity.*

**Sale #4 — Up Front Cash #2 / Continuity Downsell.**
- Prepay sale before treatment ends. Captures the people who said "not sure" earlier. Also serves as upsell for those already on continuity → prepay-for-discount.

### The Right Offer Selection Rule
After identifying needs and stage, pick each offer using four filters in order:

1. **Right Stage.** Match offer type to money-model stage. Need leads → Attraction. Need 30D Cash → Upsell/Downsell. Need LTGP → Continuity.
2. **Right Problem.** Pick problems that (a) make sense for your business, (b) can be solved with existing resources, (c) provide big value when solved. You can't solve them all.
3. **Right Way.** Solve based on customer preference. Two people want to lose weight: one wants workouts, the other wants nutrition. Don't argue them into your way; offer their way (or both).
4. **Right Time.** Offer at the moment of greatest need. Hungry person says yes to steak; full person says no to the same steak. Sell at peak need, not at your operational convenience.

### Operational Warning
Adding offers adds operational complexity. Heuristic: high profit-to-complexity ratio = green light. Hormozi's frame is **accommodating-resistance** from exercise physiology — the perfect rep matches your force capability at every point. Match ticket price and value to each customer's buying ability and desire without bloating the business.

## Strategic Implications

- **One Grand Slam Offer is not the goal — a sequence of Grand Slam Offers is.** Each downsell and upsell is itself a GSO, properly constructed. The stack is what produces the 14–15× revenue lift visible in the [[Hormozi-Value-Grid|Value Grid]].
- **"No" advances the prospect.** In a stacked sequence, the prospect always advances to the next offer regardless of answer. This is the structural reason "no sale sales" exist — see [[Hormozi-Upsell-Free-Alt-Revenue|Free with Alternate Revenue Stream]]. Most businesses lose 100% of revenue from the prospects who say no to the front end. Stacking captures some of it.
- **One-on-one sales is the high-leverage delivery mode.** Selling off a static digital page cannot improvise downsells in real time. Phone or in-person sales lets you match buying power and need exactly. Competing against off-page sellers, a one-on-one stack almost always outspends them.
- **The information advantage is the substrate.** "All businesses capitalize on an information advantage." You know the prospect's downstream problems better than they do; the stack walks them through those problems and monetizes each one.
- **Order the additions by ROI / complexity.** Don't add the whole stack at once. Add the next conversion opportunity that delivers the most money at the lowest operational cost; once it works, add the next.

## Practical Use

To build (or upgrade) a money model:

1. **List adjacent customer needs.** Map every revenue stream your customer is already paying someone for that's near your core service.
2. **Decide affiliate vs add vs acquire** for each adjacent stream.
3. **Slot the stack.** Pick one offer for each stage:
   - Attract: which wrapper? (See [[Hormozi-Promotion-Wrappers|Promotion Wrappers]].)
   - Up Front Cash: high-ticket primary GSO.
   - Upsell/Downsell: physical product, complementary service, partnered cross-sell.
   - Continuity: monthly recurring (see [[Hormozi-Continuity-Lifetime-Upgrades|Lifetime Upgrades]] / [[Hormozi-Continuity-Lifetime-Discounts|Lifetime Discounts]] / [[Hormozi-Continuity-Discount-Plus-Fee|Discount + Fee]]).
4. **Apply the four filters** (Stage, Problem, Way, Time) to each offer before adding it.
5. **Choreograph downsells** under each main offer so "no" funnels forward.
6. **Add sequentially.** Start with the highest-revenue / lowest-complexity addition. Stabilize. Add the next.
7. **Read results off the [[Hormozi-Value-Grid|Value Grid]].** If 30D Cash isn't moving, the offers are misordered or one filter is failing.

Diagnostic shortcut: *People want their problem solved in different ways. Offering multiple ways to solve it gives you more at-bats.* If the stack has one offer per stage and one problem per offer, expand the stack — one offer is leaving revenue on the table.

## Notes / Limits
- This note distills the *sequence and selection* logic. The construction of each individual offer (Problems → Solutions → Trim & Stack) lives in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Offer-Creation|Hormozi — Offer Creation]] and is not duplicated here. They are deliberately separate frameworks: Offer Creation builds one GSO; Offer Stacking sequences many.
- The four-filter rule (Stage / Problem / Way / Time) is decision logic, not a scoring rubric. There is no numeric output.
- The 6–12 week sample flow is a fitness/coaching template. B2B SaaS, e-commerce, agency models keep the same four-stage structure but shift the timing and offer types substantially.
- One-on-one sales is preferred but not required. Fully digital stacks work; they cap at lower realized take-rates because real-time downsells aren't possible.
- "Adding more offers" is bounded by operational capacity and brand consistency. A stack that confuses or fatigues customers reduces LTGP, not increases it.
