# Hormozi — Promotion Wrappers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A taxonomy and selection framework for the three "wrappers" that go around a Grand Slam Offer to drive cold traffic through the front end: **Premium**, **Free**, and **Discount**. Used to decide which wrapper to lead with given current resources, market temperature, regulation, and operational capacity. Includes the friction-tuning levers for Free offers and the four display forms for Discount offers.

## Core Idea
The promotion is **wrapping paper around the Grand Slam Offer, not a replacement for it**. The wrapper exists to give a cold audience a reason to move toward you and answer "what's in it for me." The underlying gift (the GSO) does not change. Choice of wrapper governs the trade between lead volume, lead cost, lead quality, and operational drag.

Working order Hormozi uses entering a new market: **Generate Flow → Monetize Flow → Add Friction.** Start with free or massively discounted to confirm demand and build conviction, monetize once it's flowing, then dial friction up to skim quality.

## Framework / Structure

Three wrappers, each with distinct economics. All wrap the same core GSO.

### Premium Wrapper

**What it is.** Present the GSO at full price, no discount, no trial. Conversion runs through copy + sales process.

**Worked math.** Same 190 leads:
- $50 profit × 190 = $9,500
- $9,500 profit × 5% × 190 = ~$90,250 if those same 190 are pitched a $10,000 offer
- Result: ~9.5× more profitable on identical traffic, with 9–10 clients to service instead of 190.

**Pros**
1. Simplest math, fewest moving parts (one core thing to sell).
2. Only "quality" customers — decreases operational drag.
3. No discounts/incentives — high lead quality, transparent buyer intent.

**Cons**
1. Most expensive cost per lead. Less room for error per opportunity.
2. Requires deep avatar knowledge — copy carries the conversion weight.
3. Requires good sales — no discount safety net.
4. Least efficient way to capture a marketplace (low volume).
5. Requires an extremely compelling offer (bonuses, guarantees, premium support) so it still feels like a steal at the higher price.

**When to lead with it.** Established business with existing reputation, dialed sales process, sufficient capital to absorb learning costs, niche where avatar is known cold. Hormozi: "If I lost everything and had to start over, I would NOT start with a premium offer."

### Free Wrapper

**What it is.** Lead with free or near-free. Hershey-kiss / penny-gap effect (Dan Ariely): 9× more take-up at $0 vs $0.01.

**Pros**
1. Highest lead volume — best, middle, and worst caliber in one shot.
2. Lowest lead cost — same eyeballs, higher hand-raise %.
3. Massive companies became massive on free front ends: Facebook, YouTube, Dropbox, Uber, Netflix, Claire's free piercings, European Wax Center free wax, Public Storage free month.

**Cons**
1. Volume is double-edged — operational and quality drag without friction.
2. Some leads have no intention to buy (manageable via offer design and friction).

**Counter-myth: "Free brings broke people."** Hormozi ran four split tests of free vs premium across 10 representative markets. Close rate was identical. Free vs non-free reduced lead cost by 5× or more. Mistake people make: pricing premium offers like free offers. Price/Prospect/Process/Promotion/Product all need to match the same wrapper structure — they cannot mix-and-match.

**Believability requirement.** Crazy free offers force the next question: *why?* Give a real reason ("going out of business," "founders' discount," "grand opening," "we want feedback"). Hormozi's referenced anti-example: "$100 for $1,000" ad — never responded to because too good without a reason.

#### Five Friction Levers (when Free needs quality-tuning)
Used to skim cream from a high-volume free offer without killing the funnel.
1. **Increased Qualifications.** State requirements explicitly across copy/creative/landing ("must be 25+, employed, homeowner"). Repeat everywhere.
2. **Increased Information Requirement & Question Type.** More fields, heavier fields (cell phone, income > first name), open-ended vs multiple-choice.
3. **Increased Number of Steps.** One-step opt-in vs 5-step form. Each step drops volume and lifts quality.
4. **Forced Consumption.** Require X minutes of video before any CTA appears. Gates only pre-indoctrinated leads. Best when audience is large and eyeballs are cheap.
5. **Advertisement Length.** Long-form copy/video itself filters by time investment. Fewer clicks, higher per-click value.

**Free-money math heuristic.** $1,000 → 500 leads, 250 qualified beats $1,000 → 200 leads, 160 qualified, despite higher unqualified count. Pure dollars and cents over feeling.

**Hormozi's stated default.** "If I only had one offer to make to convert or my family would be killed, it would be a free offer."

### Discount Wrapper

**What it is.** Massive discount (50%+) on a sliver of the offer, not the whole farm. Marginal discounts (5–25%) are ineffective and just cut margin. Used as the front of a two-step sale.

#### Four Discount Display Forms
Same promotion, different framings. Test all four; multiple winners give you bullets when one fatigues.
1. **Percentage Off** — "87% off first visit."
2. **Absolute Amount Off** — "$181 off first week (normally $210)."
3. **Relative Equivalent Off** — said negatively ("Save a steak dinner") or positively ("Less than going out to lunch"). Anchored to a familiar reference price.
4. **Simply Discounted Price** — "$29 New Client Special." Best when the audience already knows the normal price.

Display rule: only use absolute prices for **well-understood services** (dentist, chiropractor, gym, haircut). For unfamiliar services, pair with the original price ("Normally $210, today $29") so the discount has a reference.

**Pros**
1. Lots of cheap leads within regulated markets where "free" is restricted.
2. Some money collected up front — partial offset to acquisition cost.
3. Buyers come in expecting to spend (mental advantage for sales team and small-business owners with limiting beliefs).
4. **The two-step sale (probably the biggest benefit)** — card on file, virtually eliminates no-shows, charges no-show fee possible.
5. Upsells become "use the card you have on file?" frictionless. Amazon one-click / Disney wristband logic — every removed friction lifts conversion.

**Cons**
1. "Giving away the farm" if the *core* offer is what's discounted (trains customers to buy only on sale). Solve by splintering the offer: discount a small piece, not the whole thing.
2. Bargain hoppers (Groupon problem) — solved by intelligent offer structure, treating discount-takers as qualified leads, not customers.

**Pro Tip — close-rate equivalence.** A good salesperson closes the same % on free vs non-free leads when funnel and environment are equal. The sales-comfort advantage of discount over free is psychological for the seller, not real for the prospect.

**Pro Tip — Proprietary 5-Min Appointment Method.** When a high-cost professional's time is the bottleneck (doctor, lawyer), don't give away their time. Operationalize the first visit so a front-desk admin handles intake (info, X-rays, financing pre-approval, no-show fee card capture). Reserve the doctor for a 5-minute hello between appointments and the second appointment for the real treatment-plan sale. Maximizes available appointment slots — the single biggest lever on schedule rate.

## Strategic Implications

- **Wrapper is a function of stage, not preference.** New entry / no reputation / weak conviction → Free or Discount. Established / dialed sales process → Premium. The Hormozi default for a new market is Free or massively discounted, then add friction over time.
- **Components must match the wrapper.** Pricing/copy/process/funnel built for a Free offer cannot be repurposed for Premium without rebuilding all five. This is the single most common diagnostic error when comparing wrapper performance.
- **Discounts shine in well-understood markets.** Where the prospect knows the normal price, the discount has anchor. Where they don't, free or premium framing usually outperforms.
- **Friction is a quality dial, not a defect.** Adding friction reduces volume by design to skim quality. Used after Generate Flow, never before.
- **Information advantage is the underlying business model.** "All businesses capitalize on an information advantage" — the seller knows the customer's downstream problems better than the customer does. Wrappers exist to start the relationship; upsells convert the information advantage into LTGP.

## Practical Use

Decision tree:

1. **What is the goal of this acquisition?**
   - Volume / ice-cold market / regulated → Free (or Discount if regulation forbids free).
   - Immediate cash + pre-card-on-file + no-show elimination → Discount.
   - Capture only highest-intent buyers, dialed avatar/sales → Premium.
2. **Match all five components** (Price, Prospect, Process, Promotion, Product) to the chosen wrapper. Do not mix.
3. **For Free**: design the offer so cost-of-fulfillment is low; tune friction with the 5-lever menu after volume is validated.
4. **For Discount**: splinter the offer (don't discount the core); pick one of the four display forms; use the discount as step one of a two-step sale; capture card.
5. **For Premium**: ensure the offer is genuinely irresistible (bonuses, guarantees, premium support); accept lower volume; verify capital + sales process maturity.

Section conclusion (Hormozi's words): *"If you are not getting enough response, you probably need to make your front end more appealing."*

## Notes / Limits
- The 9× penny-gap result generalizes the *direction*, not the magnitude — gap size varies by category and price point.
- Premium close-rate of 5% on a $10k offer is illustrative, not universal. The math demonstrates the leverage; actual conversion depends on avatar fit and sales process.
- Free + discount offers do not replace the Grand Slam Offer — they wrap it. The GSO must already exist underneath.
- The "no-shows for paid trials are 85–90%+" number is an experience benchmark, not a guarantee.
