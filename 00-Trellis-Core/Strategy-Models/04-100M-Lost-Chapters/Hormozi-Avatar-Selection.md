# Hormozi — Avatar Selection

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A four-step process for narrowing the customer base to the highest-value subset and engineering the marketing + sales process around them. Hormozi's stated counter to "find a niche" beginner advice. Used as the upstream lever before any offer or acquisition decision: deciding *who you serve* sets the ceiling for every other framework. Hormozi states he uses this with every company he advises and would have included it in `$100M Offers` if rewriting it today.

## Core Idea
Profit comes from a premium you can charge for the *same work*. The premium is set by **who the customer is**, not who you are. So the highest-leverage move is not improving the offer — it is selecting the customers worth the most and engineering your business around them. Origin: a Vista Equity method for analyzing acquisition targets — score existing customers by how long they stay × how much they pay, kill channels that bring low-value customers, double down on channels that bring the top tier. Same market, different segmentation, monstrously different results.

## Framework / Structure

Four-step process to install in any business with existing customers.

### 1. Survey existing customers
Send a form (or higher-engagement: live event / call) covering every relevant attribute. Hormozi's working categories for a business-services audience:
- **Demographics** — age, gender, political lean, geography, digital location, marital/business partnership status.
- **Business stats (before & current)** — revenue, profit, employee count, churn, pricing, product mix, LTV, customer count, niche, time in business.
- **Aspirations** — goal at time of purchase, problem they were trying to solve.
- **Buying process** — single biggest reason they bought, trigger event, specific content/testimonial consumed, number of pieces consumed, time from first hearing about you to buying, first-touch source, referral status.

### 2. Find the biggest spenders
Sort responses by customers you (a) like the most, (b) who spent the most, (c) who stayed the longest. Focus on the **top 20%**. Ignore the rest.

### 3. See what they have in common
Read the top-20% answers and find the **fewest qualifiers they all share**. Usually three to five. Hormozi's Gym Launch result: right-leaning conservative, married, 25–45, male, gym owner, US-based, signed lease, 1+ employees, $10k+/mo revenue, 30+ existing clients, aspiration of $1M+ gym + open more locations + work less, primary buying triggers were "not enough leads / bad market / bad pricing / can't find good employees."

### 4. Execute on two fronts
**(a) Speak the new avatar.** State customer requirements up front in advertising. Repel non-fits. Stop selling anyone outside the profile. Increase spend on the channels these people come through.
**(b) Reverse-engineer the buying process.** Look at what the best customers consumed before buying and engineer that experience for everyone. Gym Launch finding: 78% of top customers consumed at least two long-form content pieces before buying — so the team injected two long-form pieces into the lead journey, increased content output, and built an "all-time greatest hits" list for sales reps to hand-select 2–3 pieces per prospect. Genuine value-in-advance, not disguised pitches.

## Strategic Implications

- **Same work, different customer = different price.** A sales-page designer who lifts conversion from 5% → 7% creates $40k/yr of value for a $100k/mo client and $4M/yr for a $10M/mo client. The job, hours, and skill are constant; the price ceiling is set by the customer's economics. This is the structural argument for moving up-market.
- **Start with what you know if you have no customers.** VC-style: industry experience compounds. Pick the industry where you have inside knowledge from friends/family/past jobs. Narrow target → serve them first → re-run the survey once you have customers to analyze.
- **Quality > Quantity in the buyer journey.** Removing qualification steps reliably increased lead volume and decreased revenue in Hormozi's tests. "I would rather pay $5,000 to acquire $45,000 than $1,000 to acquire $5,000." The whole funnel — including the friction — is the qualifier.
- **LTGP as the score.** Average Gym Launch competitor ran $6k–$8k LTGP. Gym Launch ran $45k+ LTGP, a 6–8× LTGP gap that produced "breathtakingly different" downstream profit because costs barely move with LTGP.
- **Sell to people who don't stop buying.** Improve the product so customers keep buying, OR only sell to customers who historically keep buying. Either solves retention. This chapter handles the second route.

## Practical Use

Trigger this process when:
- the business already has customers (otherwise apply the "Start with what you know" fallback).
- CAC is rising or feels uneconomic.
- you suspect channel mix is bringing in low-LTGP customers.
- you are about to write new ads / landing pages / sales scripts (so the new avatar voices them, not the old one).

Operating sequence:
1. Build the survey. Tie completion to a small benefit so response rates are high.
2. Sort and isolate the top 20% by retention × spend × subjective fit.
3. Distill the 3–5 shared qualifiers.
4. Rewrite ads, landing pages, and sales-call openings to state requirements explicitly. Cut channels with low concentration of the avatar; increase spend on the high-concentration channels.
5. Map the buying journey of the top 20%. Identify content pieces / testimonials / referral patterns. Force those into the standard prospect path.
6. Re-run quarterly or after meaningful product/pricing change.

Force-multiplier: this is upstream of every other Lost Chapters framework. Promotion wrappers, CFA math, offer stacking — all amplify whatever avatar you've selected. A wrong avatar burns spend at every downstream stage.

## Notes / Limits
- "Pulse-and-credit-card" segmentation produces low retention, low margin, generic advice, and high CAC. Hormozi's comparison case: a fitness-services competitor making 70× less profit on the same vertical and same total sales volume.
- Short-term revenue typically dips during the avatar reset because non-fit customers exit. The bet is that long-term retention + gross margin offset the dip.
- The 78% / two-pieces-of-content finding is Gym Launch–specific. The *process* generalizes; the threshold does not.
- This framework presumes existing customer data. With zero customers, fall back to the "industry you know" heuristic and re-run after first cohort.
