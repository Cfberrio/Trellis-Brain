# Hormozi — Continuity: Lifetime Upgrades

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A continuity-stage mechanic for **stickiness**: keep customers paying longer by stacking bonuses across their tenure. Different from `01-100M-Offers` Bonuses (which are offer-construction enhancements, used pre-purchase). This note is post-purchase: bonuses are tools for *retention*, not conversion. Defines the when × what bonus matrix.

## Core Idea
A good offer gets customers to start. **Good bonuses, given over time, get them to stick.** Most stickiness work treats retention as a fix-after-cancel problem. This mechanic engineers retention into the continuity offer up front: the customer always knows another bonus is coming, so leaving costs them future value.

Origin: Hormozi sold a $42,000/year, 3-year continuity to a gym owner with no product ready — just a promise of a new money-making play every 14 days. He delivered for nearly two years. Gym Launch revenue went from ~$300k/mo to ~$4M+/mo. *"Customers would start any offer if I made it tasty enough. But, they'd only stick if they had good reasons to. So I gave them one… every 14 days."*

## Framework / Structure

### The Bonus Matrix: When × What

**When** (timing trigger):
- **Delay** — bonus unlocks after a fixed time period (e.g., 4 months of recurring payments).
- **Milestone** — bonus unlocks on customer action / achievement (e.g., refer a friend, hit a result).

**What** (bonus type):
- **One-time** — one thing, one use, one activity, one-time access. Extends tenure by exactly one trigger event.
- **Variable** — given on a schedule, but the content changes each cycle. Renewable engagement.
- **Lifetime upgrade** — permanent high-value status change (entire feature, additional service, enduring access).

Pair any "When" with any "What" → six combinations.

### Worked Examples (in-source)

**Delayed One-Time Bonus — Local Service.**
- When: stay 4 months in a row → become "advanced member."
- What: access to annual customer-appreciation event.

**Delayed Recurring Bonus — Local Service.**
- When: 4 months → on month 5…
- What: VIP first-in-line access to in-demand timeslots.

**Milestone Bonus — Digital Product.**
- When: every friend referred.
- What: one more module unlocked.

**Recurring Bonus — Consumable Physical Product.**
- Stay recurring → new dog treat/toy/book monthly.
- Stay recurring → lifetime free bacon with every meat-box order (lose it on cancel).

**Continuous-Use Physical — Car Lease.**
- When: every 3,000 miles (milestone) or every 6 months (delay).
- What: free service.

### Choosing the Bonus Itself
Three lenses:
1. **More or better of something they already get.**
2. **Value Equation lens** (see [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Value-Equation|Hormozi — Value Equation]]) — faster, easier, risk-free.
3. **Cross-reference** the Bonuses Chapter from `$100M Offers` and Feature Downsells from `$100M Money Models` for type lists.

### Variable vs Lifetime-Upgrade Rule
Unless the bonus delivers a huge and permanent improvement, **keep it variable**. Customers acclimate to anything fixed; rotating new content (even if individually less valuable than a lifetime upgrade) keeps engagement higher across a longer tenure. Streaming services run all three — already-too-much catalog, new shows, more seasons of liked shows, better recommendation algorithms.

### Milestone Engineering
Pick milestones that either:
- Make the customer more successful (activation events), or
- Advertise the business on their behalf (public posting, referrals).
- Ideally both. Example: bonus for publicly posting "starting a weight loss challenge" — increases the customer's adherence (commitment device) and advertises the business simultaneously.

### Communication Rules
1. **Tell customers about bonuses.** Excitement requires knowledge. Mention upcoming bonuses at sign-up, mid-tenure, and right after delivering each one.
2. **Tell them the *type*, keep the *content* a surprise.** Flexibility for you, anticipation for them. Hormozi's gym owners knew they'd get a new play every 14 days; the play itself was the surprise.
3. **Bonuses are on top of what they already pay for.** Always. Adding bonus value doesn't reduce core deliverable; it stacks above.

### Combine Bonuses
Mix Whens and Whats:
- One big delayed one-time bonus to anchor early tenure.
- A permanent lifetime-upgrade after period Y.
- A milestone-triggered one-time bonus on top.
The more incentives someone has to start *and* stick, the better.

### Status & Public Celebration
When a customer unlocks a big bonus, **change their label**. From "customer" → "VIP" / "Committed" / "Lifer" / "Advanced" / "Invested" / "All-In" / "Elite." Align labels with traits of loyal customers. Bonus points if the label is something they brag about and would feel bad losing — airline-status mechanism.

**Celebrate status changes publicly.** Pair status with small ceremonies, graduations, monthly/quarterly recognitions. The more the status gets them, and the more the change is socially marked, the less they want to lose it.

## Strategic Implications

- **Stickiness is a designed asset, not a hope.** The default assumption that "good product = customers stay" is incomplete. Bonuses turn the offer into a promise-stream rather than a one-shot purchase.
- **The 14-day cadence example is load-bearing.** Hormozi delivered with no pre-built product because the *promise of recurring novelty* was the offer. The customer renewed for what was coming, not what they had. Continuity offers benefit from explicit forward-looking calendars.
- **Variable beats lifetime in most cases.** The hedonic-adaptation logic argues for variety over fixed enhancements. Streaming services demonstrate this at scale.
- **Status is a retention multiplier.** Loss aversion on status (airline tiers, club ranks) compounds the cost of leaving. Engineer it into the continuity, don't add it as marketing.
- **Milestones serve two masters.** Done well, milestones increase customer success and run free advertising for the business. Done badly, they're arbitrary hoops. The two-master test is a useful filter.

## Practical Use

To install on an existing continuity offer:

1. **Audit current bonuses.** Most continuity offers have zero post-purchase bonuses. That's the gap.
2. **Pick first bonus pair** — start with one Delay × one Variable. Cheapest to design, easiest to deliver.
3. **Tell every customer.** Communicate the bonus stream at sign-up, mid-tenure, and post-delivery.
4. **Layer in a milestone bonus** that doubles as customer-success activation.
5. **Add a status threshold** — name change + light public ceremony at a meaningful tenure mark.
6. **Add a lifetime upgrade** for very long tenure — one durable, permanent benefit that anchors the loyalty narrative.
7. **Cancellation script reminds them what they'll lose.** "If you cancel, you lose [VIP status / lifetime upgrade / discount]." Pair with discount-mechanic continuity (see [[Hormozi-Continuity-Lifetime-Discounts|Lifetime Discounts]]) for compounding stickiness.

## Notes / Limits
- Author note: chapter cut from `$100M Money Models` because Hormozi felt many businesses would struggle to fit it. Effective primarily for **continuity service businesses** — businesses without recurring revenue have nowhere to attach the bonus stream.
- The 14-day-play cadence in the Gym Launch story is aggressive and exhausting (Hormozi's own word). Adjust cadence to operational capacity; weekly/bi-weekly/monthly all work.
- Status labels must be culturally consistent with the brand. "Lifer" works for a gym; not for a B2B SaaS.
- Public celebration requires customer consent — opt-in, not surprise-broadcast.
- Bonus content must be on top of the core deliverable, not a substitute. Bonuses replacing core value is pricing erosion, not stickiness.
