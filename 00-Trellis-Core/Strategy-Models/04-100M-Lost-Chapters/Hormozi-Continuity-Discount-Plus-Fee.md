# Hormozi — Continuity: Discount + One-Time Fee

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Lost Chapters — Book Home]]

## Purpose
A continuity-stage mechanic that combines a discounted first period with a separate one-time setup/initiation fee. The discount attracts; the fee liquidates acquisition costs and locks the customer in via sunk-cost loss aversion. Used when you want both the high front-end conversion of a discount and the long stick rate of an initiation fee. Origin: a personal-training manager's ambush pitch in 2015 — Hormozi declined the partnership but kept the offer structure.

## Core Idea
You charge a discounted rate for the first term plus one or more "made-up" fees that you can waive, charge, or partially charge depending on the prospect. Two structural functions stack: the discount drives initial response (especially in well-understood services), and the up-front fee both offsets CAC and triggers sunk-cost retention. The bigger the up-front payment, the longer the customer stays.

The tanning-empire mentor framing: *"The bigger the head, the longer the tail."* Initiation fee → sign-up commitment → customers actively defend their lower rate before card expirations.

## Framework / Structure

### The Mechanic
Two pricing components combined:
1. **Discounted first period** — first month, first program, first term at a steep discount.
2. **One-time setup/initiation fee** — separate line item with a stated reason. Can be waived, charged, or partially charged.

The flexibility is the asset: the salesperson uses the fee as a lever to close. Charge it for full margin, waive it as a goodwill gift, partially charge to meet a price-sensitive prospect halfway.

### Worked Examples (in-source)

**Any recurring service.**
- Offer: 95% off first month / $1,900 off first month / first month for $100.
- Monetization: prospect pays $100 + $1,900 setup fee = $2,000 first month, then $2,000/mo recurring. Customer perceives a deal; you collect full first-month value.

**Defined-end service / 12-week program.**
- Offer: 88% off first month of a 12-week, $3,000 program.
- Monetization: $1,000/mo × 3 months stated. 88% off first month → $120 + $1,000 setup fee. Customer pays $1,120 first month, then $1,000 × 2.

### Why It Works: Two Stacked Behavioral Biases

**1. Sunk-Cost Fallacy.**
Customers who have invested capital up front continue investing time and money in the choice — even when it stops making sense. *"We've already come this far,"* *"we've already put in so much time,"* *"we might as well just finish."* The fee is the sunk cost; everything after compounds the commitment.

**2. Up-Front vs Back-Loaded Payment Risk.**
A $1,000 today + five $100 monthly payments collect reliably across the five months. $100 × 5 + $1,000 at the end has high last-payment failure rate. Front-loading capital captures cash that back-loading risks losing.

Real example: weight-loss coaching business charging $5,000 to start, $267/mo thereafter. Average client lifespan: 2+ years (vs industry average 4 months for fitness clients). The up-front fee makes leaving feel insane; if they leave and want back, they pay it again.

### Setup-vs-Recurring Disparity
Big disparities between setup and recurring fees are a feature, not a bug. The setup fee is the commitment device. The low recurring is what they "got for the commitment." Larger gap → stronger sunk-cost effect.

### Waive-as-Commitment Variant
Use the fee as a contract enforcement mechanism:
> *"You have two options: pay $100 today, then go month-to-month at $10/mo, cancel whenever — or start today for $10 and commit to the year, and we'll waive the $100 initiation fee."*

If the year-commit customer tries to cancel early: *"Absolutely. Just cover the initiation fee of $100 we waived, and we'll switch you over."* Many will finish the contract rather than pay the fee. The waived fee becomes the cancellation cost.

### Higher Setup → Lower Churn (in-source case)
John (tanning-empire mentor): $100 sign-up to drop $18/mo to $10/mo → near-zero churn on those clients. Same business, $19 down + $19/mo → measurably higher churn. *"When people pay, they pay attention."*

### When to Use
The mechanic is especially valuable when:
- The service requires customer participation (forms, info, showing up at specific times, behavior change). Without skin in the game, customers don't deliver their side, and the result fails. The fee gates participation.
- The market is well-understood (discount is meaningful) and the recurring is recurring (long tail to capture).

### Four Steps to Build a One-Time Fee
1. **Pick the fee name.** "Setup fee," "enrollment fee," "activation fee," "onboarding fee."
2. **Pick the price.** High enough to drive sunk-cost lock-in; low enough not to repel.
3. **Pick the reason.** Real, believable, tied to actual onboarding work.
4. **Decide how to deploy it.** Charge by default? Waive on commitment? Partial-waive at salesperson discretion?

## Strategic Implications

- **Combines attraction + retention in one structure.** The discount handles attraction; the fee handles retention. Most mechanics do one or the other.
- **Sunk-cost is a tool, not just a bias.** Recognized in the customer, it lengthens tenure. Recognized in oneself, it's a warning. Source explicitly cautions against the founder applying the same principle to their own bad decisions.
- **Salesperson flexibility is the real product.** The fee gives the rep something to give away. Closing prospects on the edge often hinges on "I'll waive the setup fee for you" — psychologically free, financially immaterial relative to LTGP.
- **Waiving creates goodwill more than signing up.** Hormozi's note: waiving "made-up fees" generates more goodwill than discounting the recurring. Use the fee as the friction point you can remove.
- **Front-loaded commitment captures cash that back-loading loses.** This is a structural insight independent of stickiness — even without the sunk-cost effect, paying first reduces collection risk.

## Practical Use

To install:

1. **Audit current pricing.** Where does the customer's cash arrive? If most of it is back-loaded, you have collection risk *and* leaving-is-easy risk.
2. **Carve a setup fee.** Pick name + price + reason from your real onboarding work. Don't fabricate work — find existing labor and price it.
3. **Anchor the recurring lower with the fee in.** Customer should perceive a discount when the fee exists.
4. **Train sales on three deployments.** Charge by default / waive for commitment / partial-waive for negotiations.
5. **Make leaving cost the fee.** Build the cancellation script so departing customers re-incur the fee on return — and forfeit it on cancellation.
6. **Stack with [[Hormozi-Continuity-Lifetime-Discounts|Lifetime Discounts]] and [[Hormozi-Continuity-Lifetime-Upgrades|Lifetime Upgrades]].** Sunk cost + price-protection loss + bonus-stream loss compound. The combination is stickier than any single mechanic.

## Notes / Limits
- Highly flexible — works in almost any business with a recurring or defined-end program. Less applicable to one-shot transactional businesses.
- Must be a reason for the fee that holds up under scrutiny. Fictitious fees damage trust if a customer probes. Source guidance: "even though it's made up," tie the fee to specific onboarding work and articulate it.
- Sunk-cost retention is ethically real territory. The mechanic exploits a known cognitive bias to retain customers who might otherwise leave. Use only in businesses where customer outcomes are genuinely improved by sticking.
- The $5,000 + $267/mo example is illustrative of magnitude possible; actual ratios should be calibrated to the avatar and the business.
- Author note: Hormozi did *not* take the partnership pitched to him by the personal-training manager who introduced this structure. He kept the offer; he didn't merge the team. Process insight, not partnership advice.
