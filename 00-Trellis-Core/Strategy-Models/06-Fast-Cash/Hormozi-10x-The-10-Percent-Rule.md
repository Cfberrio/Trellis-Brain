# Hormozi — 10x The 10% Rule

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Fast Cash — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Numerical principle that quantifies *why* a premium offer to a warm list pays. Justifies the 10×–50× price band used in [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Plays]] and, more broadly, any ascension or premium-tier decision against an existing customer base. Reframes "premium tier" from a niche product decision to a baseline revenue-doubling mechanism.

## Core Idea
> If 10% of your customers pay 10× the price, you double your revenue.

The rule is symmetrical and minimal: the same revenue is produced by either (a) 10 customers at $100, or (b) 1 customer at $1,000. The premium half of the equation requires *only one customer in ten*.

Three immediate consequences:
1. **Revenue doubles** when 10% pay 10× — the standard offer continues to produce its full revenue, and the premium tier adds the same amount again.
2. **Profit more than doubles**, because the customer was already acquired. Acquisition cost has been paid; the only remaining cost is delivery. On high-margin premium components, most of the additional revenue drops to net.
3. **A 1-out-of-10 conversion rate is sufficient.** The bar is not "convince everyone." The bar is "find one in ten."

Hormozi's grounding observation: **1 in 9 Americans is a millionaire**. Wealth distribution inside any reasonably-sized warm list contains buyers with the purchasing power for a 10×–50× offer. Most operators never offer them a way to spend it.

## Framework / Structure

### The arithmetic
| Configuration | Customers | Price | Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard offer (baseline) | 10 | $100 | $1,000 |
| 10x The 10% (premium tier) | 1 | $1,000 | $1,000 |
| **Combined** | **10 standard + 1 premium** | mixed | **$2,000 (2× baseline)** |

### Why the multiplier is 10×, not less
- A 2× or 3× tier gets compared against the standard offer; prospect mental model is "the bigger version of what I already have."
- 10× breaks the comparison frame. The premium tier is read as a *different category*, not a bigger size of the same thing.
- Below 10× the offer doesn't *select for* the high-purchasing-power segment — it competes with the standard offer for the same wallet.

### Why "one in ten" is the right denominator
- Within any warm customer list, the high-purchasing-power slice is structurally a minority. Hormozi pegs ~10% as a working assumption, not a guarantee.
- The cap on a [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]] is sized to this slice (5–10% of the customer base) so the play sells out instead of going long.
- Under-performance against the 10% target is operationally fine. Hormozi's stance: "Worst case — you just don't sell that many. Fine — you still make money. Who cares?"

### Extension to 2×, 3×, 4× revenue
The rule scales:
- 10% pay 10× → 2× revenue.
- 10% pay 20× → 3× revenue.
- 10% pay 30× → 4× revenue.

In *Fast Cash* specifically, the price band recommended is 10× to 50×, putting the upper-bound revenue uplift in the 2× to 6× range from the same customer base.

### How it pairs with limited spots
A premium tier *without a cap* dilutes itself: the moment many customers can buy it, the premium signal drops. **Limiting the number of buyers is what gets the attention of the big-spending group.** The 10× price selects for capability; the cap selects for urgency. Both are needed.

## Strategic Implications

- **The bottleneck is not customer willingness to spend; it is operator willingness to offer.** Most businesses cap their own revenue by only selling one fixed-price product. The wealthy minority on the list cannot spend more *because there is nothing to buy*. Removing this self-imposed ceiling is a one-decision lever.
- **Premium-tier revenue is mostly profit.** Because the customer was already acquired, the premium tier carries no incremental acquisition cost. Hormozi's documented Fast Cash benchmark: ≥90% gross margin on premium components.
- **The 10x:10% rule reframes what a "premium offer" is for.** It is not "for a different audience." It is for *the existing audience* — specifically, the slice of the existing audience that is currently capped by your own pricing.
- **Worst case is unchanged.** If the premium tier doesn't sell, the operator's standard business continues unaffected. Asymmetric upside, no downside on baseline revenue.
- **Repeated quarterly, the rule produces a quadrupled net income on a 76% revenue lift** — Hormozi's worked benchmark for the gym example. The compounding driver is margin, not volume.

## Practical Use

When evaluating whether to add a premium tier (or run a Fast Cash Play) on an existing customer base:

1. **Estimate the warm-list size** — current customers + recent customers + engaged leads.
2. **Take 5–10% of that number** as the realistic premium-tier cap. (This is the same cap rule used in [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Plays]].)
3. **Set the price at 10× the standard cart value** as a baseline; consider 20×–50× if the offer can carry meaningful unscalable value (see [[Hormozi-Unscalable-Value-Levers|Unscalable Value Levers]]).
4. **Estimate revenue uplift** as: cap × premium price. Compare against the baseline standard-offer revenue to project the multiplier.
5. **Estimate profit uplift** at ~90% margin on the premium component (Hormozi's documented benchmark for warm-list premium offers; calibrate to category if delivery is heavy).
6. **Stress-test the worst case.** If 0 premium units sell, baseline revenue is unchanged — confirm there is no downside on the standard offer.

If the math does not move the needle at 10×, the floor for action is *higher* pricing, not lower — selling the premium tier at 5× both fails to break the comparison frame and reduces the upside.

## Notes / Limits

- The "1 in 9 Americans is a millionaire" stat is contextual support, not a forecast. Use it as evidence that buyers exist on a typical list, not as a planning assumption about your specific list's distribution.
- The rule is symmetric: 10% × 10× = 2× — but real lists deviate from 10%. Treat 10% as a planning ceiling, not a guarantee. A 5% conversion at 10× still adds 50% to revenue with near-100% incremental margin.
- The rule presumes the premium tier *delivers* premium value. A 10×-priced offer with normal-tier delivery damages the operator's reputation faster than it generates revenue. Pair with [[Hormozi-Unscalable-Value-Levers|Unscalable Value Levers]] for the value side.
- Below ~10× multiplier, the premium tier is read as "the bigger size" of the standard offer. Above ~50×, fulfillment work begins to outscale margin gains. The operating band is 10×–50×.
- The rule applies to warm audiences. Selling a 10×-priced offer to a cold audience is a different problem (and a different acquisition cost structure).
