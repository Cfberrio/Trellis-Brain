# Hormozi — Fast Cash Cadence

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Fast Cash — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Defines the operating rhythm for the [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]] mechanism. A single Fast Cash Play is a one-shot tactic; this note converts it into a recurring revenue and profit lever by setting cadence (90 days), capturing the financial math (worked benchmark: ~76% revenue lift → ~quadrupled net income), and codifying the post-promo capture loop (4Rs: Reviews, Results, Referrals, Resells).

## Core Idea
> "Revenue is vanity. Profit is sanity. Cash is reality."
>
> Run Fast Cash Plays once per quarter. Weekly is too often (lever erodes). Annually is too rare (leaves money on the floor). Quarterly is the sweet spot.

The cadence rule operationalizes Hormozi's claim that Fast Cash Plays — even at ~15% of annual revenue — can be ~50% of profit, because warm-list margins approach 90%+. Repeated quarterly, the same mechanism produces compounding results: more buyers each quarter, faster sell-outs, and a structurally different profit profile from the same baseline customer base.

## Framework / Structure

### Why quarterly, not weekly or annually
Hormozi's five reasons (p. 15):

1. **Pipeline cleanup.** A great offer with urgency + scarcity converts qualified-but-on-the-fence leads who would otherwise stall indefinitely. Quarterly cadence gives those leads a forcing function 4× per year.
2. **Customer LTV uplift.** Customers who buy more often keep buying (assuming delivery quality). Each Fast Cash Play raises the LTV floor on the customers who participate.
3. **Engagement / freshness.** Quarterly plays keep the customer experience varied — there's always "something new and interesting going on." Reduces the silent-churn risk of a flat-experience customer base.
4. **Operational reset window.** 90 days is enough to deliver the previous play, audit fulfillment, and restock bonuses before the next play. Tighter cadence breaks delivery; looser cadence loses momentum.
5. **Profit dropthrough on a break-even-or-better business.** Once the baseline business covers fixed costs, every Fast Cash dollar drops to net at near-90% margin. The play converts marginal revenue into outsized profit specifically because the baseline carries the overhead.

### Worked ROI benchmark (pp. 15–16)

Hormozi's documented gym example:

| | Customers | Avg revenue/mo | Annual revenue | Net income |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Baseline (no Fast Cash)** | 166 members × $150/mo | $25,000 | $300,000 | $75,000 (25% margin) |

Add quarterly Fast Cash Plays at ~90% gross margin:

| Quarter | Buyers | Price | Cash collected | New customers added |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 | 10 | $6,000 | $60,000 | +3 |
| Q2 | 13 | $5,000 | $65,000 | +2 |
| Q3 | 8 | $6,000 | $48,000 | +1 |
| Q4 | 14 | $4,000 | $56,000 | +2 |
| **Total** | **45** | — | **$229,000** | **+8** |

Resulting profile:
- **New revenue:** $529,000/yr (76% lift over baseline).
- **New net income:** $281,000/yr (~3.75× baseline net — Hormozi rounds to "quadrupled").
- **+22 new customers over the year** through pipeline conversion.

The leverage point: the 76% revenue lift produces a quadrupled net income because the additional revenue arrives at high gross margin against an already-covered overhead.

### Why margin matters more than top-line
- Baseline business: 25% margin → $1 of revenue produces $0.25 of profit.
- Fast Cash Plays: ≥90% margin → $1 of additional revenue produces ≥$0.90 of profit (against already-covered fixed cost).
- Same dollar of revenue is **3.6× more valuable** when produced through a Fast Cash Play vs. through the baseline offer.

This is why "Fast Cash is only ~15% of revenue but ~50% of profit" arithmetic holds — the margin asymmetry, not the volume share, is the lever.

### Fallback cadence
If quarterly is too aggressive for the operator's audience, **twice a year is the documented fallback** ("if you feel you need to give it longer than twelve weeks to cool off, run it twice a year instead — it's still free money"). Hormozi's rule: cadence may be slowed, but the mechanism should not be abandoned.

### The 4Rs — post-play capture loop (p. 18)
At the end of every Fast Cash Play, after delivery, capture:

1. **Reviews** — testimonials, public reviews, written social proof.
2. **Results** — measurable outcomes the buyers achieved (numbers, before/after, milestones).
3. **Referrals** — introductions to other buyers.
4. **Resells** — re-purchase commitment for the next product / next tier / next term.

The 4Rs convert each play's buyers into the inputs for the *next* play:
- Reviews + Results → marketing assets used in subsequent campaigns.
- Referrals → expansion of the warm-list (which is the asset every Fast Cash Play depends on).
- Resells → recurring buyers who stack across multiple plays.

A play without the 4Rs collected is incomplete — the operator banked the cash but threw away the compounding inputs.

### Cool-down between plays
Between plays, **return to value-providing rhythm**: content emails, free resources, ongoing engagement. The warm-list quality is preserved by *not* monetizing constantly. Fast Cash works because the audience is otherwise treated well; converting the list to a constant-monetization channel collapses the mechanism within 1–2 plays.

## Strategic Implications

- **Cadence is the multiplier.** A single play produces a one-shot cash event. The same play, calendarized quarterly, produces a structural shift in the business's profit profile. Operators who run Fast Cash once and don't institutionalize it leave most of the value uncollected.
- **Profit, not revenue, is the metric to track.** Fast Cash plays will look small on a top-line chart and dominant on a net-income chart. Operators evaluating the program against revenue may discount it; the correct lens is contribution to net income.
- **The 4Rs make the next play cheaper.** Each cycle, the operator spends less to acquire the warm list (testimonials sell on their own; referrals come for free) and the resell rate compounds. Year 2 of quarterly Fast Cash plays is structurally easier than year 1.
- **Cool-down protects the asset.** The warm-list is the entire asset. Compressing cadence below quarterly degrades it faster than the additional cash repays. The discipline of *not* running plays between quarters is what keeps the lever working.
- **Cadence applies across business models.** B2C, B2B, recurring-subscription — the 90-day rhythm is the same. The offer body changes; the cadence does not.
- **Fast Cash sits inside a larger calendar.** Hormozi flags this playbook as one of several plays (Reactivation, Referral, "Easy Cash") that compose into a full Cash Calendar — a separate playbook he references but does not detail here. Treat Fast Cash as the first lever, not the only one.

## Practical Use

Annual cadence template for an operator running Fast Cash:

| Phase | Weeks | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| **Quarter open — prep** | Week 1 of quarter | Pick promotion. Build offer (1–3 core + 3 bonuses). Set price (10×–50×). Set cap (5–10% of customer base). Pre-write all email + text copy. |
| **Run** | Week 2 | Run the 7-day [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Promo-Sequence|Promo Sequence]]. Sell out. |
| **Deliver** | Weeks 3–10 | Deliver the offer. Honor the unscalable value. (Yes, this is most of the quarter — that is correct.) |
| **Capture** | Weeks 10–11 | Collect the **4Rs**: Reviews, Results, Referrals, Resells. |
| **Cool-down** | Weeks 11–12 | Return to value rhythm. Warm the list. Set the next quarter's date. |
| **(Repeat)** | Q+1 week 1 | Pick the next promotion. Apply lessons from the prior cycle. |

ROI tracking — measure each play on:
- **Cash collected** (top-line of the play).
- **Gross margin** (target ≥90%).
- **Profit contribution** (cash collected × gross margin).
- **Customer count delta** — new customers acquired through pipeline conversion (the play closes fence-sitters into the standard offer too).
- **4Rs captured** — qualitative, but the operator should be able to point to specific reviews, specific result stories, specific referrals, specific resells from each play.

If a play under-performs, diagnose against:
1. Was the audience truly warm? (Cooled-off leads dilute conversion.)
2. Was the price 10×–50×, or did the operator soften it to 2×–3×?
3. Was the cap visibly small enough to sell out, or so large it never became scarce?
4. Were all 3 bonuses real and dripped on schedule?
5. Was the sequence actually a 7-day window, or did it drift into a 14-day soft-launch?

## Notes / Limits

- The 76% revenue / quadrupled-net-income figure is a worked example for one business profile (small gym, ~$300k baseline, 25% margin). Direction-of-effect generalizes; magnitudes do not. Operators with different margin profiles will see different multiples — but the *principle* (high-margin plays compound on top of fixed-cost coverage) holds across categories.
- **The play does not multiply a broken business.** If baseline is loss-making, layering Fast Cash on top does not produce the documented profit profile — the high-margin cash gets absorbed by the operating loss instead of dropping to net.
- 90% gross margin assumes the unscalable value is delivered without disproportionate operational cost. Operators who fulfill the unscalable value through expensive third-party contractors will see lower realized margin. Compose the lever stack with margin in mind (see [[Hormozi-Unscalable-Value-Levers|Unscalable Value Levers]] — Status / Network / Secrets levers cost almost nothing operationally; Attention / Personalization / Access cost more).
- The 4Rs require operational discipline. Operators who run the play, bank the cash, and skip the post-delivery capture cycle lose most of the compounding benefit. Treat 4Rs collection as part of the play, not a "nice-to-have."
- Cadence must respect the audience's tolerance. List size, average customer relationship length, and category norms shape whether 4×/year is the ceiling or the comfortable steady-state. The 90-day rule is a default — operators with shorter customer relationships may need wider intervals; operators with very large lists segmented into rotations may run more frequently against different segments.
- Hormozi cross-references a forthcoming "Cash Calendar" playbook that integrates Fast Cash with Reactivation, Referral, and Easy Cash plays. That playbook is not the source of this note. When the Cash Calendar playbook is added to this vault, Fast Cash Cadence becomes one input to a broader cadence framework, not the entire calendar.
