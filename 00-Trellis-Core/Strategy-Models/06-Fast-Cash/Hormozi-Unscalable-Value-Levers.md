# Hormozi — Unscalable Value Levers

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Fast Cash — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Construction palette of 10 "unscalable" value categories used to compose ultra-premium offers — specifically the offer body of a [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]]. These are the components most operators reflexively cut for not being leveraged; in a premium tier, these *are* the value. Use this palette whenever pricing 10×–50× a standard offer or building any high-touch, capacity-capped product.

## Core Idea
> "Think of all the most valuable things you can do that are 'unscalable.'"

Premium pricing is paid for by perceived irreplaceability. The components that produce irreplaceability are, by definition, ones that cannot be mass-produced — 1-on-1 attention, customization, restricted access, status, network. A "premium" offer that is just more units of the standard product reads as a bigger size, not a different category. An offer composed of unscalable levers reads as a different thing entirely.

The trade is intentional: the operator surrenders leverage on these components in exchange for pricing power and selection of high-purchasing-power buyers.

## Framework / Structure

### The 10 levers

| # | Lever | What it provides | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Attention** | 1-on-1 access from the operator or a senior team member. *Many-on-1* (a team supporting one customer) is a stronger variant when paired with top-expert involvement. | Direct work with the founder; a team dedicated to one client. |
| 2 | **Personalization** | Customized versions of the standard solution, built around this customer's specifics. | Tailored supplements, custom-designed nutrition or content plan. |
| 3 | **Convenience** | 24/7 support, extended hours, exclusive time windows. | Off-hours-only premium training slots; weekend availability. |
| 4 | **Status** | Public or social signals of tier — perks, badges, parking, recognition. | Reserved parking, named badges, public recognition. |
| 5 | **Duration** | Longer commitment than the standard solution — 6 months to a year. | "1-Year Total Transformation" instead of monthly. |
| 6 | **Speed** | First in line; priority service; fast-response promises (≤10 min). | Priority queue, guaranteed response time. |
| 7 | **Experience** | "White Glove" service — concierge line, personal cell access, premium hospitality (dinners, drinks, gifts). | Founder's personal cell; concierge-line booking. |
| 8 | **Access** | In-person experiences, retreats, normally-restricted areas, behind-the-scenes. | Two-day luxury retreat, exclusive event, back-of-house tour. |
| 9 | **Network** | Connection with other top customers (where applicable). | Curated peer group of premium-tier buyers. |
| 10 | **Secrets** | Exclusive bonuses, trade secrets, vetted-vendor access — the "secret menu" / "black book." | Operator's preferred vendor list; off-market resources. |

These are *categories*, not a checklist. A premium offer typically composes 3–6 of them into a coherent proposition.

### Composition rule (Fast Cash specifically)
- **1 to 3 core components** drawn from this palette make up the spine.
- **3 bonuses** — also drawn from the palette — are held in reserve and dripped through the [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Promo-Sequence|promo sequence]] across days/touches to expand perceived price-to-value gap on each touch.

The palette is the same; the **role** (core vs. bonus) is determined by what justifies the price band and what creates compounding value across the campaign.

### Worked compositions (from p. 11–12)

**B2C — Fitness, $10,000:**
- Core: Tailored supplements + meals + workouts + photoshoot ("Total Transformation" Package) → **Personalization + Duration + Status**.
- Bonus 1: Year-long nutrition plan + meal-prep company introduction → **Personalization + Convenience**.
- Bonus 2: 1-on-1 workouts 3×/week with the operator → **Attention**.
- Bonus 3: 24/7 personal cell phone access → **Experience + Convenience**.

**B2B — Marketing Agency, $50,000/yr:**
- Core: Script / film / edit / post all content 4× per year ("Get Famous" Package) → **Attention + Personalization + Duration**.
- Bonus 1: Additional social platform → **Personalization (scope expansion)**.
- Bonus 2: Lead magnet creation → **Secrets (positioning asset)**.
- Bonus 3: Organic funnel + VSL → **Personalization (system asset)**.

**Recurring "Max Cash" — Subscription, Monthly Price × 12:**
- Offer: 1 Year to [ULTIMATE RESULT] (prepaid annual) → **Duration**.
- Bonus 1: Custom work on the thing they struggle with → **Personalization**.
- Bonus 2: Thing that makes it faster → **Speed**.
- Bonus 3: Thing that makes the result virtually guaranteed → **Personalization + Experience** (Hormozi explicitly notes: "That's not a guarantee" — bonus describes the structural likelihood, not a refund promise).

This third pattern — applying the palette to a *prepaid annual* of an existing subscription — is the highest-converting variant Hormozi flags ("typically ~10% say yes with little else beyond the bonuses"). It uses the palette as additional value layered on prepay, not as a separate premium tier.

### Lever × pricing relationship
- Each additional lever the offer composes adds perceived value and supports a higher price point.
- Levers stack non-linearly because they touch different prospect motivations (attention ≠ status ≠ network).
- Operators typically under-compose: most "premium" offers in market use 1–2 levers (usually Attention + Duration). Composing 4–5 is what justifies 10×–50× pricing.

## Strategic Implications

- **Unscalability is the price.** Attempts to "scale" the levers (e.g., turning 1-on-1 attention into group calls) collapse the premium signal and re-introduce comparability with the standard offer. The lever's value is exactly its inability to scale.
- **The palette is industry-agnostic.** The same 10 levers compose a fitness offer, a marketing agency offer, and a recurring subscription bonus. Operators new to premium pricing tend to assume "this doesn't apply to me" — the worked examples in *Fast Cash* span B2C, B2B, and prepaid recurring on purpose.
- **Bonuses ≠ extra hours.** A premium offer that adds "more training" raises the [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Value-Equation|Value Equation]]'s Effort & Sacrifice variable for the buyer and can perversely lower perceived value. Levers that *reduce* the buyer's effort (Convenience, Speed, done-for-you Personalization) outperform levers that *add* time.
- **Status, Network, and Secrets are under-used.** Most operators reach first for Attention and Personalization. The status / network / secrets levers — which cost almost nothing operationally — are routinely left on the table.
- **Compositional, not menu-style.** The offer is a single proposition built from levers. Listing the levers separately on a sales page reads as a feature list and weakens the wow factor. The palette is for the operator's construction logic; the buyer should see one coherent thing.
- **Operator commitment is non-negotiable.** "You. Will. Work. More. For. These. Customers." Premium pricing is honored only if the unscalable promise is delivered. An operator who under-delivers on the lever stack burns the lever's reusability for future plays.

## Practical Use

When composing an offer body for a [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]] (or any premium tier):

1. **Pick the dream outcome the buyer actually wants** in the operator's category. (See [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Value-Equation|Value Equation]] — Dream Outcome variable.)
2. **Walk the 10 levers and select 3–6 that the operator can credibly honor for a small number of buyers.** Bias toward levers that reduce the buyer's effort (Convenience, Speed, done-for-you Personalization) over levers that add buyer time.
3. **Allocate selected levers to roles:**
   - 1–3 levers → **core components** (the offer headline).
   - 3 levers → **bonuses** held in reserve for the [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Promo-Sequence|promo sequence]].
4. **Stress-test capacity.** Each lever has a fulfillment cost. The cap on the [[Hormozi-Fast-Cash-Play|Fast Cash Play]] (5–10% of the customer base) must be deliverable on the chosen lever stack. Capacity, not demand, is usually the constraint.
5. **Name the offer** with [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Naming|Hormozi-Naming]] formula — the named offer is what the buyer sees; the lever palette is internal scaffolding.
6. **Stack with [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Bonuses|Bonuses]] presentation rules** for the bonus components — name each, ascribe a defensible value, justify it.
7. **For subscription operators specifically:** consider the Recurring Max Cash variant — apply the palette as bonuses on a prepaid annual rather than building a separate premium tier.

## Notes / Limits

- Some levers are gated by category. **Network** requires a credible peer group exists; **Access** requires there's something restricted to grant access to. Don't fabricate. If a lever doesn't fit, drop it.
- **Status** levers (parking, badges, recognition) require an operating environment with foot traffic or a community to be visible. In purely-remote categories the status lever recomposes as digital signal (named tier, public credit, exclusive group).
- The Recurring Max Cash variant works best where the buyer was likely to renew anyway. Layering bonuses on "buyers who would have churned" does not save the relationship; it only accelerates inevitable cash for buyers who already trust the operator.
- "1-on-1 cell phone access" is illustrative, not prescriptive — the actual Experience-lever component is whatever the operator can credibly honor. The lever, not the literal example, is the unit of construction.
- The palette is for offer *composition*. It does not replace the [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Offer-Creation|Offer Creation Process]] — that process tells the operator how to translate problems → solutions → deliverables; the lever palette tells the operator which deliverable categories to compose.
