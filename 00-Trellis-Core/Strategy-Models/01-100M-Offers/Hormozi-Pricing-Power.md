# Hormozi — Pricing Power

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Pricing posture for a Grand Slam Offer. Defines why premium pricing is structurally required, not stylistic. Explains the commodity trap, the virtuous cycle of price, and the price-to-value discrepancy as the operative variable. Distinct from offer construction (`Offer-Creation`) and from the umbrella concept (`Grand-Slam-Offer`) — this note is specifically about **pricing posture and pricing logic**.

## Core Idea
Customers do not buy because the price is low. They buy because they perceive what they receive (value) as worth more than what they pay (price). The strategic move is therefore not "lower price" but "widen the gap between price and value by raising both — value much more than price."

> "There is no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest in the marketplace, but there is for being the most expensive." — Dan Kennedy, quoted by Hormozi.

Lowering price has a hard floor at $0. Raising value has no ceiling. Premium pricing is therefore the only pricing posture with asymmetric upside.

## Framework / Structure

### The Commodity Problem
A commodity is "a product available from many places." When products look comparable, prospects pick the cheapest one by default. Market efficiency drives margins to "just enough to keep the lights on." Bottom 10–20% of operators wash out; new entrants repeat the cycle.

Default broken pricing process (most operators):
1. Look at marketplace.
2. See competitor offerings.
3. Take the average.
4. Go slightly below to "stay competitive."
5. Provide what competitors offer plus a little more.
6. Land on "more for less."

Big secret: the competitors being copied are usually broke. Copying them imports their economics.

### Price-to-Value Discrepancy
The operative variable. Customers buy when (perceived value) − (price paid) is large and positive. The simplest way to widen the gap is to lower price — and it is usually the wrong move.

The right move:
1. Increase value (via the [[Hormozi-Value-Equation|Hormozi — Value Equation]]).
2. *Then* raise price.
3. Maintain the gap — buyer still gets a great deal ("$100,000 of value for $10,000"). Hormozi calls this "money at a discount."

### Virtuous Cycle of Price
**Lowering price** triggers a destructive cascade:
- ↓ client emotional investment (didn't cost much)
- ↓ perceived value of the service
- ↓ client results (low investment → low effort → low outcome)
- attracts the worst clients, who are never satisfied until free
- destroys margin needed to deliver, hire, retain, scale

**Raising price** triggers the reverse cascade:
- ↑ client emotional investment ("those who pay the most pay the most attention")
- ↑ perceived value
- ↑ client results (high investment → high effort → high adherence)
- attracts the best clients (cheapest to fulfill, easiest to satisfy)
- multiplies margin → enables better delivery, better hires, scale, longevity

### Higher Price Means Higher Value (literally)
Wine blind-taste experiment: same wine rated three different ways when prices visible — most expensive rated "best." Price itself is a value signal. Implication: the goal is not slightly above market — it is *so much* above that the prospect thinks "this must be entirely different." That perception creates a category of one.

### Conviction prerequisite
Premium pricing requires conviction in delivery. Hormozi: "Experience is what gives you the conviction to ask for someone's entire year's salary as payment." Without delivery confidence, premium pricing produces refunds and reputation damage. With it, the virtuous cycle compounds.

## Strategic Implications

- **Profit is oxygen.** Margin funds delivery, hiring, systems, and growth. Razor-thin pricing starves the engine that produces the value the marketing is selling.
- **Price competition is a one-way street toward $0; value competition is unbounded.** Choice of pricing posture determines the shape of the strategic frontier.
- **Premium pricing is a moral position, not just a commercial one.** Higher prices → higher client investment → higher client adherence → higher client results. Underpricing is, in this framing, a disservice to the customer.
- **Price gap, not absolute price, is what closes the sale.** A $42,000 program selling against $239,000/yr in client revenue gain is "cheap." A $99/mo program selling against an unclear outcome is "expensive."
- **Two-step sequence is fixed.** Increase value first, raise price second. Reversing the order produces resistance and refunds.
- **"Be slightly above market" is the worst position.** Either commit to category-of-one premium or accept commodity discipline. Middle is the dead zone.

## Practical Use

When setting or reviewing a price:
1. **Calculate the buyer's expected value** from the offer (revenue gain, time saved, pain removed). Express in their currency.
2. **Set price as a fraction of expected value** the buyer would consider obviously favorable (Hormozi's heuristic: deliver $100K of value for $10K). The right price is a fraction of the value, not a markup over cost.
3. **Audit pricing against the virtuous cycle.** If current price attracts the worst clients, kills margin, or feels embarrassing to charge: it is too low.
4. **Stress-test conviction.** Can the operator state the price out loud without flinching, and back it with delivery experience? If not, build delivery first or raise less aggressively until conviction catches up.
5. **Avoid "competitive" pricing reasoning.** If pricing is set by reference to competitors, the competitors are setting the ceiling. Reset to value-based reasoning.

When facing pushback ("that's too expensive"): the issue is almost always perceived value, not price. Reverse the diagnosis — strengthen the four [[Hormozi-Value-Equation|Value Equation]] variables before discounting.

## Notes / Limits

- "Charge as much as humanly possible" applies to *value-driven* offers. A premium price on a commodity offer is not pricing power; it is overpricing and produces churn.
- The wine experiment is illustrative of price-as-signal, not a license to price arbitrarily high. Delivery must support the perception.
- The 99% of businesses that "need to raise prices" claim is rhetorical, drawn from Hormozi's portfolio sample (mostly service businesses, agencies, gyms). Capital-intensive or platform-network businesses may have different pricing dynamics not addressed in this chapter.
- Premium pricing without [[Hormozi-Starving-Crowd|market fit]] still fails. Market selection is upstream of pricing posture.
- "Get them to pay so it stings" is presented as a feature for client adherence; it is not a license to extract beyond delivered value. Sting must be backed by delivery, otherwise it produces refund pressure and word-of-mouth damage.
