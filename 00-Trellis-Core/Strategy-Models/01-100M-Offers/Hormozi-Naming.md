# Hormozi — Naming

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Offer-enhancement lever that controls how the market *encounters* an offer. The wrapper around the same underlying value stack. Distinct from the offer itself: naming changes attraction and fatigue dynamics, not what is delivered. Final lever in the enhancement chain.

## Core Idea
> A Grand Slam Offer no one finds out about does not make money. Naming is the discoverability and attraction layer.

Same offer, different name → wildly different response. "Free Six-Week Stress Release Challenge" vs. "Float Tank Center Session" — same delivery, different wrapper, very different conversion.

Offers fatigue over time. **Re-naming is how an offer stays alive without being rebuilt.** This compounds especially in local markets where TAM is small and audience exposure happens fast.

## Framework / Structure

### The M-A-G-I-C formula
Five components. Use 3–5 per name; rarely all 5 (length kills punch). Order is flexible — pick what reads punchier.

| Letter | Component | Marketing concept | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| M | **Magnetic Reason Why** | Attention | Why are you running this offer / why should I respond |
| A | **Announce Avatar** | Discrimination | Who this is for (and not for) |
| G | **Give Goal** | Purpose | The dream outcome, named |
| I | **Indicate Time Interval** | Timeline | Duration to expect |
| C | **Container Word** | Method | Signals "system / bundle," not commodity |

#### M — Magnetic Reason Why
The "why now / why this." Can be literally anything as long as it is believable.
- Examples: Free, 88% off, Giveaway, Spring, Summer, Back To School, Grand Opening, New Management, Anniversary, Halloween, New Year.

#### A — Announce Avatar
Names who it is for. Specificity raises perceived likelihood ("this is for me"). For local, go sub-local.
- Examples: Bee Cave Dentists, Rolling Hills Moms, Brick & Mortar Businesses, Salon Owners, Brooklyn Busy Executives.
- Not "Chicago" — "Hinsdale." Not "Baltimore" — "Towson."

#### G — Give Goal
Names the dream outcome — single word or phrase — concrete, tangible, exciting.
- Examples: Pain Free, Celebrity Smile, 1st Place, Never Out Of Breath, Double Your Profit, Little Black Dress, 7 Figure, $100K, First Client.

#### I — Indicate Time Interval
Names duration. "$10,000 in 10 days" vs. "Make Your First Sale in 10 Days."
- Compliance note: most ad platforms reject quantified-claim + duration combinations because they imply guarantees. If a quantified outcome is involved, drop the duration in the platform-facing version. Duration with a non-quantified goal is safe.
- Examples: 4 Hour, 21 Day, 6 Week, 3 Month.

#### C — Container Word
The most important component. Signals that the offer is a system/bundle, not a commodity. Breaks comparison to single-item competitors.
- Examples: Challenge, Blueprint, Bootcamp, Intensive, Incubator, Masterclass, Program, Detox, Experience, Summit, Accelerator, Fast Track, Shortcut, Sprint, Launch, Slingshot, Catapult, System, Transformation, Mastermind, Game Plan, Deep Dive, Workshop, Comeback, Rebirth, Reset, Solution, Hack, Cheatcode, Liftoff.

### Optional craft — rhyme and alliteration
Memorability boosters. Do not force.
- Rhyme: "Six-Pack Fast Track," "5-Day Book Print Sprint," "Marriage Thrive Deep Dive," "12-Week 2-Putt Shortcut," "12-Month No-Debt Reset."
- Alliteration: "Make Money Masterclass," "Change Your Life Challenge," "Big Booty Bootcamp," "Debt Detox," "Real Estate Reset," "Life Coach Liftoff."

### Worked examples
- Wellness: "Free Six-Week Lean-By-Halloween Challenge," "88% Off 12-Week Bikini Blueprint," "Free 21-Day Mommy Makeover," "Six-Week Stress-Release Challenge"
- Doctors: "$2,000-Off Celebrity Smile Transformation," "Lakeway Moms — $1,500 Off Your Kids' Braces," "Back to School Free Braces Giveaway," "Back Sore No More! 90-Day Rapid Healing Intensive (81% off!)"
- Coaching: "5 Clients in 5 Days Blueprint," "7F Agency 12-Week Intensive," "14-Day Find Your Perfect Product Launch," "Fill Your Gym in 30 Days (Free!)"

### Offer fatigue — variation order
When response declines, change in this order, top-down. Cheap operational changes first; expensive strategic changes last.

1. **Creative** (images, video) — change first.
2. **Body copy** — change second.
3. **Headline (offer wrapper / name)** — re-name same offer (Lean Challenge → Tone Challenge; Holiday Hangover → New Year New You).
4. **Duration** of the offer.
5. **Enhancer** (free/discount component).
6. **Monetization structure / pricing / sequence** — last resort.

Lower in the list = more operationally heavy. Most fatigue is solvable with items 1–3 alone.

### Bonus and component naming
The MAGIC formula also applies to *bonuses inside the stack*. Each bonus name should follow the same structure to maximize perceived value of each component.

## Strategic Implications

- **The name is the discoverability surface area.** Strong offer + weak name = invisible business.
- **Re-naming creates evergreen lead generation in finite markets.** Local businesses with a fixed TAM use seasonal re-wraps to keep an offer alive for years.
- **Container words break commoditization.** "Membership" reads as commodity; "12-Week Bikini Blueprint" does not, even when delivery is identical.
- **Specificity outranks cleverness.** A specific avatar + specific outcome + container word outperforms a clever-but-generic line. Test specificity first; test clever variants second.
- **Naming compounds with offer construction.** The container word makes sense *because* the underlying offer is already a stacked bundle (per [[Hormozi-Offer-Creation|Offer-Creation]]). Naming a commodity offer with a container word is dishonest and gets discovered fast.
- **Offer changes are expensive; wrapper changes are cheap.** Operators who tinker with the underlying offer when only the wrapper has fatigued burn cycles and create operational drag.
- **Local businesses depend on wrapper rotation.** Smaller TAM = faster fatigue = higher rotation cadence. National operators can run the same wrapper longer.

## Practical Use

To name a new offer:
1. **List 5–10 candidates** combining 3–5 MAGIC components in different orders.
2. **Pick the punchiest** by reading aloud. Length and rhythm matter.
3. **Run 2–3 candidates in parallel ads** to identify a winner.
4. **Hold the winner as control.** Test new candidates against it over time.

To refresh a fatigued offer (do not change the underlying delivery):
1. Change creative (images/video). Wait, measure.
2. Change body copy. Wait, measure.
3. Re-name the wrapper using a new MAGIC variant.
4. Only if 1–3 fail to revive performance: change duration → enhancer → underlying structure.

For local businesses: build a rotation calendar of seasonal wrappers (4–6 weeks per wrapper). Same delivery, fresh name + creative + body copy each cycle.

For bonuses inside the stack: name each component with MAGIC. "Foolproof Bargain Grocery System" beats "shopping list" even when the underlying asset is identical.

## Notes / Limits

- Naming cannot rescue a weak offer. A great wrapper on a commodity stack wastes attention; the prospect engages, finds the inside is ordinary, and exits with reduced trust.
- MAGIC is a heuristic, not a rule. "You don't need to use all components and you don't need to use them in M-A-G-I-C order." Hormozi explicitly: 3–5 components, in whatever order reads punchier.
- Some winning names violate the formula and work anyway. "I honestly have no idea why some names win and others do not." Test, do not over-engineer.
- Compliance limits the I (Time Interval) component when paired with quantified claims. Calibrate ad-platform-facing variants separately.
- Rhyme and alliteration are nice-to-haves. Forced rhymes degrade clarity, which is the higher-priority property.
- "Once monetized, rarely change the offer" is a discipline. Wrapper rotation is not a license to re-engineer the underlying offer; doing so creates operational drag and resets learning.
