# Hormozi — Guarantees

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Offer-enhancement lever that reverses risk from buyer to seller. Operates directly on the **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** variable in the [[Hormozi-Value-Equation|Value Equation]]. The single largest sales objection is risk; the guarantee is the formal mechanism for neutralizing it.

## Core Idea
> "Reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer."

Per Hormozi citing Jason Fladlien, conversion can lift 2–4× from changing the *quality* of the guarantee alone. Operators must spend as much time engineering the guarantee as the deliverables themselves.

The structural form of any guarantee:
> **If you do not get X result in Y time period, we will Z.**

Without the "or what" (Z), the guarantee is rhetorical, not operational. Most marketers omit Z and lose the lever entirely.

## Framework / Structure

### Four guarantee types
1. **Unconditional** — money-back trial; pay first, see if you like it. Strongest pull on conversion; highest refund exposure.
2. **Conditional** — refund/outcome contingent on the buyer doing specific actions. Most creative space lives here.
3. **Anti-Guarantee** — explicit "all sales final." Used for high-leverage IP or content that cannot be unseen.
4. **Implied** — performance-based pay structures (revshare, profit-share, performance fees). Not a guarantee per se, but functions identically: no result → no payment.

### State the guarantee strongly even if weak
"Hit your guarantee hard, even if you don't have one." Bold the position; supply the reason why. A weak guarantee strongly stated outperforms a strong guarantee weakly stated.

### The math
Stronger guarantee → more sales AND (usually) more refunds. Net is almost always positive.
- Baseline: 100 sales × 5% refund = 95 net
- Guaranteed: 130 sales × 10% refund = 117 net (1.23×)

For a guarantee not to pay off, the absolute increase in refunds must equal the absolute increase in sales — almost never the case in practice.

### Type 1 — Unconditional patterns

**Variants of "what the client gets":**
A) Full refund
B) 50% refund
C) Refund of price + ad spend / ancillary costs
D) You pay for a competitor's program instead
E) Money back + an additional cash kicker (e.g., +$1,000)

**Satisfaction / "no questions asked"** — strongest, riskiest. Hormozi's weight-loss application: "If you don't feel like we gave you that level of service, I'll write you a check the day you tell me we suck." 4,000 sales → 2 takers in 3.5 years.

Best-case/worst-case close pattern: "Best case you get the result. Worst case you tell me I suck, I write you a check, and you got the program for free. The only thing guaranteed not to help you is walking out today."

Better with lower-ticket B2C; risk grows with ticket size and fulfillment cost.

### Type 2 — Conditional patterns
Hormozi enumerates ten:

1. **Outsized refund** — 2× or 3× money back, or no-strings $X payment far above price. Use with high-margin products. Jason Fladlien example: course buyers who spend $X on advertising and don't make money get their store bought for $25K. $3M extra sales generated; only 10 refunds paid out → net ≈ $2.75M.
2. **Service guarantee** — keep working free of charge until the outcome is achieved. Hormozi's personal favorite. Removes time as a risk variable; commits operator to outcome. Tie to required client actions to qualify.
3. **Modified service guarantee** — extend service for a defined period (typically 2× original duration) instead of "forever."
4. **Credit-based guarantee** — refund as credit toward any other service. Best at upsell points; preserves goodwill.
5. **Personal service guarantee** — operator works one-on-one until result. "Service guarantee on crack." Add stipulations heavily (response within 24h, must use specified products, etc.) or it becomes operationally unbounded.
6. **Hotel + airfare perks guarantee** — refund product cost + travel costs. Adds tangible value vs. flat refund. Use for in-person workshops.
7. **Wage-payment guarantee** — pay the prospect's hourly rate if they don't find the engagement valuable. Original framing; in practice almost never claimed because requires submitting a tax return for verification.
8. **Release-of-service guarantee** — let them out of the contract free. Powerful where contracts have real teeth.
9. **Delayed second payment** — don't bill again until first outcome (first sale, first 5 lbs lost, website live). Aligns operator focus on activating the client.
10. **First outcome guarantee** — pay buyer's ancillary costs (ad spend, hotel) until they hit the first outcome.

### Stacking guarantees
Stack unconditional + conditional ("30-day no-questions-asked PLUS triple-money-back if you don't make $30K in 90 days when you do A, B, C"). Conditional outcomes can also stack sequentially ("$10K by 60 days; $30K by 90 days"). Future-paces the prospect into believing the result is closer.

### Type 3 — Anti-Guarantee
"All sales are final." Use when the product is high-leverage IP that cannot be unseen (proprietary code, scripts, opening lines, processes). Two functions:
- Genuine protection against post-consumption refund abuse.
- Implied damaging admission ("this is so powerful and copyable we can't take it back") which raises perceived value.

Required: a strong "reason why" sales are final. Make the exposure visible.

Also viable for high-ticket customized work: "If you need a guarantee before jumping in, you're not the type of customer we work with."

### Type 4 — Implied (Performance / Revshare / Profit-share)
Structures where compensation depends on outcome:
- Performance: $X per sale / per show / per pound lost
- Revshare: 10% top-line / 20% profit share / 25% of revenue growth from baseline
- Profit-share: % of profit / gross profit
- Ratchets: tiered payouts (10% if over X, 20% if over Y, 30% if over Z)
- Bonuses/Triggers: payment when condition met

Most desirable structure where measurable. Pros: client-aligned, weeds out low-performers, fosters long partnerships. Cons: tracking and collection. Can be paired with a minimum ("greater of $1,000/mo or 10% of revenue") or a phased start ("$1,000/mo for first 3 months, then 100% performance after").

### Naming the guarantee
Generic ("30-day money back satisfaction guarantee") under-converts. Replace with vivid imagery.
- Bad: "30-day money-back satisfaction guarantee"
- Better: "If you wouldn't jump into shark-infested waters to keep our product after 30 days, we return every dollar"
- Memorable: "Club a Baby Seal Guarantee"

## Strategic Implications

- **Risk is the largest single objection.** Crafting the guarantee is therefore high-leverage time. Treat it as a design problem, not a default add-on.
- **Stronger guarantees attract weaker customers.** People who buy *only* because of a guarantee tend to be less invested and less likely to do the work. Tie conditional guarantees to required client actions to filter.
- **Performance models are the cleanest moral position.** Operator's incentive perfectly aligns with client's outcome. Where measurable and collectible, almost always preferable.
- **Anti-guarantees only work with a defensible reason why.** Without one, "all sales final" reads as protective rather than confident.
- **Guarantees enhance offers; they do not make them.** A great guarantee on a poor offer produces refunds and reputation damage. Build offer first; guarantee last.
- **Stacked guarantees compound.** Unconditional gets the prospect across the line; conditional locks them into doing the actions that produce results.
- **High-cost-to-fulfill services warrant conditional or anti-guarantees.** Refunding price + eating fulfillment cost compounds losses in unconditional models.

## Practical Use

When designing or revising a guarantee:
1. **Identify the client's biggest fear** — what specifically do they not want to happen if they pay?
2. **Reverse that fear into a concrete promise.** Frame as "If X doesn't happen by Y, we will Z."
3. **Pick the type:**
   - low-ticket B2C → unconditional, satisfaction-based
   - high-ticket service → conditional, with required actions
   - measurable outcome with collection mechanism → implied (performance / revshare)
   - high-leverage IP → anti-guarantee with a strong "reason why"
4. **Engineer Z** to be either matched (full refund) or asymmetric (refund + bonus, double-money-back, ancillary costs covered, free continued service).
5. **Tie qualifying actions** to conditional guarantees to filter for committed buyers and protect against entitlement refunds.
6. **Name the guarantee vividly.** Generic naming costs conversion.
7. **Run the math.** Project expected sales lift × expected refund lift. If net is positive, the guarantee earns its place.
8. **Stack** if appropriate: unconditional time-bounded refund + conditional outcome guarantee.

For under-converting offers with strong delivery: the guarantee is almost always the missing lever. Build a stronger one before touching the offer or pricing.

## Notes / Limits

- Unconditional guarantees scale poorly into high-ticket and high-fulfillment-cost contexts. Use conditional or anti-guarantees there.
- Guarantees backfire when used to mask weak delivery. The lever produces refunds rather than sales when the promise outpaces the product.
- Performance models require trackable outcomes and a defensible collection path. Without those, the model fails operationally regardless of how clean the alignment looks.
- Hormozi flags that buyers attracted *only* by guarantees tend to be lower-quality clients. Calibrate which type is used and which conditions are attached.
- The 2–4× conversion lift cited from Fladlien is illustrative of guarantee-quality elasticity, not a forecast for any specific offer.
- Some platforms (paid ads) reject quantified outcome promises tied to durations. Calibrate guarantee language separately from ad copy where compliance differs.
