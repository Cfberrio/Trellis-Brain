# Hormozi — Starving Crowd

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Market-selection lens. Sits upstream of every offer decision. Establishes that *who you sell to* outranks both offer construction and persuasion skill. A Grand Slam Offer aimed at the wrong market still fails.

## Core Idea
Demand is not created — it is channeled. The single most important commercial choice is which existing demand to attach the business to. The "starving crowd" metaphor: a hot dog vendor with bad product, bad pricing, and bad location still sells out if dropped into a crowd of starving drunks at 2am. Conversely, perfect product + perfect offer + perfect sales skill into a shrinking market still loses.

> Order of importance: **Starving Crowd (market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills.**

A "great" rating on a higher lever overpowers anything below. A "bad" rating stops the chain unless a great rating above nullifies it.

## Framework / Structure

### Four indicators of a viable market
1. **Massive Pain** — prospects do not just want the outcome, they desperately need it. Pain magnitude scales price tolerance ("the pain is the pitch").
2. **Purchasing Power** — prospects can afford the price required to make the engagement worthwhile. Resume-coaching-for-the-unemployed fails on this axis even with excellent product/offer.
3. **Easy to Target** — prospects are reachable as a definable group: associations, mailing lists, social groups, ad-platform targeting. If finding them is needles-in-a-haystack, a GSO never reaches them.
4. **Growing** — market expansion is a tailwind; contraction is a headwind. Newspapers had three of the four (pain, money, targeting) but failed because the market shrank ~25%/yr.

### Three foundational categories
Hormozi states three permanent macro-markets that always exist:
- Health
- Wealth
- Relationships

Pick a sub-segment within one of these that scores well on all four indicators.

### Three success levers (rank order)
| Rank | Lever | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Starving Crowd (market) | Greatness here can carry weak offer + weak persuasion |
| 2 | Offer Strength | Greatness here can carry weak persuasion in a normal market |
| 3 | Persuasion Skills | Last lever; required only when 1 and 2 are merely "normal" |

A "bad" market kills the equation regardless of 2 and 3.

### Niche commitment
"Don't make me niche slap you." Once a viable market is chosen, commit. Hopping niches resets all learning. Failure of one offer in a normal market does not mean the market is bad — usually the offer hasn't been found yet.

### Riches-in-niches pricing logic
Same product, narrower avatar → higher justifiable price. Hormozi's worked example:
- "Time Management" → $19
- "Time Management for Sales Professionals" → $99
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Sales Reps" → $499
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Power Tools & Gardening Sales Reps" → $1,000–$2,000

Mechanism: specificity raises the prospect's perceived likelihood of achievement ("this is for me") and lets messaging speak directly to the avatar's situation. Core content can be ~identical.

## Strategic Implications

- **Market is the first audit, not the last.** Before reworking offer or sales script, audit whether the market itself can support the goal. A doomed market consumes effort that should redirect.
- **Most operators will be in "normal" markets.** Hormozi explicitly: normal markets are fine; great markets are great; only "bad" (shrinking, no money, unreachable, low-pain) markets must be avoided.
- **Niche down until ~$10M/yr.** Below ~$10M revenue, niching narrower almost always produces more profit per customer. Above that, TAM becomes the constraint and selective broadening (up-market, down-market, adjacent) may be required.
- **Targetability is non-negotiable in a paid-acquisition world.** A market with pain, money, and growth but no clean targeting layer is operationally unworkable.
- **Pain magnitude sets price ceiling.** Connects directly to the [[Hormozi-Value-Equation|Hormozi — Value Equation]]: bigger gap between current reality and dream outcome = more justifiable premium.

## Practical Use

Pre-offer market check (run before any [[Hormozi-Offer-Creation|offer construction]]):
1. **Pain** — score 0/normal/high. Can the prospect describe the pain unprompted? If yes, normal+. If they cry/curse describing it, high.
2. **Purchasing power** — what is the prospect's annual disposable spend on this category? If you can't price to a viable unit economic, market fails on this axis.
3. **Targetability** — name the list, group, channel, or platform targeting parameter that reaches them. If you cannot name one, market fails.
4. **Growth** — is the segment growing, flat, or shrinking? A 5%+/yr decline is a red flag; >10%/yr decline is a kill signal.

If all four pass: commit to the niche, run trial offers, do not switch markets after one failed offer.

For an existing business diagnosing under-performance: run the audit *before* tweaking the offer. Lloyd-the-newspaper-software example: product, offer, and persuasion were all good; the market was 25%/yr decline. Solution was not a better offer — it was a different market.

## Notes / Limits

- The four indicators are heuristic, not measured. Pain is qualitative; purchasing power is segment-level; targetability is binary in practice; growth varies by sub-segment vs. category.
- "Niche down to make more money" has a ceiling at TAM. Hormozi flags this explicitly; practitioners under $10M usually have not hit it; over $10M sometimes confuse "I've capped" with "I can be better."
- "Don't be in a bad market" does not mean "abandon at first sign of friction." Distinguish a bad market from a normal market with a weak offer. Most operators misdiagnose the second as the first.
- Health/Wealth/Relationships taxonomy is a starting heuristic, not exhaustive. B2B segments often map cleanly onto Wealth (more revenue, lower cost, less time) but not always.
