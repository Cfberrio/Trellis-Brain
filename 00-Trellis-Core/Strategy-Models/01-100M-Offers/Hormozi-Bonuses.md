# Hormozi — Bonuses

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Offer-enhancement lever that widens the price-to-value gap by adding components rather than discounting the core. Operates on the **value** side of the equation while leaving price intact. Distinct from offer construction (`Offer-Creation` builds the core stack); this note is about how to **present** the stack and add adjunct value at the close.

## Core Idea
> A single offer is less valuable than the same offer broken into its component parts and stacked as bonuses.

The core mechanism: anchor the price to the headline offer, then layer additional value on top. Each additional bonus widens the perceived price-to-value gap until the prospect's resistance breaks. Same money charged; perceived value multiplied.

> "Add bonuses, do not discount." Discounting teaches prospects that prices are negotiable. Bonuses preserve price integrity and signal strength.

## Framework / Structure

### Core principle
Enumeration creates value. A service that "includes lots of stuff" carries less perceived value than the same service presented as a stack of named, individually-priced components. Until each piece is named, it is invisible.

### When to introduce bonuses (1-on-1 selling)
- **First** ask for the sale on the core offer.
- If yes → after they have signed up, surface the bonuses to create a wow experience and reinforce the decision.
- If no → present a bonus that matches their stated objection, then ask again. Repeat for additional objections. Reciprocity makes refusal harder with each accommodation.

### Bonus presentation checklist (per bonus)
1. Always offer them — use the bundled deliverables from the [[Hormozi-Offer-Creation|Offer-Creation]] trim & stack output.
2. Give a special name with a benefit in the title.
3. Tell the prospect:
   - how it relates to their issue,
   - what it is,
   - how you discovered or created it,
   - how it specifically improves their life — faster, easier, less effort/sacrifice (Value Equation language).
4. Provide proof — stat, past client, personal experience.
5. Paint a vivid image of the prospect's life *after* they have used it.
6. Always ascribe a price tag and justify it.
7. Prefer **tools and checklists over additional trainings** — lower effort and time for the buyer means higher Value Equation score.
8. Each bonus should crush a specific objection or concern.
9. Solve the prospect's *next* problem before they encounter it.
10. **Total bonus value should eclipse the value of the core offer.** Subconsciously signals the core must be even more valuable than the bonuses.
11. Stack scarcity and urgency on the bonuses themselves to amplify (see "Layered enhancers").

### Layered enhancers — bonus + scarcity, bonus + urgency
- Bonus + scarcity: "Only buyers of XYZ get bonuses #1, #2, #3 — never sold or available elsewhere." Or: "I have 3 tickets remaining for my $5,000 virtual event. Buy this program and get one as a bonus."
- Bonus + urgency: "If you buy today, I'll add bonus XYZ ($1,000 value) for free. I do this to reward action takers."
- Scarcity-bonuses are not time-bound. Urgency-bonuses are. Both stack with the core scarcity/urgency on the offer itself.

### Advanced — partner bonuses (other people's products)
Trade adjacent businesses' products/services into your bonus stack in exchange for exposure to your customer base. Mechanism:
- They get free distribution to your high-quality buyers.
- You get high-perceived-value bonuses at zero hard cost.
- Negotiate group discounts the partner could never get individually.
- Negotiate a referral commission to yourself per customer who acts on the partner's offer.

Hormozi pain-clinic example: massage therapist (free sessions), chiropractor (free adjustments), supplement company (discounts), local gym (free training session + month of pool), pharmacy (drug discounts). Each item adds bonus value; each can also pay an affiliate commission per referral. A $400 core offer can be backed by $1,000+ in partner bonus value plus an extra $350+ in referral revenue.

For maximum effect: design the partner bonus itself as a Grand Slam Offer using the same constructions, so the bonus is not a commoditized service.

### What should be bonus vs. core (when you fulfill it yourself)?
**Wow factor.** Items that are short, distinct, high-quality, easy to perceive in isolation belong as bonuses. Checklists, infographics, swipe files, recordings — they compress meaningful value into a small surface area, easy for prospect to perceive as a discrete win.

Items that are operationally embedded ("Tuesday call") stay as core. Items that can stand alone as a perceivable asset (a specific template, a launch-map PDF) work better surfaced as bonuses.

## Strategic Implications

- **Stacking is enumeration as value creation.** The same delivery, named into 8 named bonuses, is perceived as multiples more valuable than the same delivery described in one paragraph.
- **Bonus value > core value (perceived) is a feature, not a bug.** It triggers a useful subconscious inference: "if these are the bonuses, the main thing must be incredible."
- **Partner bonuses convert sunk acquisition cost into adjacent revenue.** Customers were going to buy adjacent services anyway; routing those buys through partnership commissions monetizes a flow the operator already paid for.
- **Discounting trains negotiation; bonuses train value expectation.** Operators who discount to close train future buyers to ask for discounts. Operators who add bonuses train future buyers to expect value-stacked offers.
- **Bonus assets compound over time.** Every recorded workshop, every webinar, every completed project becomes a future bonus inventory item. Operators with longevity should maintain a "bonus vault."
- **High-perceived-value low-operational-cost items dominate the optimal bonus mix.** Information products, recordings, templates, login access. Easy to stack endlessly; almost zero marginal cost.

## Practical Use

When converting an offer's deliverables into a bonus stack:
1. **Take the [[Hormozi-Offer-Creation|trimmed and stacked deliverable bundle]].** Each component is already targeted at a specific problem.
2. **Rename each component** with a benefit-laden title (use the [[Hormozi-Naming|naming]] formula on bonus titles too).
3. **Ascribe a defensible dollar value** to each bonus (alternative cost, time saved × hourly rate, equivalent product price).
4. **Order the presentation:** core offer first, then bonuses in ascending perceived value, ending with the bonus that addresses the prospect's most likely final objection.
5. **Run the partner-bonus pass.** List adjacent services the customer will want next. Approach each provider for an exclusive group discount and a referral commission.
6. **Build the bonus vault.** Catalog every existing asset (recordings, templates, swipe files) and tag with the objection it crushes. Pull from inventory as objections arise.
7. **In 1-on-1 sales:** ask for the sale on the core. Lead with bonuses only after a "no." Stack bonuses against specific objections, ask "fair enough?" between each.

For an under-converting offer: most often the deliverables are already strong but presented as a single block. Re-presentation as a stack of named bonuses with ascribed values often lifts conversion without changing fulfillment.

## Notes / Limits

- Bonus stacking does not save a weak core offer. The first ask is still on the core. If the core is commodity, bonuses on top read as desperation.
- Ascribed values must be defensible. "$10,000 value" without a basis the buyer can audit erodes trust. Use alternative-cost, hourly-rate, or substitute-price logic.
- Partner bonuses require ongoing relationship maintenance. Treat as a small partnership program, not a one-time negotiation.
- Bonus presentation order matters. Leading with bonuses before the core offer dilutes the anchor and weakens the price-to-value gap mechanism.
- Tools/checklists outperform additional trainings as bonuses *because* they reduce effort. Adding more "training hours" can perversely lower perceived value by raising the Effort & Sacrifice variable in the Value Equation.
