# Hormozi — Value Equation

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Quantifies what makes any offer feel valuable. Lens for diagnosing why an offer wins, loses, or feels overpriced. Foundation for every other framework in `$100M Offers` — pricing, offer construction, scarcity/urgency/bonuses/guarantees all act on one of its four variables.

## Core Idea
Value is not "what the thing costs to make." Value is the prospect's *perceived* trade between a desired outcome and the friction to reach it. Hormozi expresses it as a division equation so that reducing the bottom variables toward zero drives perceived value toward infinity. Beginners chase the top (bigger claims, louder dream). Best companies compete on the bottom (immediacy and effortlessness).

## Framework / Structure

```
            Dream Outcome  ×  Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Value  =  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
            Time Delay     ×  Effort & Sacrifice
```

Two drivers to **increase**, two to **decrease**.

### 1. Dream Outcome (increase)
The gap between current reality and the prospect's envisioned end state. Job is not to *create* desire — desire pre-exists — but to channel existing desire through your vehicle and reflect it back so the prospect feels understood.
- Multiple vehicles can satisfy the same desire (e.g., makeup, surgery, fitness all serve "be perceived as beautiful"). Most underlying desires reduce to status.
- When two offers share the same dream outcome, dream outcome cancels out — competition shifts to the other three variables.
- Frame benefits in terms of how others will perceive the prospect's achievement (status visibility), not just internal feeling.

### 2. Perceived Likelihood of Achievement (increase)
The prospect's belief that *this specific offer* will actually deliver the result for them. People pay for certainty.
- Same service, different track record → wildly different price tolerance (surgeon's 10,000th patient vs. their first).
- Increased through messaging, proof, social proof, third-party edification, what is included/excluded, and guarantees.
- Same work on your end; the lever is conviction in the buyer's head.

### 3. Time Delay (decrease)
Time between purchase and receiving the promised benefit. Has two sub-components:
- **Long-term outcome** — what they actually bought.
- **Short-term experience** — milestones along the way that reinforce the decision.
The dream is what they buy; the short-term wins are what keep them long enough to receive it. Engineer fast emotional wins close to purchase to lock in conviction.
- "Fast beats free." Speed is a defensible differentiator even against $0 alternatives (FedEx vs. USPS, Uber vs. walking, premium DMV vs. DMV).

### 4. Effort & Sacrifice (decrease)
Tangible and intangible costs accrued *along the way* — work required, lifestyle changes, social cost, willpower.
- Drives the price gap between done-for-you and do-it-yourself versions of the same outcome (liposuction vs. fitness program).
- Decreasing perceived effort and sacrifice can be more powerful than reducing actual effort.

## Strategic Implications

- **Division, not addition, is intentional.** If bottom drivers approach zero, perceived value approaches infinite — even with a modest top side. The strategic ceiling lives in the denominator.
- **Get the bottom to zero.** Top-side claims (bigger dream, louder proof) are easy and lazy and therefore less defensible. Time delay and effort are harder to compress and therefore more defensible. Apple, Amazon, Netflix won by attacking the denominator.
- **Perception is the unit, not reality.** Actual reductions in time/effort are not value until the prospect *perceives* them. The London tube example: a dotted-map showing wait time created more rider satisfaction than physically faster trains, at a fraction of the cost. Communication is part of value creation, not separate from it.
- **Logical solutions are usually exhausted.** If a logical fix worked, someone already shipped it. Most remaining gains are psychological — reframe, sequence, or surface what is already true.
- **Same outcome, different vehicles → price gap.** When dream outcome cancels, the entire pricing differential is explained by likelihood × 1/time × 1/effort. Used to explain why supplements ($123B) are 2× the size of health clubs ($62B) despite serving the same desires.

## Practical Use

Diagnostic checklist for any offer:
1. **Dream outcome** — does the copy reflect the prospect's exact envisioned end state, including status visibility to peers? If two offers share this, do not compete here.
2. **Likelihood** — what proof, guarantees, exclusions, or expert framing raise belief that *this* will work *for them*?
3. **Time delay** — what is the long-term outcome's true horizon, and what fast win can be engineered close to purchase to bridge it?
4. **Effort & sacrifice** — what can be removed, automated, done-for-them, or reframed to reduce perceived cost along the way?

Score each on a 0/1 binary (Hormozi's working method) for quick comparison between two competing offers. Whichever scores higher across the four wins on perceived value, regardless of actual price.

Used by Hormozi as the lens behind:
- pricing decisions (premium pricing requires demonstrable denominator reduction)
- offer construction (Problems → Solutions → Trim & Stack — every solution chosen targets one variable)
- guarantees, scarcity, urgency, bonuses (each operates on one of the four variables)

## Notes / Limits

- Hormozi notes the equation is hypothetical — true zero on time delay and effort is unreachable; it is the limit to strive toward, not a literal target.
- Variables are **interlocked in practice**. Most offer levers move more than one at once; isolation is for analysis, not delivery.
- Equation does not address *whether* the prospect can pay. Purchasing power is handled separately under market selection (Starving Crowd).
- The 0/1 scoring is illustrative, not a measurement system. It forces a forced-choice comparison; it does not produce a price.
