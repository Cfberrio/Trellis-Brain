# Hormozi — Scarcity

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Offer-enhancement lever that increases perceived demand without changing the core offer. Operates on the **supply** side of the supply-demand curve. Distinct from urgency (which operates on time, not quantity).

## Core Idea
> "Scarcity is a function of quantity. Urgency is a function of time."

People want what they cannot have. Restricting supply — or making restricted supply visible — raises perceived value, because perceived demand is read from perceived supply constraints. Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain; scarcity weaponizes loss aversion.

The strategic posture: **the person who needs the exchange less always has the upper hand.** A "0 fucks given" stance — not faked, but structurally true because supply is genuinely limited — produces the strongest pricing power.

## Framework / Structure

### Three types of scarcity
1. **Limited supply of seats/slots** — capped capacity, in general or over a window.
2. **Limited supply of bonuses** — the bonus pool is finite even if the core offer is not.
3. **Never available again** — one-shot release, no repeat.

### Application by business type

**Physical products** — limited releases (flavors, colors, sizes, designs).
- Critical: **always sell out.** Better to under-supply than over-supply.
- Always announce when sold out. Sold-out signal is social proof.
- Cadence sweet spot: ~once a month for repeat operators.
- Chanel example: 1–2 of each piece per store; every store has a different selection; every item is the last or second-to-last in stock.

**Services** — three operational patterns:
1. **Total Business Cap** — only ever serve N clients. Waiting list opens periodically (slots increase by 10–20% when capacity allows). Best for highest-tier service levels.
2. **Growth Rate Cap** — only N new clients per week/month, ongoing. ("We accept 5 new clients per week; 3 spots taken; you can take this one or wait until reopen.") Banks on the operational reality that capacity is finite anyway.
3. **Cohort Cap** — only N clients per class, started on a fixed cadence (monthly, quarterly). Doubles as operational structure for onboarding.

### Honest scarcity (most ethical form)
The simplest scarcity is real capacity. State current capacity ("we are 81% full this week"). Real → defensible → no integrity cost. Implies social proof: 81% full = many other prospects already chose to buy, and the closer to 100%, the faster the remaining slots disappear. The operator chooses where the line is "full."

### Scarcity stacking with bonuses
Scarcity also applies to *bonuses* without restricting the core offer's supply. Variant 1: "Only buyers of XYZ get this bonus, never sold elsewhere." Variant 2: "3 tickets remain to my $5,000 virtual event — buy this program and get one as a bonus."

### Extreme scarcity (high-leverage form)
Sell a very limited supply of 1-on-1 access (DM, email, phone, voice memo, Zoom) at very high prices. Cap the number tightly. Pick a delivery medium the operator does not hate. The "1% of 1%" will pay; these tend to be the best clients.

### Once-out-never-back
Capping a service level and stating that anyone who leaves cannot rejoin. Increases retention friction in addition to acquisition pull. Works best with small, exclusive groups; loses teeth at scale.

## Strategic Implications

- **Implied demand commands premium pricing.** Authority figures, celebrities, and specialists charge egregious rates because their supply is perceived as fixed and tiny relative to demand. Mechanism is identical at any scale.
- **Selling out is part of the strategy.** Under-supply that always sells out compounds in effectiveness over time — a rare property in marketing tactics, where most levers fatigue.
- **Scarcity makes free things desirable.** A free download capped at "20 per week" outperforms an always-available free download. Mechanism extends to lead magnets, not just paid offers.
- **Scarcity is structural, not cosmetic.** Faked scarcity that the market sees through erodes trust faster than it generates sales. Honest scarcity (real capacity) is durable.
- **Scarcity raises consumption.** Restricted-access products get used more once obtained. Compounds the perceived value loop: harder to get → more valuable → more used → better outcomes → stronger word of mouth.
- **Scarcity belongs to the offer, not the sale.** Hormozi places it at offer-construction layer, not in the sales conversation, because it changes how the market perceives the offer before any 1-on-1 contact.

## Practical Use

When adding scarcity to an existing offer:
1. **Pick the type that matches the business model** — products → limited releases; ongoing services → total cap or growth-rate cap; cohort-based delivery → cohort cap.
2. **Set the cap deliberately low.** Better to be visibly sold out than ambiguously available. Capacity number should be operationally honest and visibly believable.
3. **Make the constraint visible.** Public counter, capacity statement, sold-out announcement. Invisible scarcity does not influence perception.
4. **Never lie about scarcity.** A single discovered fake destroys the lever. Use only honest, structurally enforced caps.
5. **Layer with bonuses.** If supply on the core offer cannot be capped, cap the bonus instead (limited bonus seats, exclusive companion access).
6. **Plan the sold-out announcement.** "Closed — wait list opening on X" is part of the loop. Re-opens convert at higher rates because the prior close created social proof and pent demand.

For high-ticket upsells specifically (workshops, intensives, consulting): pair limited spots with a deliberately small cap. These compound in effectiveness over time as past sell-outs become market memory.

## Notes / Limits

- Scarcity does not rescue a weak core offer. Capping supply on a commodity offer reads as manipulation. Hormozi places scarcity *after* offer construction for this reason.
- Cadence matters. "Once a month" sweet spot for products; too frequent → lever erodes; too infrequent → market forgets.
- "Once you leave, you can never come back" loses teeth in large groups. Works in tight, identity-driven cohorts.
- Scarcity is partially context-dependent. B2B segments with formal procurement processes can rationally tolerate "we're full this quarter"; impulse-buy consumers respond more to "100 units left this week." Calibrate to category.
- Scarcity stacks with [[Hormozi-Urgency|urgency]] but is a separate lever. Confusing the two collapses both into "deadline thinking" and loses the supply-side mechanism.
