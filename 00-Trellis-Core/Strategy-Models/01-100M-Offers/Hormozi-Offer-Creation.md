# Hormozi — Offer Creation

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|100M Offers — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Operational construction method for building a Grand Slam Offer step by step. Distinct from `Grand-Slam-Offer` (which defines what a GSO is) and from `Pricing-Power` (which sets pricing posture). This note is the **how-to-build-it process**: from blank page to deliverable bundle.

## Core Idea
A Grand Slam Offer is constructed, not invented. The construction is **divergent before convergent** — generate every possible solution and delivery vehicle first, then trim to the highest value-to-cost combinations and stack them into a single irresistible bundle. Every piece kept must visibly improve at least one of the four [[Hormozi-Value-Equation|Value Equation]] variables.

> Sales-to-fulfillment continuum: every offer trades ease of sale against ease of fulfillment. Mantra: **"Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction."** Over-deliver to prove the model, then optimize the cost-to-fulfill.

## Framework / Structure

### The five-step construction sequence

#### Step 1 — Identify the Dream Outcome
The prospect's actual envisioned end state. Sell the vacation, not the plane flight. Not "membership" — "lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks." Frame as the destination experience.

#### Step 2 — List Every Problem
For each thing the prospect must do to reach the dream outcome, list every friction in insane detail. Sequence problems in the order the prospect will hit them. Each problem maps cleanly to one of the four Value Equation drivers:

| Problem framing | Maps to |
|---|---|
| "This won't be financially worth it" | Dream Outcome |
| "It won't work for me. I won't stick with it. External factors will get in the way." | Perceived Likelihood |
| "This will be too hard, confusing, I'll suck at it" | Effort & Sacrifice |
| "This takes too much time. I'm too busy. It will take too long." | Time Delay |

Hormozi's example: ~16 core problems × 2–4 sub-problems = 32–64 problems for a single weight-loss offer. The more problems surfaced, the more the offer can solve.

#### Step 3 — Convert Problems to Solutions
Mechanical step. Reverse each problem into solution-oriented language. Add "How to" + the inverse of the problem. This produces a checklist of what the offer must address. At this stage *what* the offer must solve is defined — *how* comes next.

#### Step 4 — Create Solution Delivery Vehicles ("the How")
For each solution, brainstorm every possible way to deliver it. Push divergent thinking — the goal is variety, not yet feasibility.

**Delivery cube cheat codes** (use to expand the option set):
1. **Personal attention level** — 1-on-1 / small group / one-to-many
2. **Effort expected from client** — DIY / DWY / DFY
3. **Live medium** — in-person / phone / email / text / Zoom / chat
4. **Recorded format** — audio / video / written
5. **Response speed/availability** — 24/7 / 9–5 / within 5 min / within 1 hr / within 24 hr
6. **10x / 1/10× test** — if they paid 10× the price, what would I add? If 1/10×, how would I still make them succeed?

#### Step 5 — Trim & Stack
Two sub-steps.

**5a — Trim** by cost-to-business and value-to-prospect:
- Remove **high-cost, low-value** items first.
- Remove **low-cost, low-value** items next.
- Keep **low-cost, high-value** and **high-cost, high-value** items.

To judge "high value," route each candidate through the Value Equation: does it raise dream outcome / likelihood, or lower time / effort? If yes, keep.

Bias toward "one-to-many" assets (templates, calculators, recorded systems, checklists). High one-time cost → infinite zero-marginal-cost reuse. This is why software, books, and information products carry such high margins.

**5b — Stack** the remaining components into a bundled deliverable. Each component:
- has a sexy name,
- targets a specific problem,
- carries an ascribed dollar value,
- composes into a single coherent offer.

Hormozi's worked weight-loss stack: total ascribed value $4,351 — sold for $599. Multiple problem→solution→bundle clusters (food, cooking, eating, exercise, travel, accountability, social) each treated as a named component.

The bundle does three things at once:
1. Solves *every* perceived problem (not just some).
2. Gives the operator conviction the offer is one-of-a-kind.
3. Makes side-by-side comparison with competitors impossible.

## Strategic Implications

- **Offer construction is a one-time investment.** The output lasts years. Hormozi's meal plans (built in two weeks) have been used by 4,000+ gyms over years. High-leverage thinking work; do not skip or shortcut.
- **Solve every problem, not "the main ones."** A single unaddressed perceived problem can stop a sale. The asymmetric cost: an hour to build a fix vs. lost sales for years.
- **Divergent before convergent.** Skipping the "list every possible delivery vehicle" step means the trim/stack picks from a small option set. Quality of the final bundle is bounded by the size of the candidate set.
- **Sales-to-fulfillment trade is dynamic.** Start over-delivering to generate cash flow. Once cash flow is stable, engineer cheaper delivery for the same perceived value. Do not optimize before there is demand.
- **One-to-many components carry the bundle.** Operationally, a stack of 1-on-1 commitments cannot scale. Most components should be reusable assets; reserve high-cost components for high-value moments.
- **Naming each component is part of value creation.** A "Foolproof Bargain Grocery System" carries a different perceived value than "shopping list." The container word converts a feature into a system.
- **Bundle structure is what breaks comparison.** Competitors sell single items. The GSO sells an integrated solution to the entire problem-set, which has no like-for-like comparable.

## Practical Use

Construction checklist:
1. Write the dream outcome in one specific sentence. Specificity = "20 lbs in 6 weeks," not "get healthy."
2. Sequence the prospect's journey. List every step they must take.
3. For each step, list every friction (≥ 30 problems for most categories).
4. Tag each problem with which Value Equation variable it hits.
5. Convert each problem into a "How to..." solution statement.
6. For each solution, run the delivery cube. Generate 4–8 candidate vehicles per solution.
7. Score each candidate on (a) cost to deliver, (b) perceived value, using the Value Equation.
8. Trim to keep low-cost-high-value and high-cost-high-value items.
9. Cluster the kept items into named bundles (one per major problem area).
10. Ascribe a dollar value to each component, sum, then set price as a clear fraction of summed value (per [[Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Hormozi — Pricing Power]]).

For an existing offer that under-converts: the failure is usually at step 2 (incomplete problem list) or step 4 (under-built delivery cube), not at pricing or copy. Diagnose upstream before tweaking downstream.

When sales conversations hit "what about X?" repeatedly: that X is a missing problem in the construction. Add it to the bundle (build a template, guide, or asset once) and use forever.

## Notes / Limits

- Process is method-driven, not creativity-driven. Operators who "skip ahead" because the steps feel mechanical produce thin offers. The mechanics are the value.
- Five-step output can over-deliver against margin if components are not screened by cost. The trim step is where margin discipline lives. Do not skip it.
- Bundle components must be coherent. Stacking unrelated items inflates ascribed value but reduces conviction. Each component should resolve a specific problem the prospect can name.
- Heuristic value-ascription ("$1,000 value") is illustrative for buyer perception, not accounting. Used responsibly when the buyer can audit the figure (saved time × hourly rate, alternative cost, etc.).
- The "create flow first, optimize later" mantra assumes survivable unit economics during the over-delivery phase. Operators with thin runway should compress the over-deliver phase, not skip the principle.
