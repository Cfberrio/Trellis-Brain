# Hormozi — Pricing Profit Leverage

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Pricing — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Quantifies why **price is the highest-leverage variable in the profit equation** — higher than acquisition (more customers) and higher than retention (more purchases per customer). Establishes the structural reason every pricing tactic in this book is worth doing: a 1% improvement in price drops to the bottom line at a multiple no other lever matches. Sets the operating thesis the rest of the book operationalizes.

## Core Idea
> "Profit is unnatural. You must force it into existence." — Hormozi (Guiding Principle, p. 3)

Doubling acquisition or doubling purchase frequency each produce roughly 3.5× profit. Doubling price produces 6× profit — almost twice the leverage of the other two combined. The mechanism is not magical: price increases lift revenue *and simultaneously cut variable cost as a percentage of revenue*, because cost-to-deliver and cost-to-acquire don't scale with price. This is independently confirmed by Profitwell's 512-company study: a 1% price improvement is ~2× as efficient at increasing profit as a 1% retention improvement and ~4× as efficient as a 1% acquisition improvement.

The implication: pricing is the lowest-effort, highest-return profit lever available to almost every business — yet it is the variable most operators leave fixed for years.

## Framework / Structure

### The Genie Thought Experiment
A "business genie" offers to double exactly **one** variable while holding the others constant. Three options, all of which look identically attractive on revenue:

- Option #1: 2× new customers × 1× price × 1× purchases = 2× revenue
- Option #2: 1× new customers × 1× price × 2× purchases = 2× revenue
- Option #3: 1× new customers × 2× price × 1× purchases = 2× revenue

Run on identical baseline business stats — 30 new clients/mo, 33% monthly churn, $100/mo price, 100 total clients, 20% net margin, $10K revenue, $2K profit — the *profit* answers diverge sharply.

| Lever doubled | Mechanism | New profit | Multiple |
|---|---|---|---|
| New customers (#1) | CAC halves; 200 total clients @ $100; net margin 35% | $4K → $7K (margin lift) → 3.5× | **3.5×** |
| Purchase frequency (#2) | Churn halves (33% → 16.5%); LTV doubles; 200 total clients; net margin 35% | $7K → 3.5× | **3.5×** |
| Price (#3) | Same 100 clients @ $200; cost-to-deliver and CAC unchanged; gross margin 50% → 75%; net margin 20% → 60% | $12K → 6× | **6×** |

The asymmetry is structural. Doubling acquisition saves CAC; doubling frequency saves CAC + extends LTV. But doubling price applies the entire revenue lift against an unchanged cost base — so the lift drops to the bottom line at a much higher rate.

### Profitwell 512-Company Study
Independent confirmation from Profitwell's dataset (subscription/membership companies). Hormozi reproduces three findings:

1. **Profit efficiency per 1% improvement.** A 1% price improvement increases profit ~2× more than a 1% retention improvement and ~4× more than a 1% acquisition improvement. *Lowest effort, highest return.*
2. **Pricing-test frequency correlates with profit.** Companies that test pricing more often make more profit.
3. **Pricing-test frequency correlates with growth rate.** Same companies grow faster.

The directional rank (price > retention > acquisition) holds independently of Hormozi's genie example.

### The "Just Raise It" Case (worked example of the thesis)
A mall sit-down restaurant adds a 4% credit-card processing fee on a private-equity board's instruction.

- Baseline: $1,000,000 revenue, 9% net margin → $90,000 profit.
- After 4% fee: $1,040,000 revenue, *same costs* → $130,000 profit.
- Outcome: 4% revenue lift → **44% profit lift**.

Cost: print new menus + update checkout. No operational change, no service change, no headcount change. The case is the book's working illustration that the genie math is not theoretical — it shows up at small percentages too, because thin-margin businesses convert price increases into profit increases at extreme multiples.

### Why the math works (general form)
Most operating costs of a sale (delivery cost per unit, CAC) are roughly invariant to price. So a price lift of *p%* on a business with *m%* net margin generates approximately a (*p* / *m*)% profit lift. A 1% price lift on a 10%-margin business doubles profit. A 4% price lift on a 9%-margin business adds ~44% to profit. A 10% price lift on a 10%-margin business doubles profit. This is the engine behind every play in [[Hormozi-Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays|Instant Profit Pricing Plays]].

## Strategic Implications

- **Pricing should be touched first, not last.** When operators look for profit, default instinct is "more leads" or "reduce churn." Both are valuable, but price is structurally the higher-leverage move and usually the lowest-effort one. Audit price *before* opening an acquisition channel or a retention initiative.
- **Margin is the multiplier.** The thinner the net margin, the more violently a price lift swings profit. A 5% price lift on a 50%-margin software company adds 10% to profit. The same lift on a 5%-margin restaurant adds 100%. Low-margin operators are the ones who most need to raise price and most often refuse to.
- **Same costs + higher revenue = nonlinear profit.** Acquisition and frequency carry incremental cost (more clients to deliver to, more sessions to run). Price doesn't. This is why even price tactics that produce only 3–8% revenue lift (28-day billing, processing fees, .99 endings) routinely double bottom-line profit.
- **Test cadence matters as much as price level.** Profitwell's #2 and #3 findings: the act of testing pricing more frequently is itself correlated with profit and growth. Operators who treat price as a once-a-year (or once-ever) decision leave the compounding gain on the floor.
- **"It will hurt sales" usually doesn't pencil.** When you raise price, conversion drops by less than the price lift in nine cases out of ten (per Hormozi's portfolio sample) — so revenue rises and unit costs fall simultaneously. The premise of the genie story is the inverse: holding everything else *constant* understates the case for price, because in practice raising price also reduces unit cost (fewer units to deliver).
- **The revenue-only view hides the real prize.** Operators looking at "+4% revenue" and shrugging are missing that, on typical small-business margins (7–10%), 4% revenue ≈ 40–50% profit. The book's repeated rhetorical move — restate revenue lifts as profit lifts — is the diagnostic worth internalizing.

## Practical Use

When evaluating where to invest the next unit of effort against profit:

1. **Estimate the profit multiple of a 1% lift on each lever.** Use baseline revenue, baseline net margin %, and current CAC/churn/conversion to back-of-envelope:
   - Price lift 1% → profit lift ~ (1 / margin %)
   - Retention lift 1% → profit lift ~ ½ of the price equivalent (per Profitwell)
   - Acquisition lift 1% → profit lift ~ ¼ of the price equivalent
2. **Default to price first** unless a specific reason rules it out (regulated price, contractual freeze, deliberate land-grab phase, customer-acquisition bottleneck more painful than profit).
3. **Stack low-risk price tactics** rather than betting on one big price jump. The 10 plays in [[Hormozi-Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays|Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays]] are deliberately designed to combine to 26.8%–63.8% combined revenue lift with minimal conversion hit.
4. **Set a pricing-test cadence.** Annual minimum. Quarterly preferred. Add it to the operating calendar, not the wishlist. Profitwell's data is explicit: cadence itself is a profit lever.
5. **Translate every revenue claim into a profit claim** before deciding whether a tactic is "worth it." 4% revenue ≠ 4% profit — on most operators it's 40%+.

## Notes / Limits

- The 6× profit multiple in the genie example assumes everything else stays constant. In real businesses, doubling price will move conversion and churn — usually less than proportionally, but not zero. Treat 6× as the upper bound, 3–5× as a more realistic working number for a 100% price lift.
- The "1% price = 2× retention = 4× acquisition" finding is from Profitwell's subscription-software dataset. The directional rank generalizes well; the exact multiples are approximate outside that vertical.
- The thesis is fragile to one input: the **value** behind the price. A price lift that exceeds delivered value produces refunds, reviews damage, and word-of-mouth decline that show up in next-quarter churn — so the price-leverage gain is real-time but the cost is delayed. Hormozi's defense against this is the "raise prices = raise value" rule (see [[Hormozi-Pricing-Rules|Pricing Rules]]) and the [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]] virtuous-cycle posture.
- The genie example simplifies cost structure to "advertising + delivery." Real operations have fixed overhead (rent, payroll) that doesn't scale with units; this generally *strengthens* the pricing case, because price lifts don't add overhead either.
- Capital-intensive businesses (commodity goods, regulated utilities, low-WTP categories) may have a price ceiling enforced by elasticity rather than persuasion. The leverage thesis still applies; the headroom is smaller.
