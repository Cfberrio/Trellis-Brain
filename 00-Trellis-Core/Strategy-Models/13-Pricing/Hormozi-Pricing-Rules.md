# Hormozi — Pricing Rules

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Pricing — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Operating rules that govern when to raise price, how to know price is too low or too high, how to bill, and how to handle multi-segment customers. These are the beliefs that sit underneath every tactical play in [[Hormozi-Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays|Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays]] — the "if/then" decision logic for any pricing change. Distinct from posture (`Pricing-Power`), model (`Value-Driven-Pricing-Model`), and tactics (`Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays`).

## Core Idea
Pricing decisions are not aesthetic — they are governed by a small set of operating signals. Capacity, close rate, customer segments, and billing frequency each carry diagnostic information about whether price is too low, too high, or wrong-shaped. Most operators ignore the signals because hearing "no" feels worse than leaving money on the table — even though, nine times out of ten, raising price produces more profit than the conversion drop costs.

## Framework / Structure

The book lists 13 operating rules. Grouped here for retrieval; original wording preserved where load-bearing.

### A. Why raising price wins (the dual-effect)
1. **Raising prices makes money in two ways.** More gross profit per customer **and** lower cost-to-deliver because you have fewer customers. Revenue rises and costs fall simultaneously. *Worked example:* gym tripled price from $99 → $299, lost 30% of customers (200 → 140). Revenue more than doubled (200×$99=$19.8K → 140×$299=$41.9K) while delivery costs dropped 30%. Profit moved disproportionately.

### B. What you optimize for
2. **Price for lifetime profit, not first-purchase or customer count.** If customers buy multiple times or buy multiple things, the right price is the one that "people will keep paying at," not the highest price they will buy at once. Hormozi explicitly retired the "highest price I could get them to buy at" framing.

3. **Higher prices = fewer new customers, but often more total money.** Doubling price does not halve conversions — that asymmetry is the engine every price increase exploits.

### C. Diagnostic signals (price too low)
4. **High close rate = price too low.** Closing >50% of sales consistently means there's room to raise.

5. **Full capacity = price too low.** If you've hit capacity and want more profit, raise price.

6. **Fixed capacity + sold out = raise price.** Worked example: an artist who can paint only 10 paintings/month and sells out every month should be raising prices. Same logic applies to consult-based services, fitness studio class slots, etc.

### D. The stop rule (price too high)
7. **Keep raising price until extra revenue from new sales no longer offsets lost sales.** "Hear more nos, make more money." You must be willing to delay reverting after the first "no." Going from 50% close rate to 30% close rate while doubling price is net positive — but you'll hear "no" 40% more often, which feels bad enough that operators frequently cave and revert.

8. **Pricing too high = stopped sales OR customer complaints about value-for-cost.** Measure complaint signal objectively (e.g., NPS scores), not by how the operator feels about the price.

### E. Value coupling
9. **Raising price usually means raising value.** Bigger price increase → bigger value increase. Pair every meaningful price lift with a documented value lift. (Connects to the virtuous-cycle posture in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]].)

10. **Believe in your price.** If you don't, the product needs to be better, not the price lower.

### F. Emotional discipline
11. **Hearing "no" more often does not mean making less money.** Most operators forget this. Fewer yeses + higher revenue per yes is a winning trade as long as the math works. The reason operators revert isn't the math — it's the emotional cost of more rejections.

### G. Multi-segment pricing
12. **If you have more than one type of customer, you need more than one price.** Different avatars commonly have 5–10× different WTP. Multi-tier pricing is the operationalization. Each tier must be **named after an aspirational title the customer wants for themselves**, not after internal language ("Pro", "Enterprise"). Multi-tier requires operational capacity to deliver at different levels — it is an advanced move.

### H. Billing-frequency rules
13. **Bill less frequently for lower churn.** "The more often you bill, the more often people cancel. The longer between billing cycles, the longer you have to provide value." Daily billing produces more churn than annual.

14. **Display in the smallest increment, bill in the longest.** Display "$5/day," bill $1,825/year. Display and billing cadence don't have to match.

15. **Match billing cadence to value cadence.** One-time value should be billed once; ongoing value should be billed ongoing. Mixing them produces underpriced one-time value and overpriced ongoing value (e.g., information vs. accountability).

### I. Customer surplus
16. **Bill as much as you can while keeping customers happy. Customer surplus = value − price.** The size of customer surplus drives word-of-mouth and repurchase. Hormozi's preferred way to manage it: **set a premium price** so the resulting gross profit funds a far superior product, which produces both customer surplus *and* operator net profit. (Same dynamic as virtuous cycle in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]].)

### Author note — The Lookback Window of Value
Customers compare value received against the **most recent billing cycle**, not the lifetime relationship. Worked example: a $5,000/mo client whose first month produced $60,000 in returns has been "paid for the year" *from the operator's view* but has only been validated for one billing cycle *from the client's view*. If month two produces $0, they cancel — because $60,000 is compared inside the previous billing cycle, not against the relationship.

Implication: **longer billing cycles produce less churn**, because they widen the lookback window over which the customer evaluates value. They also pre-qualify customers (higher upfront commitment) and reduce involuntary churn opportunities. Hormozi flags this as a theory, not a measured law — but it aligns with the billing-frequency-vs-churn data in [[Hormozi-Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays|Instant-Profit-Pricing-Plays]] Play #5 (annual billing).

## Strategic Implications

- **Capacity and close rate are leading indicators of underpricing.** They show up before financial reports do. Build a habit of reviewing both monthly — they trigger price-raise decisions earlier than waiting for revenue plateau.
- **The stop rule is a test, not a threshold.** "Until extra revenue stops covering lost sales" is observable only by raising price and watching what happens. The rule is permission to keep going past the first "no" — not a number.
- **Multi-tier pricing is ops-gated, not pricing-gated.** Operators frequently want multi-tier without the delivery infrastructure to support it. The result is one tier that's inconsistent in delivery and one tier that's never sold. Build delivery capacity first, then price.
- **Emotional discipline is the binding constraint.** The math on raising price is straightforward. The reason most businesses don't raise is the operator's reaction to hearing "no" more often. Treat this as a real cost — and design around it (e.g., raise price for new customers only, decoupling sales-team morale from existing-customer rate sheets).
- **The Lookback Window changes how you bill, not just how much.** If churn is high *and* customer outcomes are strong, the diagnostic isn't "increase value" — it's "lengthen the billing cycle so the customer evaluates over a longer window." This is the structural reason behind Plays #1 (28-day cycles), #5 (annual billing), and #7 (annual renewal fee).
- **Customer surplus is the brand variable.** Underpricing produces no surplus and weak retention; overpricing produces negative surplus and refunds. The premium-price-funds-superior-delivery loop is how Hormozi reconciles "charge as much as possible" with "keep customers happy."

## Practical Use

When reviewing pricing on any business:

1. **Check the diagnostics first.** Close rate >50%? Capacity full? Either signal alone is sufficient justification to raise price.
2. **Apply the stop rule deliberately.** Pre-commit to *not* reverting after the first "no." Define how many lost sales you're willing to absorb before re-evaluating (e.g., "I'll raise from $X to $Y, hold for 60 days, and only reconsider if conversion drops more than 30%").
3. **Pair the price lift with a value statement.** Document what the operator is doing differently to justify the new price. Even small value lifts (faster onboarding, added bonus, better materials) re-anchor the customer's evaluation.
4. **Audit billing cadence against churn cadence.** If churn aligns with billing dates (customers cancel close to renewal), the lookback window is the diagnosis. Lengthen the cycle.
5. **Audit segment count.** If customer interviews surface 2–3 distinct buyer types with different WTP, build multi-tier — but only if delivery can scale. Name tiers aspirationally.
6. **Separate one-time and ongoing value into separate prices.** Information once, accountability monthly. Mixed pricing is the leakage.
7. **Score complaints with NPS, not gut feel.** "Customers complaining about price" is signal only when measured. Vocal-minority complaints are not the same as NPS-scale erosion.

## Notes / Limits

- The "9 out of 10 times raising price makes more profit" is a portfolio observation from Hormozi's holdings (services, gyms, agencies, education). Highly elastic categories (commodity goods, regulated services) may sit in the 1-out-of-10 case.
- The Lookback Window is explicitly Hormozi's theory, not a measured law. Treat it as a strong working hypothesis when designing billing cadence; do not over-claim causation.
- Multi-tier pricing without delivery capacity is worse than one tier — it produces unfulfillable expectations on the high tier and brand confusion on the low tier. Capacity-first.
- "Believe in your price" is psychological, not strategic — but it operationally matters. Sales teams transmit conviction (or lack of it) to buyers. Operators who raise price without conviction often see conversion drop more than the price lift, because the sales delivery itself degraded.
- These rules assume value-based pricing as the underlying model (see [[Hormozi-Value-Driven-Pricing-Model|Value-Driven Pricing Model]]). Cost-plus operators should reset the model before applying these rules — capacity-and-close-rate signals are diagnostic only against a value-priced baseline.
