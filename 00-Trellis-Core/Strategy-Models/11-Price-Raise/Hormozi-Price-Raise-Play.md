# Hormozi — Price Raise Play

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Price Raise — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Umbrella concept of the *Price Raise* playbook. Defines the price-raise mechanism applied to **existing customers** — when to do it, why it works, the operational rollout sequence, the single-jump vs. stair-step choice, and how to handle incoming customers during a raise. Sets the structural context the Rules, Price-Test-Math, and RAISE-Letter notes operationalize. Distinct from [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]] — that note is the principle (premium pricing posture for a *new* offer); this note is the rollout mechanism (raising price on a *base of existing customers*).

## Core Idea
> "The easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff."

Price-raise is not a marketing tactic. It is a structural profit lever applied on the existing customer base, executed once per year minimum, gated by a quantitative test on new customers, communicated through a five-section letter that softens the news with a vanishing loyalty discount, and protected by a fixed set of operating rules.

The mechanism inverts the operator's intuition: most owners fear losing customers and therefore lose all of them (slow margin compression until the business fails). The play is to raise *before* disaster forces it.

> "If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by a tenth of a cent, then you've got a terrible business." — Warren Buffett, quoted by Hormozi.

## Framework / Structure

### Why it works — the cycle, operationally extended
The principle (premium pricing creates a virtuous cycle, low pricing creates a vicious cycle) is canonical in [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Pricing-Power|Pricing Power]]. Price Raise extends the cycle with operational variables that show up specifically when prices change on an *existing* customer base, not when a new offer is launched.

**Vicious cycle — when prices DECREASE (or stagnate while costs rise):**
- Clients' emotional investment **decreases**.
- Perceived value of the service **decreases**.
- Results **decrease** (less invested clients try less, get less).
- Client demandingness **increases** (cheapest customers ask for the most).
- Service level **decreases** because the operator has less margin to spend on them.
- Profit per customer **decreases**.
- Operator's perceived self-value **decreases**.
- Operator's conviction in the sales process **decreases** (team can no longer guarantee what they sell).
- Operator's gratitude for customers **decreases** ("giving away the farm" feeling).

**Virtuous cycle — when prices INCREASE:**
- Emotional investment **increases**.
- Perceived value **increases**.
- Results **increase**.
- Demandingness **decreases** (highest-paying customers are the most easygoing).
- Service level **increases** (operator has margin to invest in them).
- Profit per customer **increases**.
- Operator self-value, conviction, and gratitude all **increase**.

The cycle direction is the substantive output of the play. A correctly run raise compounds; a failure to raise compounds in the other direction.

### When to raise
- **At least once per year.** Rule: see [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Price Raise Rules]]. Buffett raised See's Candies ~10% per year for 50+ years (some years up to 17%). If price was the *only* variable he reserved the right to control, that's a strong tell about its leverage.
- **Inside 90 days of a launch** if possible. Frame as "early adopter pricing" so the raise drafts off the launch's momentum.
- **Before disaster forces it.** The Trey-the-gym narrative is the cautionary baseline: a $49/mo gym losing $2,000/mo went to triple price, lost some customers, and ended at $4,000/mo profit in one week. The play works in distress — but distress is the *worst* time to learn the mechanism.

### Rollout sequence
The full operator sequence (synthesized from the body chapters and the p. 23 checklist):

1. **Decide on the new price.** Use [[Hormozi-Price-Test-Math|Price Test Math]] to identify the price point that maximizes total return per 100 clicks (or per cohort), not the highest price the market tolerates.
2. **Test on new customers first.** Cold buyers see the new price with no context. If conversion + churn behave acceptably under the new price, the math has been proven on real money before the existing base ever sees the change.
3. **Segment "old" customers** that came in before the raise. The letter only goes to this cohort. New customers are already paying the new price.
4. **Write the RAISE letter.** Five sections — see [[Hormozi-RAISE-Letter|RAISE Letter]]. Personalize Section #1 (Remind) with concrete usage data. Pre-decide the loyalty discount window (3–6 months) and PS statement.
5. **Send. Don't delay.** Default channel is email/letter; for community-led businesses, post a video version into the community with comments off and route all questions to a 1-on-1 channel via the PS line.
6. **Handle the three response types** (defined inside [[Hormozi-RAISE-Letter|RAISE Letter]]): see-the-value buyers, materially-affected buyers (extend their discount), and "going-to-cancel-anyway" buyers (you pulled forward churn).
7. **Watch for the churn pattern.** Expect a churn spike in month 1, a dip in month 2 (typically below baseline), return to baseline by month 3. This pattern indicates "pulled-forward" churn, not net loss — the cancellers were leaving anyway.
8. **Meet 1-on-1 if the raise is >50%.** Rule: see [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Price Raise Rules]].
9. **Set a calendar reminder for the next raise.** Default cadence: annual.

### Single-jump vs. stair-step rollout
Two operator choices when the new price is materially higher than the old price:

| Option | When | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Single jump** | Operator is unprofitable; needs the math to flip immediately | Raise to the full new price now. The loyalty discount handles the softening. Trey's case. |
| **Stair-step** | Raise is large but operator has runway and wants to ease customers in | Position the discount as a **disappearing** discount that falls off in scheduled increments. Example: "$200/mo off for 4 months, then $100/mo off, then $50/mo off for 3 months, then full price." Customers experience three small bumps instead of one large one. People tolerate vanishing discounts better than rising prices. |

The stair-step version is technically the same final price; it is a softening packaging of the same raise.

### Incoming customers during a raise
Get new customers to the new price **immediately**. There is no rollout for new customers — they have no prior reference price. If a new prospect protests because a friend told them the old price, the sales team's script:

> "That may feel like bad news but it's actually good news. First — we just keep making the value better. Second — to give you confidence, [X]% of our existing customers agreed; they should know, they're already using it. Third — the good news is, this is the cheapest it'll ever be. So there will never be a better time to get started."

The script reframes the prospect's complaint into three pieces of social proof.

### The 5–10x leverage observation
Hormozi cites a Shopify-competitor case: $30/mo software where the largest customers were *unprofitable* because fulfillment costs scaled with their volume. The CEO called all 300 affected customers and raised them from $30 to $3,000 (100×). They lost **one** customer.

The lesson: the elasticity assumption ("if I raise X%, I'll lose Y%") is usually wrong on the downside. When value is real, raises substantially larger than operators expect tolerate cleanly. The audited expectation is "raise causes proportional loss"; the empirical reality is "raise often causes minimal loss when value is intact."

## Strategic Implications

- **Price is the highest-leverage profit lever the operator controls.** Most variables (CAC, COGS, conversion rate) require capital, time, or skill to move. Price moves with a letter. The same business with a raised price often triples profit because operating costs are largely fixed.
- **Inaction is a price decrease.** Costs rise with inflation. A flat nominal price is a falling real price every year. The "I haven't raised prices" operator has been silently *decreasing* prices for years and accepting the vicious cycle as a result.
- **Raise on the base, not just on new customers.** New-customer pricing alone does not solve the existing-customer-margin problem. The play's distinguishing feature is its mechanism for raising on the *installed base* without losing it — and that mechanism is the RAISE letter, not the raw price decision.
- **Don't lock in pricing structures you can't escape.** The most expensive mistakes happen when operators sell "lifetime access for one payment" or "grandfather forever." Both transfer the entire upside of future raises away from the operator. Rule layer: see [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Price Raise Rules]].
- **The raise rebalances the customer mix.** The customers who churn after a raise are typically the lowest-value, highest-demandingness customers. The raise is a self-administered customer-quality filter. The cycle's effects (less demandingness, more service capacity) compound on top of the price math.
- **Pulled-forward churn ≠ net churn.** A month-1 spike that reverses by month 3 is not a loss event. It is the operator surfacing existing leaving-intent on their own schedule rather than waiting for the customer to do it on theirs.
- **Reinvestment is not optional.** Hormozi's framing of "raise prices" is paired with "reinvest the extra margin into product, advertising, talent." A raise that goes to the operator's profit alone risks the customer-side of the cycle (results, service level) regressing — which then reactivates the vicious cycle from a higher price point.

### Why-you-should-actually-do-this — the upside/downside lists
The closing chapter (pp. 21–22) is a forced choice:

When pricing is optimized correctly:
- Attract better customers.
- Make more profit per customer.
- Reinvest extra profit into a better customer experience.
- Reinvest into more advertising to grow.
- Unlock more expensive acquisition channels mispriced competitors can't afford.
- Feel good about what you sell.
- Keep the best talent (you can pay them).

When pricing is wrong (or never raised):
- Bring in the wrong customers.
- Watch profits slowly erode.
- Stop being able to reinvest.
- Keep less every month.
- Feel worse about what you sell.
- Lose the best talent to competitors who can pay more.

These are not motivational bullets. They are the cycle's downstream effects on the *operator's life and business*, not just the P&L.

## Practical Use

Operator checklist when running a price raise (synthesized from the playbook's tear-sheet on p. 23):

1. **Decide on the price increase.** Use [[Hormozi-Price-Test-Math|Price Test Math]] to identify the profit-maximizing point.
2. **Test with new customers first.** If they buy and stay at rates that increase total return, continue. If they don't, the math hasn't proved out — re-test or pick a different price.
3. **Segment out "old" customers** that came in before the raise. The letter goes to them only.
4. **Write 1–5 bullets of current value** for Section #1 of the letter. Personalize as much as possible — calls attended, deliverables used, revenue generated, profit gained, etc.
5. **Tell them the price raise happens now.** Section #2. One sentence. Don't bury it.
6. **Write 3 bullets of the biggest investments** the new revenue will fund. Section #3. These must be things you were already going to do — never invent expenses to justify the raise.
7. **Explain how each investment benefits the customer.** Pair every "more good thing" / "less bad thing" with the specific upside.
8. **Give an expiring loyalty discount.** Section #4. 3–6 month window. Vanishing-discount mechanic, not a permanent discount.
9. **Add the PS statement.** Section #5. Tells customers to write personally if the raise materially affects them. Generates the optionality to extend the discount on a case-by-case basis.
10. **Sign in ink** if mailed. If email, sign personally — not "the team."
11. **Meet 1-on-1** if the raise is ≥50% (and if call volume is manageable). See [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Rules]].
12. **Use stair-step** if the raise is large enough to need easing — drop the discount in increments rather than at one date.
13. **Watch the churn pattern** (spike → dip → return to baseline) over months 1–3 to confirm the loss is pulled-forward, not net.
14. **Set the next raise date.** Default cadence: annual.
