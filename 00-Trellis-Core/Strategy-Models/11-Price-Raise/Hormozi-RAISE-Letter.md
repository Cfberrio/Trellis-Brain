# Hormozi — RAISE Letter

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Price Raise — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Five-section letter framework used to communicate a price raise to existing customers. The letter is the operational mechanism that allows a base of customers, accustomed to the old price, to absorb a new price without mass churn. Acronym: **R**emind, **A**ddress, **I**nvest, **S**often, **E**xplain. Distinct from the [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Play|Price Raise Play]] (the umbrella mechanism) and [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Rules]] (the operating constraints) — this note is the *communication* mechanism that delivers the raise.

## Core Idea
A price raise is two events the customer experiences simultaneously: (1) a price change, and (2) a relationship change with the operator. The letter exists to manage both. The five-section structure works because each section addresses a specific objection in a specific order:

1. **Remind** — counters "what have you done for me lately?" with concrete value already delivered.
2. **Address** — counters "they're being shady" by stating the raise directly in one sentence, no burying.
3. **Invest** — counters "this is greed" by showing exactly where the new revenue goes (and how it benefits the customer).
4. **Soften** — counters "this is sudden" with a vanishing loyalty discount that reframes the news as a gift now and a price change later.
5. **Explain** — counters "I have no recourse" with a personal PS-statement door, surfacing the small subset of customers materially affected so they can be handled individually.

The structure is co-developed with Patrick Campbell of Profitwell, who oversaw 100+ price increases directly and analyzed 500+. The original Hormozi gym-letter version is longer and less concise; the RAISE structure is the refined, validated form.

## Framework / Structure

### Section #1 — R: Remind them of the value you already provided
**Purpose.** Anchor the customer in the value they've already received before introducing any change.

**Content.** Specific, personalized value bullets. The more personalized, the better — calls attended, deliverables used, revenue generated, profit gained, weight lost, etc.

**Tone.** "Look at what we've already done together."

**Sample shape:**
> Over the past 12 months, we've added so much value to [PRODUCT/SERVICE THEY USE]:
> - We've made you X.
> - Feature A helped you do Y.
> - Feature B is great and you use it daily.
> - Etc.

**Bottom line:** make it about them.

### Section #2 — A: Address the price change directly
**Purpose.** Rip off the bandaid. Don't bury the news. Don't wrap it in qualifiers. The remaining sections soften the blow; this section delivers it.

**Content.** Usually a single sentence.

**Sample shape:**
> For us to continue to invest in making [THE PRODUCT THEY USE] for you and the [YOUR COMPANY NAME] team, we need to increase our prices.

**Bottom line:** tell them the news.

### Section #3 — I: Invest in their future
**Purpose.** Reframe the raise as customer-facing reinvestment. Show that the extra revenue funds specific upgrades to the product or service they use. Counters "they're being greedy."

**Content.** 3 bullets. Each bullet is a specific investment paired with a specific customer-facing benefit.

**Investment categories that work:**
- Hiring better people.
- Training the existing team.
- Better equipment.
- Better technology / software.
- Facility upgrades.
- Higher level of service.

**Pairing rule.** Every investment maps to either:
- **More good stuff:** more aligned with their desired outcome, faster, easier, risk-free.
- **Less bad stuff:** less misalignment, slower, harder, riskier — *removed*.

**Sample pairings:**
- Hiring & retaining great people → so we can build out [feature everyone has been asking for] / deliver a higher level of service / faster response times / extended hours.
- Training the existing team → so we can deliver a higher level of service.
- New equipment → so you can use this rather than paying separately / get better widgets faster.
- Technology / software → faster, more accurate experience.
- Facility improvements → elevated experience.

**Three constraints (do not violate):**
1. **Don't say things you're not going to do.** That's lying.
2. **Frame all investments as value for them.** Not "we need a new server" — "your experience will be faster and more reliable."
3. **Don't add expenses you didn't already plan to incur.** Inventing expenses to justify the raise eats the margin gain the raise was supposed to deliver.

**Bottom line:** tell them how you'll invest the money. Show how each investment improves their bang-for-buck. Don't lie. Don't burn the new margin on new costs.

### Section #4 — S: Soften the news with a loyalty reward
**Purpose.** Reframe the raise from a price increase into a gift. People tolerate a vanishing discount better than a rising price — and tolerate later discounts better than they tolerate this one ending.

**Mechanism.** Raise the price now. Simultaneously offer a discount that **expires** in 3–6 months. Bonus points if you can show the credit on the customer's invoice each cycle — it makes the discount feel like money being given to them now, rather than a price hike being delayed.

**Sample shape:**
> You've been insanely loyal to [YOUR COMPANY NAME], using us for the past X months/years. So as of today we're raising prices on new customers, but since you've been so loyal, we're going to keep you on your existing plan for the next [3 to 6 months]. We're giving you a [$XXXX credit] as a way of saying thank you, after which we'll bump you up to the new price of $XXX.

**Stair-step variant** (for large raises). Drop the discount in increments instead of a single date:
> We'll give you $200/mo off for the first 4 months, then $100/mo off for the next 3, then $50/mo off for the last 3.

This gives customers 3 small bumps instead of 1 large one. The net price is identical; the perceived friction is much lower.

**Bottom line:** they're special. New customers pay full price today. Existing customers get a discount today and a price raise in the future.

### Section #5 — E: Explain away their concerns
**Purpose.** Surface the subset of customers materially affected, so they can be handled individually rather than in a public comment thread. Also operates as the trust signal for the rest of the base ("the operator cares enough to take direct replies").

**Mechanism.** A PS statement that opens a one-on-one channel for genuinely-affected customers.

**Sample shape:**
> Thank you for letting us be a part of [COMPANY]'s mission. If you have any questions at all, let me know — all replies go directly to me.
>
> PS — B2C: if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries, let me know and we'll work something out.
> PS — B2B: if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll work something out.

This is **not scalable** by design. The operator must be ready to roll up sleeves and handle individual replies. But: not as many customers will reply negatively as the operator fears.

### The three customer-response types
After the letter goes out, the response distribution falls into three buckets. Recognizing them is the operator's response strategy:

| Type | Behavior | Operator response |
|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 — Sees the value** | May not like it; understands it; complies | No special action. Continue delivering value. |
| **Type 2 — Materially affected** | Writes a sob story; genuine financial impact | "Don't worry, we got you. Why don't we extend your discount another 6 months, then reach out then. Is that fair?" — Section #4's mechanic stretches further on a 1-on-1 basis. |
| **Type 3 — Was going to cancel anyway** | Cancels at the raise; would have cancelled within a month or two regardless | Accept. This is **pulled-forward churn**. Pattern: spike in month 1, dip in month 2 (often below baseline), return to baseline in month 3. The raise didn't cost you these customers — you just learned about it sooner. |

The three-type model is critical for reading post-raise data correctly. A churn spike that fits the pulled-forward pattern is a feature of the play, not a failure of it.

## Strategic Implications

- **Order matters.** The five sections are ordered by the customer's emotional sequence: anchor → news → reframe → softening → recourse. Reordering them (e.g., leading with the price change before reminding of value) makes the letter substantially worse — the news lands before the relationship has been re-established.
- **The Soften section is the riskiest to skip.** Operators sometimes treat "I'll just announce the raise" as enough. Without the vanishing discount mechanic, the raise lands as pure cost — no offsetting "gift" experience, no temporal buffer, no perceived loyalty recognition. Churn spikes harder.
- **The PS statement is asymmetric.** It costs the operator's time on a small fraction of customers and earns trust from the entire base that reads it. The PS line is read by everyone; the personal-reply offer is taken by very few.
- **The letter does not work alone — it works because Section 1 was already true.** If the operator can't fill Section 1 with concrete delivered value, the rest of the letter falls flat. The letter is a communication mechanism on top of a real value relationship. It cannot manufacture one.
- **Gym-letter case (the long version).** Hormozi's original gym letter (pp. 17–20) covers the same five tenets, but at much greater length and with more reminiscing. It still worked for hundreds of gyms. The lesson: **getting the structure mostly right beats waiting to perfect it.** Bold imperfect execution wins. The shorter RAISE structure is the refined version.

## Practical Use

### Verbatim template (from Appendix, pp. 24–25)

```
Dear [NAME],

Over the past 12 months, we've added so much value to [THE PRODUCT/SERVICE YOU USE]:
- We've made you X.
- Feature A helped you do Y.
- Feature B is great and you use it daily.
- Etc.

For us to continue to invest in making [THE PRODUCT YOU USE] for you and the
[CUSTOMER'S COMPANY NAME] team, we need to increase our prices.

*** Optional: if you have strong things you can add that clearly benefit them. ***

We plan to invest the extra revenue to make your experience even more valuable
in the following ways:
- Thing one — more good stuff you like (faster, higher quality, more reliable)
- Thing two — less bad stuff you hate (slower, lower quality, less reliable)
- Thing three — more good stuff you like

*** End of optional section ***

You've been insanely loyal to [YOUR COMPANY NAME], using us for the past
X months/years. So as of today we're raising prices on new customers, but
since you've been so loyal, we're going to keep you on your existing plan for
the next [PICK: 3 TO 6 MONTHS]. We're giving you a [$XXXX] credit as a way
of saying thank you (after which we'll bump you up to the new price of $XXX).

Thank you for letting us be a part of [COMPANY]'s mission. If you have any
questions at all, let me know. All replies go directly to me.

[YOUR NAME, SIGN IN INK IF PHYSICAL MAIL]

PS — B2C: if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries,
let me know and we'll work something out.
PS — B2B: if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll
work something out.
```

### Operator pre-send checklist
- [ ] Section #1 personalized to this customer (or at minimum to this segment)?
- [ ] Section #2 — the price-change sentence — is in the letter and not buried?
- [ ] Section #3 has 3 investment bullets, each paired with a customer-facing benefit?
- [ ] Section #3 investments are real (already-planned) and don't add new costs that eat the raise margin?
- [ ] Section #4 discount has an expiration date 3–6 months out (or stair-step schedule for large raises)?
- [ ] Section #5 PS statement is included and the operator is ready to handle direct replies?
- [ ] Letter is signed personally (not "the team")?
- [ ] If raise ≥50%, 1-on-1 calls are scheduled for affected customers (per [[Hormozi-Price-Raise-Rules|Rule 7]])?
- [ ] If the customer base lives in a community / group, a video version is prepared with comments off?

### Channel notes
- **Email/letter** is the default channel.
- **Physical mail** with hand-signed signature is higher-trust for high-LTV B2B accounts.
- **Community video** (with comments off) is the right channel for membership-style businesses where the customer base congregates in a group. Route all questions to a 1-on-1 channel via the PS statement to prevent a public comment-thread bitchfest.

## Notes / Limits

- **Don't fabricate Section #1 bullets.** If the operator can't list real value already delivered, the letter exposes the lack of it. Fix the value problem first; raise prices second.
- **Don't promise Section #3 investments you won't make.** Customers will notice. Trust collapses faster than a price raise can rebuild it.
- **The $XXXX credit framing matters.** Showing the credit on the invoice each cycle ("Loyalty Credit: -$50.00") makes the discount feel like money being given. Bundling it silently into the line-item price loses the framing benefit.
- **The longer Hormozi gym-letter (pp. 17–20)** is included in the source for completeness — it's a real letter Trey-style gyms used and it worked. It is *not* the recommended template; the shorter RAISE-structured version is. Use the long version only when extensive context is genuinely needed (e.g., introducing a fundamental service-model change alongside the price raise, like the gym letter's switch from drop-in to scheduled appointments).
- **Stair-step is optional, not default.** Use it when the raise is large enough that a single-date jump would shock the base. For 10–20% annual raises, a single-date jump with a 3–6 month discount is the cleaner play.
