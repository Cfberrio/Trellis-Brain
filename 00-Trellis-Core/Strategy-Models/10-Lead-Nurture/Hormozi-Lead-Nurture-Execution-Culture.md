# Hormozi — Lead Nurture Execution Culture

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Lead Nurture — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The implicit fifth pillar of the Lead Nurture playbook. Defines the **measurement system** (three-metric ranking by sales rep), the **reward system** (monetary + non-monetary), and the **behavioral norms** (-isms) that hold the four pillars in place over time. Without this layer, the four pillars degrade within weeks because the boring work of working leads is the first thing reps drop under pressure.

## Core Idea
> "Do the boring work."
>
> Culture = the rules that govern good and bad behavior in an organization. A lead-nurture culture **accepts** behaviors that make the team available, fast, personal, and persistent — and **rejects** behaviors that make it unavailable, slow, boring, and fragile. The four pillars are the *what*; this is the *how it stays in place*.

The chapter's narrative arc (Jacob, the cold-call hire who became the top setter then top closer) is illustrative — the extracted mechanism is the metrics + rewards + -isms triad below.

## Framework / Structure

### The three-metric ranking by sales rep
Track and rank every sales rep on **all three** simultaneously. Rep performance reviewed weekly:

| Metric | What it measures | Failure mode if measured alone |
|---|---|---|
| **Show rate** | Work ethic — does the rep nurture leads to actually arrive? | Hidden if you only track close rate. |
| **Close rate** | Skill — can the rep convert a shown lead? | Hides reps who only take cherry-picked leads. |
| **Lead-to-close (total call-to-close %)** | Joint output — leads handed to this rep that became sales. | The actual scoreboard. |

If the team is healthy, the same reps appear at the top of all three lists. If the lists scatter, the team has skill/work-ethic asymmetry that requires intervention.

The book's framing: most operators obsess over close rate. **Close rate alone is wrong**. The number that matters is the conversion rate on the leads you actually have — which requires all three.

**Setter → closer pipeline tip:** the best closers are typically the best setters. New hires should ramp on setting (low-stakes, high-rep volume); top setters should be promoted into closing.

### The reward system

**Monetary:** add a **commission bump for show-up rates** — typically **5–30% of what a salesman makes on a sale**, scaled to ticket size. This compensates the work of nurturing the lead to arrival, separately from the work of closing them.

**Non-monetary** — the three As: **attention, affection, approval.**
- **Public kudos** to reps with the highest show rates.
- **Ask them what they did.** When they say "I just followed the process" — give *more* approval. This rewards the right behavior loop.
- **Team -isms** reinforce the right behaviors socially.

The book's parallel mechanism: **publicly disapprove** of reps who post a high close rate but skip the lead-nurture work. Frame as "entitled and wasteful" within the team's language.

### The -isms (cultural shorthand)
Short phrases that embed the right behaviors into team vocabulary. Each one rejects a specific failure mode:

| -ism | What it rewards | Failure mode it rejects |
|---|---|---|
| **"Volume negates luck"** | Doing more touches than feels comfortable. | Quitting after 1–2 attempts. |
| **"Speed is king"** | <5-min response. | Calendar-driven (not lead-driven) work pace. |
| **"Yellows are the new gold"** | Working medium-quality leads as practice volume. | Cherry-picking only top-tier leads. |
| **"Be a garbage man"** | Taking under-qualified leads as free practice. | Judging leads by appearance / form-fill quality. |
| **"BAMFAM as a way of life"** | Booking the next meeting before this one ends. | "I'll follow up with times." |
| **"Do the boring work"** | Tedious lead-working as the actual job. | Treating lead-working as beneath the rep's skill level. |
| **"Process > all"** | Adherence over individual heroics. | Star-rep entitlement. |
| **"Free practice"** | Seeing every conversation as skill-building. | Frustration with bad-fit calls. |

### The Jacob mechanism (narrative folded)
The Jacob narrative on pp. 33–35 is illustrative of how the -isms transmit. Three operationally-extractable points from it:
1. **Outwork the top performer's quota.** New hires who explicitly aim to do *2× the leader's volume* close the gap fastest.
2. **Take the discarded leads.** The lowest-status leads (under-qualified, off-hours, weekends) are the practice volume that builds skill at low-risk.
3. **Reframe bad-fit calls as free practice.** Removes the emotional cost that makes reps avoid them.

These are mechanisms, not platitudes — they generalize beyond the specific Jacob story.

## Strategic Implications

- **Close rate as a sole metric distorts behavior.** Operators who track only close rate train reps to refuse marginal leads — which throws away the funnel volume that compounds over time. Three-metric ranking forces reps to value the whole funnel.
- **The commission bump is structural, not incentive theater.** Reps who get paid a fraction for shows and a fraction for closes will work both. Reps paid only on closes will skip nurture work because it has no direct payoff. The pay structure must mirror the metric structure.
- **Public approval is the most underused free lever in sales orgs.** Praising reps for *following the process* rather than for individual heroics shifts the social norm. The cost is zero. The reps who chase status will follow whichever behavior the leader visibly approves.
- **-isms are operating system patches.** Each one is a one-line rule that rewrites a default rep behavior. Embed them into the daily vocabulary (huddles, Slack, scorecards) and reps will self-correct. Use them sparingly enough that each one stays sharp.
- **The "garbage man / yellows are gold" stance is a competitive moat.** When the rest of the team cherry-picks, the rep who works yellows is doubling effective lead volume at zero cost — and developing a skill set the cherry-pickers never build.
- **Culture decays without measurement.** -isms without ranking are slogans. Ranking without -isms is mechanical. Both must run together for the four pillars to hold.

## Practical Use

Operator execution-culture rollout checklist:

1. **Stand up the three-metric weekly ranking** — show rate, close rate, lead-to-close — by sales rep. Make it visible to the team.
2. **Add a show-rate commission bump** — start at 5–10% of close commission, calibrate by ticket size.
3. **Pick 3–5 -isms** to embed into team vocabulary. Use them in huddles, scorecards, kudos posts. Don't use all 8 at once — they dilute.
4. **Reward process adherence publicly.** When a top performer says "I just followed the process," amplify it.
5. **Call out the inverse explicitly.** Reps with high close rates but poor show rates get the "entitled and wasteful" framing — directly, kindly, in private if needed but never silently.
6. **Onboard new reps to setter role first.** Best closers come from best setters. Use setting as low-stakes ramp.
7. **Make new reps explicitly chase 2× the leader's volume.** Sets the work norm immediately.
8. **Reframe bad-fit calls as free practice.** Remove the emotional cost of working low-yield leads — it doubles effective lead volume.
9. **Rank lead-to-close, not close-rate, as the primary scoreboard.** Bonuses, status, promotions all tied to the joint metric, not the close-rate alone.

## Notes / Limits

- **Culture takes longer than the four pillars.** Pillars I–IV produce throughput lift in 30–60 days. Cultural norms take 90+ days to embed and longer to harden. Operators expecting immediate culture change will under-invest in the patience required.
- **-isms only work top-down.** If the operator doesn't say them, the team won't. Pick the ones the operator will personally use in everyday speech — borrowed slogans collapse.
- **Three-metric ranking exposes uncomfortable truths.** Some "top closers" reveal as cherry-pickers; some "low closers" reveal as best setters underutilized. Rolling out the ranking produces awkward conversations early. That is the system working, not breaking.
- **Public disapproval is risky.** "Entitled and wasteful" framing can demoralize if used loosely. Reserve for clear cases of gaming the system, and pair every public correction with public approval of the right behavior elsewhere.
- **The Jacob narrative is illustrative, not prescriptive.** Don't try to recreate Jacob; use the three extracted mechanisms (outwork the leader, take discards, reframe practice) as the actual playbook.
- **Sales compensation law and structure varies.** Show-rate commission bumps may interact with local labor / compensation regulation, especially for non-W-2 reps and cross-jurisdictional teams.
