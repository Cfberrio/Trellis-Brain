# Hormozi — Pillar I: Availability

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Lead Nurture — Book Home]]

## Purpose
First and **highest-leverage** of the four lead-nurture pillars. Defines availability as the joint product of (a) how many appointment slots exist and (b) how soon leads can use them. Provides the four tactical levers operators pull to expand availability without adding sales heads at the same rate as throughput grows.

## Core Idea
> "If leads cannot schedule, they do not show."
>
> Availability is the **single biggest predictor** of throughput across portfolio data — "an almost perfect correlation." More slots, more days, more flexible start times, more booking channels = more schedules → more shows → more sales.

The mechanism is asymmetric: when business availability does not overlap with lead availability, leads either (a) do not schedule, (b) schedule at a time inconvenient for them (high ghost probability), or (c) take a slot that would have converted with another buyer. All three are losses; option (b) is the worst because it also poisons the calendar.

## Framework / Structure

### Definition
Availability = (days per week) × (hours per day) × (slot density per hour) × (channels through which a slot can be booked).

Diminishing returns exist but **large gains persist into the upper quartiles** — not just the bottom-end fixes.

### The four availability tactics

**1. Take Appointments More Days Per Week.**
Most operators run 5 days/week. Going to 7 = +40% available business surface area. Fixed costs (rent, payroll, insurance) are 7-day costs already; sales should match. "Rip off the Band-Aid" — incremental day-add typically delays the gain rather than smoothing the pain.

**2. Take Appointments More Hours Per Day.**
"Be available to sell when your customers are available to buy." For US-nationwide businesses, **6am–6pm PST** captures the highest sales-density-per-hour. Brick-and-mortar 9–5 M–F structurally misses the working-hours customer.

**3. Give Leads More Flexible Appointment Times — slot density.**
Most operators offer one or two start times per hour to match their internal block size. That serves the business, not the lead. **Recommended: four start options per hour (:00, :15, :30, :45)** even if appointment length stays the same. Side-effects:
- Awkward gaps between appointments. (Use those gaps for fast-contact outreach — see [[Hormozi-Lead-Nurture-Speed|Pillar II: Speed]].)
- May require additional sales capacity. The break-even is generous: most reps cost less than the 20–40% revenue lift from filling the slots.
- For high-ticket flows: separate **15-minute qualifying call** + **closing call** schedules eliminate the slot-density problem entirely.

**4. Have Inbound, Outbound, and Self-Scheduling — multi-channel booking.**
- Operator calls leads to set appointments (outbound).
- Leads call operator to set appointments (inbound).
- Leads schedule themselves online (self-serve).
Run all three. The more booking channels, the more bookings.

### Self-scheduler design rules
For online scheduler pages specifically:

1. **Header / Headline / Subheadline structure.** Header = "Attention [Avatar]" — confirms the visitor is in the right place. Headline + subheadline tell them what to do and why.
2. **Slots visible on page load.** Available dates and times must be obvious without scrolling — on both mobile and desktop.
3. **Strip every removable step.** Pre-fill any field the lead has already given you upstream. Eliminating duplicate-entry friction has outsized impact on schedule rate.
4. **Put a human face on it.** The book's anecdote: switching the scheduler from a faceless "you'll get a text" to "Text Leila at this number, here's her photo" produced a step-change in bookings. Real photo of a real person → higher conversion than abstract scheduler.

## Strategic Implications

- **Availability is the gate that opens the other three pillars.** Without slots, Speed has nothing to convert into; Personalization has no anchor; Volume has no destination. Fix this first.
- **Capacity gaps are not waste — they are the resource for Speed.** Slot-density expansion creates inter-appointment gaps. Those gaps become the time during which reps execute fast contact, pull-forward calls, and bonus drips. The "cost" of an open slot is the funding mechanism for the other pillars.
- **Booking friction is invisible revenue loss.** The book's framing: see the money you didn't make as money you lost. A 20-minute scheduler audit (slot density, mobile visibility, pre-fill, multi-channel) is one of the highest-ROI hours an operator can spend.
- **More availability does NOT mean lower-quality appointments.** That is the standard objection. The book's response: if too many bad-fits *do* book, **add deliberate friction** (qualifying questions, sales letter above scheduler, price disclosed, video pre-watch, delayed scheduler reveal). Friction is a quality lever, not a default. Set quality with friction; set quantity with availability.
- **B&M businesses pay rent 7 days a week and sell 5.** This is a structural value leak that the operator has already paid for. The marginal cost of selling on the unsold days is far below the average cost.

## Practical Use

Operator availability checklist (synthesizing the playbook checklist on pp. 39–40):

1. **Schedule appointments all 7 days per week.** No exceptions outside genuine staffing constraints.
2. **Open the longest day you can staff.** Aim **9am–9pm EST** as a starting goal; widen toward 6am–6pm PST for nationwide flows.
3. **Offer four start times per hour** on the scheduler (every 15 minutes), even if appointment length is 30 or 60 min.
4. **Stand up an automated self-scheduling page** with header/headline/subheadline structure, mobile-first, slots visible above the fold.
5. **Strip duplicate fields** — pre-fill anything the lead already gave you in opt-in.
6. **Add inbound + outbound paths.** Leads can call you; you can call leads. Multi-channel.
7. **Put a human face on the scheduler.** Real photo + first name of the actual person who follows up. Increases bookings.
8. **Audit for friction-add when bad-fit rate is too high** (not when it isn't). Use friction as a deliberate quality lever, not a default.

## Notes / Limits

- **Availability is an upper-bound, not a guarantee.** Adding slots without staffing the speed-to-contact follow-through fills the calendar with ghosters. Pillars II–IV must move with this one.
- **Friction-add is a deliberate inversion.** Tactics 1–4 reduce friction. The "Solution For Too Many Bad Appointments" tactic *adds* friction. Deploy it only when bad-appointment volume is the operator's actual constraint — adding it preemptively caps throughput.
- **Diminishing returns exist** at the upper end of slot density. The book notes the curve flattens but does not collapse — large gains persist into upper quartiles. Don't stop at "we doubled our slots"; keep optimizing until the marginal gain genuinely flattens.
- **Tech assumption.** Modern booking software with field pre-fill is assumed. Operators on legacy schedulers may need to upgrade tooling before tactics 3–4 work.
