# Hormozi — Brand Measurement

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Branding — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Quantitative frame for diagnosing where any brand stands. Replaces vague "strong/weak brand" talk with three measurable variables and the eight positions they form. Used to decide whether a brand needs more reach, a different pairing, or both.

## Core Idea
Brand performance is the product of three independent variables:

```
Brand impact  =  Reach  ×  Influence  ×  Direction
```

- **Reach** = how many people know about your stuff. Output of advertising. *A brand cannot exist without reach.*
- **Influence** = the probability that exposure changes behavior. Pairing strength.
- **Direction** = the sign of that behavior change — toward (purchase, refer, defend) or away (avoid, attack, warn). Audience-dependent.

Direction is not a property of the brand alone; it is the product of brand × audience. The same politician produces "toward" in one audience and "away" in another. A brand with high reach and high influence in *both* directions is not a strong brand — it is a polar brand, and only the toward-audience pays.

## Framework / Structure

### Three variables

| Variable | Definition | Driver | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reach | Number of people aware | Advertising volume + distribution | Brand does not exist; invisible |
| Influence | Probability of behavior change on exposure | Quality and consistency of pairings | Recognized but ignored |
| Direction | Sign of behavior change in target audience | Whether the pairing matches what the *ideal* audience likes | Audience moves away; sales fall |

### Eight brand positions

Reach × Influence × Direction yields 2³ = 8 cells. Hormozi's own examples (calibrated to him personally — same brand can occupy a different cell for a different observer):

| # | Reach | Influence | Direction | Example | Strategic read |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High | High | Toward | Elon Musk | Best-case business position. Buys generously and defends. |
| 2 | High | High | Away | Terrorists | Strongest negative — high reach drives revenue *down*. |
| 3 | High | Low | Toward | Taylor Swift (for non-fans aware of her) | Liked but not converting; brand is famous, not commercial. |
| 4 | High | Low | Away | Hollywood actors (in his read) | Known and disliked but not enough to drive action. |
| 5 | Low | High | Toward | Close friends | Tiny reach, decisive influence — referral engine. |
| 6 | Low | High | Away | Copycats / scammers | Few have heard of them; those who have stay clear. |
| 7 | Low | Low | Toward | Acquaintances | Default-friendly background brand. |
| 8 | Low | Low | Away | Strangers in the street | Default-avoidant background. |

**Read:** Cell #1 is the target. Cell #2 is the trap of mistaking volume for brand. Cell #5 is where most strong B2B referral practices actually live and explains why local trust often beats famous strangers.

### Money as the operational scoreboard

For business owners, the test collapses to: *Good branding means your audience likes and buys. Bad branding means your audience hates and does not.* Sales movement is the single sufficient diagnostic.

### Author Note: "Bad publicity" — clarified

"Any publicity is good publicity" conflates two variables. The accurate formulation:

> Good beats bad. Bad beats none. Lots of good beats everything.

In numbers: an unknown product earns no purchases; a widely-disliked product still earns the residual fraction of buyers who tolerate or favor it; a widely-liked product earns the most. The saying is true only as "any reach beats no reach," and only because reach itself is so scarce that even negative-direction reach produces some sales.

### Author Note: Polarization is a side effect, not a strategy

Strong brands need not be polar. Counter-examples: Taylor Swift, Apple, Mother Teresa — high reach, predominantly positive direction, no manufactured enemies. Polarization happens automatically when reach grows large enough; deliberately *trying* to polarize injects friction into selling. The strategic move:

> Make promises that serve a large audience, and keep them. When you sell a product, polarization occurs whether you want it to or not.

## Strategic Implications

- **Reach is necessary but not sufficient.** A brand stuck at high reach × low influence is a fame brand, not a commercial brand. Adding pairings (influence) yields more lift than adding more advertising.
- **Direction is audience-conditional.** Re-targeting the same brand at a different audience can flip direction without changing the asset itself. Pivots (see [[Hormozi-Brand-Bouquet|Brand Bouquet]]) are direction-flipping moves dressed up as audience moves.
- **Polar brands are paid by half their reach.** If 60% love and 40% hate, only 60% are buyers. Apparent reach overstates addressable market by the haters' fraction.
- **Influence compounds; reach decays.** A pairing that sticks (people who experienced the product) keeps producing direction long after the ad spend stops; reach without influence vanishes when spend stops.
- **Pepsi/Kendall Jenner reread.** Good advertising (people learned), bad branding (the protest pairing repelled the buying majority). The fault was the *pairing*, not the celebrity — Jenner with a different ad would have produced reach plus toward-direction. Diagnose pairings as objects, not the people carrying them.

## Practical Use

To diagnose any brand (own brand, competitor, partner, candidate spokesperson):

1. **Score reach.** Approximate audience size who could recognize the asset. Crude tiers (low / med / high) are enough.
2. **Score influence.** When exposure happens, what fraction take *any* action? Click-through, save, share, search, purchase — pick the relevant one.
3. **Score direction *for your ideal audience specifically*.** Not for the general public. For B2B, this is the buyer persona; for consumer, the actual customer cohort, not the cultural audience.
4. **Locate the cell.** Identify which of the 8 positions the brand occupies *for that audience*.
5. **Pick the lever.**
   - Low reach → advertising / distribution problem. Run more / better advertising.
   - High reach × low influence → pairing problem. The brand is famous but means nothing actionable. Choose deliberate pairings (see [[Hormozi-Brand-Building-Steps|Brand Building Steps]]).
   - High reach × high influence × wrong direction → pairing is wrong for *this* audience. Either change pairings or change audience (see [[Hormozi-Brand-Bouquet|Brand Bouquet]] pivot model).

## Notes / Limits

- The 8-cell map is a discrete grid over continuous variables. It is a forced-choice tool for triage, not a measurement system. Two brands in the same cell can differ materially.
- Direction is not the same as sentiment. A brand can produce favorable sentiment ("I love them") with low influence ("…but I'd never buy"). Influence × direction must be measured against an action, not a feeling.
- "Lots of good beats everything" creates a temptation to scale early. Scale before pairings are stable amplifies whichever pairings are accidentally dominant — which is how brands accidentally land in cells #2 or #4. Stabilize pairings, then scale.
