# Hormozi — Levels of Authority

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Branding — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Explains *whose voice changes the audience's behavior most*, and how the three sources interact to produce the virtuous (or vicious) cycle that compounds — or destroys — a brand. The mechanism that determines whether a brand earned by [[Hormozi-Brand-Building-Steps|Brand Building Steps]] keeps growing or hollows out.

## Core Idea
Three sources of authority shape the audience's beliefs about a brand, with strictly increasing weight:

1. **What you say** — owned media, ads, outreach.
2. **What other people say** — affiliates, customers, third parties.
3. **What they experience themselves** — the product or service in use.

The third dominates the first two over time. Advertising opens the door; the product decides whether the door stays open. The same loop runs in either direction: deliver and the cycle compounds positive associations; under-deliver and it compounds negative ones — *faster than any other marketing channel*.

## Framework / Structure

### The three levels

| Level | Source | Authority | Operator control | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | What you say | Lowest | Highest | Ads, content, outbound, your own claims |
| 2 | What other people say | Higher | Medium | Affiliate posts, customer reviews, third-party edification |
| 3 | What they experience themselves | Highest | Lowest (driven by product quality) | Personal use, results delivered |

The asymmetry is key: control is inversely proportional to authority. The voice the audience trusts most is the one the operator least directly controls — which is precisely why product quality is the load-bearing input.

### The branding cycle

The same three-stage loop runs in two directions, decided by whether the product delivers on the promise:

**Virtuous cycle (good branding)**
1. Advertising lands a first sale.
2. Product delivers on the promise.
3. Customer forms a strong positive association.
4. Customer buys again *and* tells others.
5. Others enter at level 2 ("what other people say") with more authority than the original ad.
6. They buy and experience the product themselves — feeding back into level 3.
7. Each loop lowers CAC, raises LTV, lowers risk (see [[Hormozi-Brand-Economics|Brand Economics]]).

**Vicious cycle (bad branding)**
1. Advertising lands a first sale.
2. Product fails to deliver.
3. Customer forms a strong negative association.
4. Customer does not buy again *and* discourages others.
5. Negative word of mouth enters level 2 with the same elevated authority — but pointed against the brand.
6. Acquisition costs rise; repeat purchase collapses.
7. Hormozi's anchor: *"Bad products make it harder and harder to get customers."*

> Buffett, cited: *"Your premium brand had better be delivering something special, or it's not going to get the business."*
> Hormozi's addition: *"It won't keep the business."*

The premium pricing earned in [[Hormozi-Brand-Economics|Brand Economics]] is conditional on the product clearing the bar set by the brand. Premium pricing without premium delivery accelerates the vicious cycle.

### Brand goes both ways

The brand–product relationship is bidirectional, and the relationship between brand and *recommender* is also bidirectional:

- **Brand → product perception.** Strong brand raises the prior on a product before the audience tries it; weak brand lowers it.
- **Product → brand perception.** Each product experience updates the brand. A bad product hit under a strong brand drags the whole brand down ("this brand sucks now"). A great product hit under a weak brand lifts the brand quickly.
- **Recommender ↔ brand.** A trusted recommender raises the prior on the brand; the brand's quality then updates the audience's view of the recommender. A bad referred product makes the audience trust the recommender less — and a good referred product makes them trust the recommender more.
- **Pairing transfers categorically.** Hormozi's example: *"if a porn star recommends an accounting firm, I may assume it's an accounting firm for porn stars."* The category of the recommender transfers onto the recommended thing. This is why pairing selection is a strategic, not cosmetic, decision.

### Time horizon

Different inputs dominate at different timescales:

| Horizon | Dominant input | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Short term | Advertising (level 1) | First impressions, no product experience yet |
| Long term | Product (level 3) | Repeat experiences accumulate; word of mouth compounds |

This is why short-term branded growth from clever campaigns can mask a worsening underlying brand. Sales today, run on level 1, are repaid (or punished) at level 3 over months and years.

## Strategic Implications

- **Premium pricing imposes a delivery contract.** Charging more obligates the product to over-deliver, not just deliver. The level-3 voice will be the one heard most by the next cohort.
- **Negative word of mouth compounds faster than positive.** Hormozi's claim is asymmetric: bad branding propagates faster than any marketing hack can compensate. Operators feel this as "we just need a new offer / new ad" while losing the silent cohort that no longer buys.
- **Choose recommenders by category, not by reach alone.** A high-reach recommender from the wrong category transfers wrong-category associations onto the brand. The Brand Measurement variable that matters for level 2 is *direction in your ideal audience*, not raw reach.
- **Product is a branding asset.** R&D, QA, support, and onboarding sit on the same balance sheet as marketing — they are the only inputs that move the level-3 voice. Underfunding them while overfunding level 1 is the most common path into the vicious cycle.
- **The "first sale" trap.** A brand that consistently wins first sales but loses the second is on a vicious cycle masquerading as growth. Cohort retention is the leading indicator of which loop the brand is in.

## Practical Use

To diagnose which cycle a brand is in:

1. **Score each level.**
   - Level 1: are claims clear, consistent, and on-target for the avatar?
   - Level 2: what fraction of new customers report a referral or third-party source? What is the sentiment of available reviews?
   - Level 3: what is the second-purchase / renewal rate vs. category benchmark? What does post-purchase NPS or qualitative feedback say?
2. **Identify the dominant voice.** The voice with the largest delta-on-purchase decision is the one that is currently driving the brand. If it is level 1, the brand is unstable — level 3 has not yet had a chance to confirm or refute the claim.
3. **Apply the directional test.** If level-3 sentiment trails level-1 promises, the vicious cycle has begun. Fix product before scaling reach. Scaling reach on a delivery gap multiplies the negative compounding.
4. **For partner / affiliate / influencer pairings, vet category fit.** Do not optimize purely for reach; check that the recommender's existing audience and category are at most one step from the avatar in [[Hormozi-Brand-Building-Steps|Brand Building Steps]].
5. **Build the level-2 pipeline deliberately.** Reviews, case studies, affiliate commissions, customer-led content. Level 2 does not appear automatically from a good product — it appears when the operator engineers the path from a satisfied level-3 experience into a public level-2 voice.

## Notes / Limits

- The three levels are stylized; in practice, the audience hears them blended (an ad inside a podcast inside a friend's recommendation). The model is for diagnosis, not classification.
- Strong brands can survive isolated level-3 misses if the surrounding pairings remain heavily positive — this is the [[Hormozi-Brand-Bouquet|Brand Bouquet]] mistake-recovery mechanism (overwhelm with positives). The model does not say a single bad experience is fatal; it says systematic level-3 failure is.
- Cycle direction is observable only with cohort-level data. Aggregate revenue can rise during the early stage of a vicious cycle while underlying retention is collapsing. Cohort retention and second-purchase rates are the right instruments.
