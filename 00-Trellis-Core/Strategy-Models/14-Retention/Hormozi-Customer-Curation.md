# Hormozi — Customer Curation

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Lever #5 of the [[Hormozi-Churn-Checklist|Churn Checklist]]. Recognizes that **some customers cause other customers to churn**, and treats that as a retention problem the operator must actively manage. Provides the two-sided behavior-shaping mechanic (incentivize good behavior + correct or eject bad behavior) and the structured topic-category system that channels customer activity in directions the operator can scale.

## Core Idea
> "Bad customers make it bad for everyone else."

Curation is dual: reward and reinforce the behaviors you want; correct and eject the behaviors you don't. Operators who shy away from this lever usually do so out of revenue protection — they keep paying members who damage the cohort. The math goes against them: one toxic member retained at revenue X often churns ≥2 other members at revenue X each.

## Framework / Structure

### Reinforce good behavior
- **Pin the best 1–2 new posts daily.** Three effects:
  1. Elevates the contributing member (status — see [[Hormozi-Activation-Incentives|Activation Incentives]]).
  2. Provides fresh value to everyone else who sees the pin.
  3. **Signals to the group what content you reward** — others copy the format. (Bonus: pinned posts also help members reach activation level 3 — see [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]].)
- **Praise in public.** Use the public channels for recognition; reserve private channels for correction.
- **Provide value to everyone else.** Each recognition act is also a content asset for the rest of the cohort.

### Correct (or eject) bad behavior
- **Delete bad posts.** Do not leave them up.
- **Tell the offender why the post sucked.** Vague moderation does not change behavior.
- **Tell them what good looks like.** Pair the correction with the standard, so they can recover.
- **Three-strike rule.** Three corrected violations → removal from the community.

The 3-strike pattern is intentionally finite. It gives the customer a clear opportunity to course-correct (you correct twice, eject on the third), and it sets the cohort's expectation that the standard is enforced.

### The four canonical topic categories
Hormozi's structured category system — tested across many Skool groups — channels member contributions into directions that double as operator assets:

| Category | Customer-facing prompt | Operator's strategic use |
|---|---|---|
| **Wins** | Share results / progress | Source of testimonials |
| **Fun** | Anything light — keeps the room human | Casual engagement, lowers churn from monotony |
| **Discovered** | Share what you learned, with data and proof | High-quality knowledge artifacts; teaches the cohort |
| **Meetups** | Connect on or offline | Direct fuel for [[Hormozi-Community-Linking\|Community Linking]] |

Name them whatever fits the brand voice. The function is what matters: each category serves both the member (a clear thing to post about) and the operator (a content asset, a moderation framework, or a community-linking surface).

## Strategic Implications
- **Curation is retention work for the people you're keeping, not just the people you're firing.** Every bad post left up signals to good members that the operator does not enforce standards; their participation drops.
- **The 3-strike rule converts ejection from drama into procedure.** Without a clear policy, every removal is a charged event. With the policy, removal is impersonal — which makes operators more willing to actually do it.
- **Topic categories are activation mechanics in disguise.** Telling members exactly what to post ("Wins / Fun / Discovered / Meetups") removes the blank-page friction that depresses participation, which lifts engagement, which lifts retention.
- **Public praise + private correction is the operator's most efficient signal architecture.** Same effort, better effect than the inverse (correcting in public, praising in private).
- **Bad-customer math.** Retaining a customer who damages the cohort is negative-NPV even when the immediate revenue is positive. Always net the cohort effect.

## Practical Use
1. **Define your "good" and "bad" behavior list.** What posts / interactions raise the cohort's level? What drags it down? Make this list explicit before enforcement starts.
2. **Set up the four topic categories** (Wins / Fun / Discovered / Meetups, or your branded equivalent) on every community surface you operate.
3. **Stand up the 3-strike policy publicly.** Document it; reference it on every correction; eject on the third strike without negotiation.
4. **Daily pin routine.** Pin 1–2 best new posts each day. This becomes a standing operator (or community manager) task.
5. **Public praise / private correction discipline.** Train any moderator on this split.
6. **Audit the cohort quarterly for net-negative members.** Ones whose presence reduces the cohort's engagement or retention. Engage corrective process; eject if it does not change.

## Notes / Limits
- **Ejection has revenue cost.** Some operators flinch at firing paying customers. The math (lifetime damage to other members) usually justifies it; do the math before flinching.
- **Topic categories work best with active moderation.** Without enforcement, members default to whatever post format gets the most attention regardless of category. Pinning + correction enforce the structure.
- **Curation is a community-shaped lever.** SaaS tools without a community surface do not have a posts-and-replies object to curate. The closest analogues are user-onboarding feedback loops, support-ticket norms, and customer-event behavior — same principles apply.
- **3-strike is a default, not a doctrine.** Egregious violations (harassment, fraud) warrant immediate removal; the 3-strike pattern is for behavior that is salvageable with feedback.
