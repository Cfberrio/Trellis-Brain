# Hormozi — Churn Economics

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Establishes why retention investment outperforms acquisition investment on the same dollar. Provides the four economic arguments operators need to (a) commit budget to retention work, (b) defend that budget against acquisition-only thinking, and (c) frame churn as a structural valuation lever, not a cost line. Sets the financial stake the rest of the Churn Checklist operates against.

## Core Idea
> "Your existing customers are easier to sell — and cost significantly less — than new customers. So invest effort accordingly."
>
> "If referrals are greater than churn, you never stop growing."

A business with a revolving door of customers is hardly a business at all. Acquiring customers raises the top line; retaining them raises the floor, the multiple, and the rate of compounding. The ceiling on acquisition is the auction-clearing CAC of your channel; the ceiling on retention is set by the value you deliver. Only one of those two ceilings moves with discipline.

## Framework / Structure

### The four economic arguments

**1. Math of churn → LTV (the 3.3x argument).**
Lifetime months ≈ `1 / churn`. Cutting churn from 10% → 3%:
- Old lifetime: `1 / 0.10` = 10 months.
- New lifetime: `1 / 0.03` ≈ 33 months.
- LTV multiplier: **3.3×** for the same customer at the same price.

The "leaky bucket" complement (p. 4):
- 15% MoM churn = `(1 − 0.15)^12` = **17% retained** annually = 83% lost.
- A gym with 250 members on Jan 1 needs 208 new clients by Dec 31 just to stay flat.
- At 12.5% margins, falling two members behind per month sinks the business.

**2. Cost ratio (the 5–25× argument).**
> "It costs five (5x) to twenty-five times (25x) more to acquire a new customer compared to retaining one."

Operator rule of thumb: budget retention spend at **1/5 of CAC** per customer. At that level retention work pays back inside the first save, and any over-budget retention investment is still cheaper than the acquisition alternative.

**3. Profit elasticity (the HBR 5%/25–95% argument).**
A 5-percentage-point lift in retention rate has been documented to lift profit **25–95%** (Harvard Business Review). Practical translation: extending average customer life from 1 month to 1.05 months can increase profit by a quarter to nearly double. Retention is the most leveraged dollar in the P&L.

**4. Compounding condition (referrals > churn).**
If `referrals_rate > churn_rate`, the business grows even without paid acquisition. Doing retention activities directly increases referrals (happier customers refer more), which compounds the lift. Acquisition spend on top of a compounding base scales; acquisition spend on top of a leaking base only delays the failure.

### The "easier customer" profile
Existing customers are the only prospects who already:
- Trust you.
- Have received value from you.
- Have given you their credit card (no friction to repurchase).
- Carry zero acquisition cost.
- Match your ICP (you sold them once).

Selling to anyone else is a downgrade on five dimensions simultaneously.

### High-churn vs. low-churn business outcomes (synthesis from "Putting It All Together")

| Low-churn business outcomes | High-churn business outcomes |
|---|---|
| Grows every year even if sales stay flat | Must sell new customers every month just to stay flat |
| Profit compounds | No compounding |
| More valuable / easier to sell | Hard to sell, lower multiple |
| Operator sleeps well | Operator is "only as good as last month" |
| Positive word-of-mouth → free CAC | Negative word-of-mouth → rising CAC |
| Retains best talent (employees see customers winning) | Talent leaves (employees see customers failing) |
| Tailwind from reputation | Forced to invent "new stuff" to stay alive |

## Strategic Implications
- **Retention is the highest-leverage spend in the P&L.** A 5-percentage-point retention lift returns 25–95% on profit. No acquisition channel matches this elasticity at scale.
- **CAC has a floor; LTV does not.** Auction-based acquisition channels inflate over time. The only durable response is to make each customer worth more — which starts with keeping each customer longer.
- **Compounding is conditional, not automatic.** Compounding requires `referrals > churn`. The Churn Checklist tightens both sides: every retention activity also lifts referrals.
- **Valuation is a churn function.** Acquirers price recurring businesses on net retention. Two businesses with identical revenue and identical growth trade at radically different multiples based on retention. Churn work is not opex; it is balance-sheet work.
- **Acquisition investment is undermined by churn.** Spending on CAC for a leaky bucket converts marketing dollars into temporary revenue and accelerates negative word-of-mouth ("everyone in your market knows you suck") — a worse-than-zero return.

## Practical Use
1. **Compute current churn and current `1/churn` lifetime.** Restate every per-customer revenue figure as `price × (1/churn)`.
2. **Set a churn target.** Default initial target: cut current churn in half. Re-target after 6 months.
3. **Allocate a retention budget = 1/5 × CAC per customer.** Spend this on the activities in the [[Hormozi-Churn-Checklist|Churn Checklist]].
4. **Watch the referral rate alongside churn.** When `referrals_rate − churn_rate > 0`, the business compounds without paid acquisition; that becomes a strategic option.
5. **Frame the spend internally as valuation work, not opex.** Boards and investors price recurring businesses on retention; retention work is direct multiple expansion.

## Notes / Limits
- **Structural churn is real.** Businesses that serve VSMBs (very small business owners) carry baseline churn from customers going out of business — Shopify and Skool both operate this way. High-structural-churn businesses can still build large companies; the rule is "still reduce it; do not target zero." The 3.3× LTV math still applies on the controllable portion.
- **Some churn is healthy.** Customers who do not fit the ICP should churn — extending bad-fit lifetimes raises support cost and depresses NPS. Retention work should pair with [[Hormozi-Customer-Curation|Customer Curation]] (firing/correcting bad customers).
- **Retention investment has diminishing returns.** Below ~3% monthly churn, the next percentage point gets expensive. Hormozi: "I have yet to see a business with less than 3% churn that does not make great money." 3% is the operator's reasonable floor.
- **Numbers cited (3.3×, 5–25×, 25–95%) are illustrative ranges from Hormozi's reading of HBR and personal portfolio data.** Use them to motivate the spend; reproduce the *shape* of the argument with your own numbers before defending a budget.
