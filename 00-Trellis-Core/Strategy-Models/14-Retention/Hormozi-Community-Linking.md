# Hormozi — Community Linking

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Lever #4 of the [[Hormozi-Churn-Checklist|Churn Checklist]]. Reframes retention as a network problem rather than a customer-to-operator problem. Customers leave subscriptions easily; they do not leave relationships easily. The retention move is to **convert single ties (customer → operator) into mesh ties (customer ↔ customer ↔ customer).**

## Core Idea
> "Connect members to each other, not you. It's more scalable to facilitate multiple, deeper connections than one single tie to the group."
>
> "It's easy to quit a membership; it's hard to leave a relationship."
>
> "They come for the bikini. They stay for the community."

The single-tie model has a structural ceiling: the operator's bandwidth and the customer's likelihood of feeling personally bonded to a business. The mesh model compounds with cohort size — every additional connection per customer multiplies the friction of leaving.

## Framework / Structure

### The four community-linking mechanics

**1. Group events.**
Convert 1-on-1 onboarding into 1-on-N onboarding **specifically to introduce members to each other**, not to save operator time.
- Skool example: a community owner moved from 1-on-1 → **1-on-6 onboarding**. Same depth of personalization, but six new members met each other in the same session. Result: low churn.
- Gym example: regular group events. "People came for the 6-pack and stayed for the community."

**2. Manually connect people.**
If you think two members would benefit from knowing each other, introduce them. This is the highest-trust form of connection because the operator vouches for both sides.

**3. Start a community podcast.**
Interview members. Two effects: (a) the interviewed member gains status (see also [[Hormozi-Activation-Incentives|Activation Incentives]]), and (b) other members see who's in the room and want to meet them.

**4. Elevate micro-celebrities.**
Call out specific members in public channels for specific areas of expertise. This creates known-quantity members the rest of the cohort wants to engage with.

### Why mesh ties retain better than single ties
- **Scalability.** Operator capacity does not scale; member-to-member connections do — every new member is a potential new tie for every existing member.
- **Switching cost.** Cancelling a relationship requires explaining the cancellation to people; cancelling a subscription does not.
- **Identity reinforcement.** Belonging to a known group reinforces the customer's identity as someone who is part of *this thing*. Identity is harder to leave than a service.
- **Free retention work.** Members hold each other accountable, encourage each other, and onboard each other — work the operator otherwise pays for.

### The compounding logic
Pair this lever with [[Hormozi-Customer-Onboarding|onboarding]] (group cohort onboarding) and [[Hormozi-Customer-Curation|customer curation]] (Wins / Fun / Discovered / Meetups topic categories — the meetups category is explicitly designed for community linking). The three together produce more ties per member per month than any single mechanic.

## Strategic Implications
- **Member-to-member is the dominant retention asset for any community-shaped product.** Operators who build their model around themselves (the founder, the brand) hit a structural retention ceiling. Operators who build the mesh do not.
- **Group onboarding is sometimes superior to 1-on-1.** For [[Hormozi-Customer-Onboarding|onboarding]], 1-on-1 generally wins — but a deliberately small-group onboarding (1-on-6) wins on retention specifically because the group form double-counts as community linking.
- **Status systems are dual-purpose.** Public callouts and micro-celebrity elevation incentivize the called-out member ([[Hormozi-Activation-Incentives|Activation Incentives]]) and create known nodes for the rest of the cohort to connect to (Community Linking). Build them once, get both effects.
- **The community is the moat.** Competitors can copy product features; they cannot copy the relationships members have inside your community. Once mesh density is high, the product becomes hard to replace even when feature parity exists.

## Practical Use
1. **Audit current ties per member.** How many other members does your average customer know by name? If <2, this lever has the most leverage in the checklist for you.
2. **Convert at least one onboarding cohort into a small-group format.** 1-on-6 is Hormozi's reference number; pick a number that fits your cadence.
3. **Run a manual-introduction routine.** Monthly: identify 5–10 pairs of members who would benefit from meeting; make the intros explicitly.
4. **Schedule recurring group events.** Cadence per the [[Hormozi-Five-Horsemen-Of-Retention|Five Horsemen]]: 21 / 42 / 63 days is a reasonable default.
5. **Launch a community podcast** featuring members. Even an internal one (members-only) has the status + connection effect.
6. **Pin / praise micro-celebrity members in public** — tied to their specific expertise, not generic praise.
7. **Add a "meetups" category** to whatever community surface you have (forum, Discord, Skool, in-person calendar) so members can self-organize.
8. **Measure ties-per-member every quarter.** Survey or count interactions. Watch the metric climb.

## Notes / Limits
- **Community linking only works where customers can actually interact.** Solo SaaS tools without community surfaces have to invent the surface (forum, group, customer slack) before this lever applies.
- **Bad customers in the mesh damage the mesh.** Curate aggressively (see [[Hormozi-Customer-Curation|Customer Curation]]). One toxic member retained for revenue can churn five others through the social effect.
- **Operator-facilitated intros do not scale to massive cohorts directly.** At scale, the mechanic shifts from "operator introduces individuals" to "operator structures formats that produce intros automatically" (small-group cohorts, peer programs, structured matchmaking).
- **Community work is real labor.** Hormozi's framing is straightforward: this matters, so resource it. Operators who try to "let the community happen organically" usually get nothing.
