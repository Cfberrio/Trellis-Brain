# Hormozi — Customer Survey & ACA

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Lever #8 of the [[Hormozi-Churn-Checklist|Churn Checklist]]. Two coupled mechanisms operating on different cadences against the same goal — keep the product aligned with the 2–3 things customers actually value, and keep the relationship warm in between.

1. **Twice-yearly forced-tradeoff survey** to find the core 2–3 things the product/service is actually doing for customers.
2. **ACA reach-out cadence** (Acknowledge / Compliment / Ask) every 2–3 weeks between surveys to maintain relationship signal.

Distinct from [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]] (find the *leading indicator* of retention) — Survey & ACA finds the *trailing indicator of value* and maintains the relationship that protects against churn drift.

## Core Idea
Operators build features. Customers consume value. The list of features and the list of valued things diverge over time. Survey twice a year to re-anchor what the product is actually for. Reach out in between to keep the relationship signal active so you have someone who answers the survey when it goes out.

> "Reaching out to customers 1-on-1 every two to three weeks retained more people than never speaking with customers."

The survey finds the 2–3 things to double down on. ACA earns the right for the survey to be answered.

## Framework / Structure

### The forced-tradeoff survey

Run twice a year. List everything the product/service provides. Ask **two paired questions**:

> Q1. "If I removed everything on this list but one, what would you want to keep the most?"
>
> Q2. "If I kept everything on this list but one, which would bother you the least to see me remove?"

Q1 surfaces the **core value** (highest-importance items). Q2 surfaces the **dead weight** (lowest-importance items, candidates for cutting).

The forced-tradeoff structure beats Likert / "rate from 1 to 5" formats because customers cannot avoid trading off. Generic positive ratings mask which items actually matter.

### Worked example (per source)
A portfolio company ran this survey. Customers said they loved the **events** the company threw. The company listened, doubled down, and **events became one of their largest product lines.**

Outcome pattern:
- Operator's perception of the most valued thing ≠ customers' lived perception.
- The survey closes the gap.
- The discovered top item often deserves elevation from feature → product line.

### The ACA reach-out framework
Frequency: every 2–3 weeks between large surveys.
Channel: 1-on-1 (text / DM / call — whatever channel the customer responds to).
Mechanism: three-step touch.

| Step | Letter | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **A**cknowledge | Acknowledge what you've seen them do. Specific behavior, named. |
| 2 | **C**ompliment | Compliment them on something tied to that behavior. |
| 3 | **A**sk | Ask a question. Open-ended, low-effort to answer, signals interest. |

The structure produces an inbound that does not feel like sales pressure or scripted CSM check-in. It reads as personal because each step requires real attention to the specific customer.

### Wealth-and-handholding pattern (counterintuitive)
> "Counterintuitively, the wealthier the customers, the less handholding they typically want."

Recalibrate ACA cadence by customer tier:
- Lower-tier / earlier-career customers → benefit from frequent ACA touch.
- Higher-tier / wealthier customers → tolerate, sometimes resent, frequent touch.

Default to 2–3 weeks; loosen for high-net-worth customer bases.

### Action step (per source)
> "Survey your customers twice per year and ask them what they value and what they don't. Do more of the stuff they like. And cut that stuff they don't. Reach out to them regularly so it looks like you care (because you should)."

Two operational moves: survey twice yearly with the forced-tradeoff format; ACA reach-out every 2–3 weeks.

## Strategic Implications

- **Twice-yearly is enough; quarterly creates survey fatigue.** Hormozi specifies the cadence — it is calibrated to the rate at which customer perception of value actually shifts. Surveying more often produces noise.
- **The forced tradeoff is the design choice that makes the survey useful.** Likert-scale and rating surveys produce flat positive feedback that is operationally unactionable. The "remove everything but one" framing forces customers to expose the *priority order* hidden under the positive ratings.
- **Q2 is as important as Q1.** Most operators only ask the keep-the-best version. Cutting the bottom is structurally harder and more often skipped — but cutting dead weight reduces operator load and raises perceived value (less overwhelm; see [[Hormozi-Value-Per-Second|Value Per Second]]).
- **ACA outperforms automation.** Drip emails, lifecycle nurture sequences, and templated check-ins do not produce the same retention lift. Source explicitly: 1-on-1 reach-out > silence. The retention lift is in the personalization, not the touch frequency.
- **The discovered-favorite-feature pattern is repeatable.** Source provides one worked case (events became a product line) but the underlying pattern — customers value something operators underweight — appears in most surveys when properly run. Survey results should change roadmap, not just confirm it.
- **ACA is a soft-skill leverage move.** Three steps; ~3 minutes per customer per touch; produces durable retention. One CSM running ACA across 200 accounts every 2–3 weeks is high-leverage labor.
- **Survey results plus [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]] data triangulate strategy.** Activation Points find what *leads* to retention; surveys find what customers *experience as valuable*. When both align, double down. When they diverge, investigate — the messaging or onboarding is probably wrong.

## Practical Use

### Survey rollout (twice a year)
1. **List every distinct thing** the product/service provides. Be granular — each feature, asset, call, deliverable, template, community access tier.
2. **Send Q1 first**, Q2 in a follow-up wave (or both in one message with the two paired questions).
3. **Tag responses** by customer tier / cohort / tenure for slicing.
4. **Identify the top 2–3 from Q1** across the customer base. These become your double-down list.
5. **Identify the bottom 2–3 from Q2.** These are cut candidates. Verify they are genuinely low-value before removing (not just unfamiliar).
6. **Update messaging, onboarding, and sales scripts** to lead with the top 2–3. The marketing pitch should match what customers actually value.
7. **Cut or de-emphasize the bottom items** to free up operational and customer attention.

### ACA cadence (every 2–3 weeks)
Per touch, per customer:
1. **Acknowledge.** "Saw you posted in the community about [specific thing]" / "Noticed you completed [specific milestone]."
2. **Compliment.** "That's exactly the right move at this stage" / "The way you handled [X] is going to compound."
3. **Ask.** "What's the next thing you're working on?" / "What's the bottleneck right now?"

Operationalization:
- Use whatever signal source the operator already has (community posts, login frequency, support tickets, milestones).
- Calibrate to customer tier — high-net-worth customers may want monthly or only-when-asked, not bi-weekly.
- Track responses. ACA is also a churn radar — silence after 2–3 unanswered ACAs is a signal.

### Combination
The survey tells you *what* to deliver; ACA keeps the relationship that delivers it. Both mechanisms are needed. Survey-without-ACA produces low response rates. ACA-without-survey produces a warm relationship around an aging product.

## Notes / Limits

- The survey is forced-tradeoff format specifically. Substituting a Likert-scale satisfaction survey defeats the lever — Hormozi explicitly contrasts the two-question structure against generic surveys.
- ACA cadence (every 2–3 weeks) is a default, not a hard rule. Source flags wealthier customers want less. Calibrate.
- ACA is genuinely 1-on-1. Templated "Acknowledge / Compliment / Ask" mass emails read as sales — they do not produce the documented retention lift.
- Survey results that contradict operator intuition are *more* valuable, not less. The temptation is to discount surprising findings — resist.
- Cutting low-value items based on Q2 must be verified — sometimes the bottom item is genuinely valuable but to a small high-paying segment. Cross-check by tier before removing.
- The "wealthier = less handholding" pattern is an *empirical observation* in the source, not a universal law. Test in your specific customer base.
