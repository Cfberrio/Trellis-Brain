# Hormozi — Customer Onboarding

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Lever #2 of the [[Hormozi-Churn-Checklist|Churn Checklist]]. Defines onboarding the way Hormozi means it (not "tour the app" — *teach the customer to hit the activation point*) and provides the five-axis quality ranking that orders onboarding investment from cheapest-and-weakest to most-expensive-and-strongest. Without onboarding the activation point identified in [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|lever #1]] is unreached and retention work has no foundation.

## Core Idea
> "Onboarding means: teach customers how to hit the activation points I just painstakingly investigated."

Onboarding is not a welcome email. It is the engineered first-week journey from purchase → activation behavior. The activation point is the destination; onboarding is the route. If the route is unclear, the customer wanders and churns.

## Framework / Structure

### Five-axis quality ranking
Each onboarding decision lands on one of these axes. Move right whenever budget and capacity allow — every move right reduces churn at the cost of operator time.

| Axis | Weaker → Stronger |
|---|---|
| Generic → **Custom** | The closer to the customer's specific situation, the better |
| Group → **Personal** | One-on-one outperforms one-to-many |
| Recorded → **Live** | Real-time outperforms async |
| Sticks → **Carrots** | Rewards outperform threats |
| **None → Some** | Anything onboarding beats no onboarding |

The bottom row is the most important — operators frequently ship products with no onboarding and lose the activation step entirely. The first move is *some*; the rest of the axes are progressive upgrades.

### The onboarding skeleton
Hormozi's structure (Skool example, generalizable):

1. **Outline how to get value → drive to the activation point.**
   - Tell them where to start.
   - Tell them what specific behaviors to do (e.g., make a post in this format; comment on 1–2 other posts).
   - Connect the new customer to 4–6 other people during onboarding so they already know community members on day one.
   - Tell them what they will *unlock* once they finish onboarding (level 3, etc.). Homework gets them to the unlock.
   - Show them what to cancel/replace to free the budget for this membership; have them do it as homework and prove it.

2. **Resell the value of the purchase.** Frame it inside the customer's stated goals. They just bought; reinforce why.

3. **Tell them how they unlock more value as they stay longer.**
   - Skool example: members can earn the group free for life at level 8 ("it'll make you more than it costs you").
   - This is the bridge to [[Hormozi-Activation-Incentives|Activation Incentives]] (lever #3).

4. **Set communication best practices.** How posts work, group rules, expectations between you/team and the customer.

5. **Follow up.**
   - Onboarding is the install of the long-term communication rhythm.
   - **Customers should always know when they will hear from you next.** This is the structural anchor that makes [[Hormozi-Customer-Survey-ACA|ongoing ACA reach-outs]] (lever #8) feel routine instead of intrusive.

> "Do this for sales prospects as well. It will increase your close rate."

### Optional: sell the onboarding
Make onboarding a paid prerequisite to the main offer.

> Gym example: a new member buys six personal training sessions before they can join the general group classes. Wildly effective. The customer earns the right to enter the general base — which raises (a) the activation rate (1-on-1 is the best onboarding) and (b) the perceived value of the general base.

This pattern is also a [[../03-100M-Money-Models/Hormozi-Attraction-Offers|attraction-offer]] mechanic — paid onboarding as the entry vehicle.

### Real-world progression (portfolio company)
- **Step 1:** moved from no onboarding → group onboarding. Activation lifted.
- **Step 2 (a year later):** moved from group → 1-on-1 onboarding (despite high volume).
- **Result:** **+25% ascensions**, higher LTV, scaled ad spend.
- **One of the largest contributors to going from $2M / month → $2M / week.**

The lesson: the most personalized onboarding the operator can sustain wins.

## Strategic Implications
- **Onboarding is the activation step's only reliable delivery mechanism.** Identifying an activation point ([[Hormozi-Activation-Points|lever #1]]) without engineering a route to it produces analysis paralysis. Onboarding is the route.
- **Per-axis improvements compound.** Moving from generic to custom AND from recorded to live AND from sticks to carrots produces multiplicative effect, not additive.
- **1-on-1 onboarding is the LTV multiplier.** Counterintuitively expensive in operator time, but documented as a primary driver of category-leading growth (Hormozi's $2M/month → $2M/week portfolio case). Always re-test whether 1-on-1 is feasible at the next scale step.
- **Selling onboarding flips the unit economics.** Paid onboarding turns the activation step from a cost center into a revenue line, while filtering for higher-commitment customers. Where the model fits, do it.
- **The follow-up promise made during onboarding sets the retention rhythm forever.** Customers who never hear from you after onboarding default to a "what am I paying for" mental loop. The onboarding is where the operator commits to the cadence.

## Practical Use
1. **Identify the activation point** for your business ([[Hormozi-Activation-Points|lever #1]] — must be done first).
2. **Audit current onboarding against the five axes.** Where are you on each axis?
3. **Move right on the cheapest axis first.** Easiest wins:
   - None → Some (build *anything*).
   - Sticks → Carrots (rewrite copy in incentive language).
   - Generic → Custom (insert customer name + their stated goal).
4. **Build the skeleton.** Outline value path → resell → unlocks → comms norms → follow-up promise.
5. **For high-ticket products, default to 1-on-1.** Treat it as a non-optional cost.
6. **For lower-ticket products, do "what makes the most sense for the cost"** — but ship *some* version. Cohort-based group onboarding with an automated email skeleton is a reasonable starting point.
7. **Test selling the onboarding.** Either as a paid pre-requisite or as a value-front-loaded paid setup (see [[Hormozi-Annual-Payment-Options|Big-Head-Long-Tail]] in Annual Payment Options).
8. **Measure activation rate, not satisfaction.** Did they hit the activation point during week 1? That is the only success metric for onboarding.
9. **Re-test annually.** Move one more axis to the right.

## Notes / Limits
- **More steps in onboarding ≠ better onboarding.** Overwhelming new customers contradicts [[Hormozi-Value-Per-Second|Value Per Second]]. The skeleton above is intentionally short. Add a step only when measured activation lifts.
- **1-on-1 does not always scale linearly.** At very high volume, "1-on-many small group" (Skool example: 1-on-6 onboarding to intentionally connect members) outperforms 1-on-1 because it does double duty — onboards + community-links. See [[Hormozi-Community-Linking|Community Linking]].
- **Paid onboarding has acquisition friction.** It will reduce top-of-funnel conversions — sometimes meaningfully. Net LTV usually wins; verify with your own numbers before defaulting.
- **The "tell them when you'll speak next" promise is a real commitment.** Make it only if you can keep it. Broken cadence is more damaging to retention than no cadence.
