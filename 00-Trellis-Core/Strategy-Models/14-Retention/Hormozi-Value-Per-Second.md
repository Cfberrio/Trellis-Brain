# Hormozi — Value Per Second

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Retention — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Cross-cutting design principle that governs every other lever in the Retention canon. States the dominant cause of churn (overwhelm) and the inverted design rule operators should use when building product, onboarding, and ongoing communications. Sits one layer above the nine-lever Churn Checklist because each lever should be evaluated against this principle before shipping.

## Core Idea
> "Think value per second, not seconds of value."
>
> "Less but better is better than more and decent."
>
> **Overwhelm is the #1 reason for churn.**
>
> Retention comes down to making sure customers **CONSUME** the value, not to overwhelming them with it.

The unit of analysis is the customer's clock, not the operator's catalogue. A customer who receives ten things and consumes one is less retained than a customer who receives one thing and consumes it. Operators default to addition (more features, more content, more calls); customers default to abandonment when faced with too much. The retention move is **deletion**.

## Framework / Structure

### The principle stated three ways
1. **Value per second > seconds of value.** Density beats duration. A 20-minute call that delivers one decision beats a 60-minute call that delivers four.
2. **Less but better > more and decent.** Two excellent core deliverables beat ten passable ones. Trim until the operator can defend each remaining piece on its own.
3. **Consumption > provision.** Whatever fraction of provided value the customer actually consumes is the only fraction that retains them. Maximize consumption, not provision.

### Why overwhelm causes churn
- Most customers do not finish what they start. They start YouTube videos and stop. They start courses and stop. They set goals and abandon them. This is baseline customer behavior, not a deficiency.
- Adding more options multiplies the cognitive cost of starting. When the cost of starting exceeds the perceived next-step payoff, the customer disengages.
- Disengagement is the leading indicator of cancellation (see "Usage Churn" in [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]]).
- Therefore: every additional component the operator adds raises the churn floor unless it directly accelerates consumption of the core 2–3.

### Worked case: Hormozi's newsletter friend ($500K/mo)
- **Lowest churn period:** one Q&A call per month, stayed on until every question answered + one long physical newsletter per month. Two deliverables.
- **Operator addition #1:** call cadence raised from monthly to weekly. Churn rose.
- **Operator addition #2:** layered "all these other things." Churn rose with each.
- **Lesson stated by the operator:** "Everything I added increased churn."

### The deletion discipline
- Decide on the **core 2–3 things** the product delivers.
- Make those 2–3 the best in category.
- Cut everything else.
- When customers stop consuming, **contact them more, not less**. Reduce their friction to consume; do not add more options.
- Each successive product version should be **shorter and simpler**. Hormozi: "Gym Launch is on its 10th version. Each version has gotten shorter and simpler because the #1 reason for churn is overwhelm."

### The reduction levers
When designing or pruning a product / membership / community for retention, reduce:
- **Effort** required to consume the core value.
- **Sacrifice** the customer makes to consume (time, attention, calendar overhead).
- **Optionality** at any decision point (one path beats three).
- **Surface area** (number of features, number of channels, number of meetings).

## Strategic Implications
- **Product additions should be defended against churn cost, not just feature value.** Default product-management instinct ("ship more") is anti-retention. Each new feature must demonstrably accelerate consumption of the core 2–3 or it raises churn.
- **Overwhelm is invisible in the metrics until it surfaces as cancellation.** Usage decline precedes cancellation by weeks; tracking consumption (see [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]]) is the only way to see overwhelm before it becomes churn.
- **Versioning is a retention activity.** Operators who never re-prune their product accumulate a year-over-year overwhelm tax. Plan a deletion pass at every major release.
- **Communication frequency increases when consumption decreases.** This inverts the operator's instinct ("they aren't engaging — back off"). The retention move is the opposite — reach out more, reduce friction more, until consumption resumes.
- **Pricing model must match consumption shape.** A heavy one-time-value product priced as a recurring stream creates ongoing overwhelm by definition. Use Big-Head-Long-Tail ([[Hormozi-Annual-Payment-Options|Annual Payment Options]]) when the value is one-time-heavy.

## Practical Use
1. **Audit current deliverables.** List every promise and every channel. Rank by consumption rate.
2. **Identify the core 2–3.** The deliverables your top 20% (your activation cohort — see [[Hormozi-Activation-Points|Activation Points]]) actually use most.
3. **Cut or hide the rest.** Anything outside the core 2–3 either gets deleted, hidden behind a level-up unlock ([[Hormozi-Activation-Incentives|Activation Incentives]]), or moved to optional self-serve.
4. **Re-write onboarding** to drive customers to the core 2–3 only ([[Hormozi-Customer-Onboarding|Customer Onboarding]]).
5. **Set a reduction pass on the release cadence.** Every major release includes a list of things removed, not only added.
6. **Plot consumption per customer.** When a customer's consumption drops, the operator's outreach goes up — not down.
7. **Re-test pricing model.** If recurring revenue is being charged on a one-time-value product, restructure.

## Notes / Limits
- **"Less is more" can be misused as an excuse to under-deliver.** The principle is "less but better," not "less and the same." The 2–3 deliverables that remain must be excellent. If the deletion discipline degrades quality on the core, retention falls regardless.
- **Some customers want depth.** Wealthier customers especially want less hand-holding (see [[Hormozi-Customer-Survey-ACA|Customer Survey & ACA]] — "the wealthier the customers, the less hand-holding they typically want"). The deletion discipline tends to align with high-end customer preference.
- **Deletion is operationally hard.** Internal champions of removed features push back; customers who used the removed feature complain. The operator needs to weigh the visible cost (complaints) against the invisible benefit (lower churn from new and existing customers).
