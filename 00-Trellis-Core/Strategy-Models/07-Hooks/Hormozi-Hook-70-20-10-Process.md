# Hormozi — Hook 70-20-10 Process

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Hooks — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The operating process for generating, testing, and iterating hooks at scale. Adapts Google's 70-20-10 innovation rule (Larry Page / Sergey Brin) into a hook-creation discipline: most effort goes to proven winners, some goes to winner-adjacent ideas from other niches, a little goes to high-variance new bets. Pairs the allocation rule with concrete recording ratios per asset type and a "let the data teach you" feedback loop. This is the system that turns the [[Hormozi-Hook-Definition|hook]] thesis from theory into a repeatable production practice.

## Core Idea
> "Most effort goes to what's already working well, some to new ideas that seem promising, and a little bit to trying totally new things."
>
> "Let the data do the teaching."

Hook quality is not produced by inspiration. It is produced by a portfolio: a large bucket of "more of what already works", a medium bucket of "winner-adjacent" experiments modeled on outliers from other niches, and a small bucket of high-variance new bets. Survivors from the small bucket migrate into the large bucket. Failures are documented and not repeated. Over time, the portfolio compounds — the operator's own swipe file becomes the highest-ROI hook source they have.

## Framework / Structure

### The 70-20-10 allocation

#### 70% — Core Business: more of what works
The largest bucket of effort goes to hooks already proven — either in the operator's own past content/ads, or in publicly visible winners from other operators. Stabilizes baseline performance. Removes dependence on whether a specific experiment "pops off."

**Operating mechanism — the swipe-file spreadsheet.** Hormozi's documented method:
- Build one spreadsheet.
- One tab per platform the operator advertises on.
- Columns: **name** (or short ID), **hook** (the actual opening line), **views** (or other performance metric), **link** (back to the asset).
- Every time content is being made, the operator opens the file and reviews their best hooks of all time before writing new hooks.

The best-performing posts on one platform (e.g., X) feed back as hooks for other platforms (e.g., Shorts captions). The 70% bucket is internally cross-platform.

#### 20% — Emerging Business: winner-adjacent
Promising but less-proven ideas. Conceptually: *"if I see something that works in another niche, I may try and model the concept."*

**Operating mechanism — the outlier deck.**
- Use third-party software tools that surface outlier-performing content by platform (Hormozi declines to endorse any specific one — multiple work). For operators who can't or won't pay, search the platform directly for the topic and isolate the highest-performing content manually.
- Paste each outlier into a slide deck: screenshot of the outlier post + a few notes on how the concept could be repackaged or reused for the operator's niche.
- Pull from this deck when generating winner-adjacent variants.

The 20% bucket is the operator's pipeline of "I haven't proven this yet but the pattern is winning somewhere."

#### 10% — Big New Ideas: high-variance bets
The smallest bucket. Genuinely new visual formats, hooks, or concepts. Hormozi's worked example: *"like me catching a banana in this ad."* High failure rate is expected and acceptable.

**Promotion / demotion logic:**
- Wins migrate down into the 70% bucket — they become a mainstay until something tops them.
- Failures get **documented** in a "do-not-repeat" log so the operator doesn't accidentally re-run a losing experiment.

This bucket is what keeps the portfolio from staling out. Without it, the 70% bucket eventually plateaus.

### Recording ratios — how big-data sets translate into produced assets
Once the operator has the swipe file + outlier deck + new-bets list, they have more hook ideas than content to put them on. The hooks dictate the content, not the other way around. Hormozi's documented production ratios:

| Asset type | Hooks-to-asset ratio |
|---|---|
| **Ads** | **10 hooks recorded per 1 piece of ad content.** Yes, 10×. |
| **Short content** | Every winning tweet on every other platform → reused as a Short, then expand on the hook in the Short's body. |
| **Long content (long-form video)** | Record **3–4 hook variants** per video; team reviews in post and selects the strongest. |
| **Email** | **Active split tests on every campaign sent.** Winners documented in a separate file (the email swipe file). |

These ratios are not aspirational — they are the documented operating norm.

### The data-teaches feedback loop
For operators with no past content (no swipe file yet):
1. Make a bunch of content with no idea what's going to work.
2. Look at what actually performed.
3. Apply 70-20-10 going forward.

The promise: *"no matter how bad your content is, some of it will be in the top 10% of that content."* Look at the hooks for the top 10%. Make more like that. Look at the bottom 10%. Make less like that. Repeat indefinitely. This is how an operator without a teacher gets better — the data is the teacher.

### The operating checklist (from p. 22 body / p. 25 PDF)

**First time through:**
- Look at top-performing ads or content of other people.
- Isolate the hooks.
- Write down your favorite 50.
- Use all of them in ads and content.

**Every time after:**
- Look at your top 10% performers (top 5 or so).
- Make 70% of your next batch of ads using those top 5 hooks.
- Make 20% doing permutations of those hooks (visuals and words — see [[Hormozi-Hook-Types-Palette|Types Palette]] for variant formats).
- Make 10% completely different hooks (model the "first time through" loop again).

**Every hook published must contain:**
- A call out (catches attention).
- An implication of value if consumed.

## Strategic Implications

- **Hook production is a portfolio problem, not a creativity problem.** The 70-20-10 split is what makes hook output stable, scalable, and improving over time without depending on whether the operator "feels inspired" in any given week. Treating hooks as inspiration-driven is the failure mode this process is designed to prevent.
- **The swipe file is a compounding asset.** Every winning hook ever written gets logged. Future hook generation starts from the file, not from a blank page. After a year of disciplined logging, the operator's own file becomes the highest-leverage hook source they have — better than any external resource, because it's calibrated to their audience.
- **Outliers from other niches are the cheapest source of fresh winners.** The 20% bucket exploits the fact that hooks generalize across niches more than operators expect. A hook that wins for fitness can be repackaged for finance. The slide-deck-of-outliers method is the documented way to industrialize this borrowing.
- **The 10:1 hooks-to-ad ratio is the budget reality.** Operators who record one hook per ad and wonder why ads don't work are off by an order of magnitude. The math behind any successful Hormozi-style ad campaign assumes most hooks lose; the winners pay for the losers. Under-recording hooks is the most common cause of "ads don't work for us" diagnoses.
- **Document failures, not just wins.** The "do-not-repeat" log on the 10% bucket is the cheapest insurance against re-running losing experiments six months later when the original lesson has been forgotten. Most operators only log wins; the asymmetric value is in logging losses.
- **For new operators, the on-ramp is volume + observation.** No swipe file means no 70% bucket. The bridge is "make a bunch with no idea, then read the data." This is structurally different from the steady-state process — for a new operator, all 100% of effort is essentially the 10% bucket, and the swipe file is being built in real time.

## Practical Use

**Standing infrastructure (set up once):**
1. **Hook swipe file (spreadsheet).** One tab per platform. Columns: name, hook, views/performance metric, link. Update after every campaign / content batch.
2. **Outlier deck (slides).** Screenshots of outlier posts from other niches + repackaging notes. Add new outliers weekly.
3. **Do-not-repeat log.** Failed 10% experiments + brief diagnosis. Reviewed before launching new big-bet hooks.
4. **Email-winners file.** Separate from the main swipe file (different cadence, different metric — opens vs. views).

**Per-asset workflow:**
1. Open the swipe file. Pick the top 5 hooks for the relevant platform.
2. Decide the 70/20/10 split for this batch.
3. **70% bucket** — write/record hooks using the top 5 directly.
4. **20% bucket** — write/record permutations and winner-adjacent hooks (use the [[Hormozi-Hook-Types-Palette|Types Palette]] to translate winners across formats; pull from the outlier deck).
5. **10% bucket** — write/record genuinely new hooks (new visual formats, new angles).
6. Apply the recording ratios: 10 hooks per ad; 3–4 hooks per long video; reuse winning Twitter posts as Shorts hooks; split-test every email.
7. Publish. Measure.
8. **After publishing:** Sort by performance. Top 10% → swipe file. Bottom 10% → adjust pattern away. Failed 10%-bucket experiments → do-not-repeat log. Repeat.

**For an operator starting from zero:**
- Skip the swipe-file step until you have produced content. Make a high volume of varied hooks. After ~30–50 pieces, sort by performance and the swipe file builds itself.
- Until then, use the 50-hooks-from-other-people's-winners drill (from the "first time through" checklist) as a substitute 70% bucket.
