# Hormozi — Hook Types Palette

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Hooks — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Construction palette for writing hooks. Lists the verbal hook formats Hormozi has documented as winners across his ads, content, IG, email, and Twitter, plus the nonverbal category (visuals + sounds). Used as a generation toolkit when writing new hooks or producing variants of a known winner — pick a format, fill it with the asset's call out and value promise, repeat across formats to multiply variants.

## Core Idea
> "Anything that gets someone to notice your ad is a hook." — Alex Hormozi

A hook is not a single linguistic form. It is a *function* — capture attention + imply value — that can be expressed in many surface forms. Cataloguing those surface forms turns hook writing from a blank-page problem into a fill-in-the-blank problem.

The palette has two branches:
- **Verbal hooks** — words that get attention.
- **Nonverbal hooks** — noises and visuals that get attention. Used together with verbal hooks where the platform allows.

Each format below is paired with at least one example Hormozi documents as an actual winner from his content or ads.

## Framework / Structure

### Verbal hook formats (8)

#### 1. Labels
Words the avatar identifies with. The call out is the audience's name in the cocktail-party sense.
- *"Local business owners, I have a gift for you."*
- *"Chiropractors, I have a gift for you."*

#### 2. Questions
Two sub-types:

**Yes-questions** — designed to elicit an internal "yes" that pulls the prospect deeper.
- *"Would you pay $1,000 dollars to have the business of your dreams in 30 days?"*

**Open questions** — frame a choice or comparison the prospect must resolve.
- *"Which would you rather be?"*

#### 3. Conditionals
Scenario or condition → result, lesson, or command.
- *"If you're working all the time and your business isn't growing, you're working on the wrong sh*t."*
- *"If you're a chiropractor, this video will get you more leads."*

#### 4. Commands
Direct imperatives — tell the audience what to do, conditioned on identity.
- *"Read this if you're tired of being broke."*
- *"Read this if you want to win."*
- *"Watch this if you want to get more patients."*

#### 5. Statements
Declarative claims that imply value if consumed.
- *"The smartest thing you can do today…"*
- *"How to get ahead of 99% of people."*
- *"The top 1% of chiropractor clinics follow these rules…"*

#### 6. Lists / Steps
Numbered promise — quantified, finite, scannable.
- *"In this video I'm going to talk to you about the 28 ways to stay poor."*
- *"11 ways Chiropractors get more patients without paid ads."*

#### 7. Narratives
Stories and anecdotes — pattern-break by entering mid-scene.
- *"One day I was in the back and this old lady comes in and she was piss angry."*
- *"All of a sudden, my phone starts ringing off the hook…"*

#### 8. Exclamations / Provocative Statements
Strong emotion (surprise, frustration, anticipation) in the first beat — orients the prospect by emotional contagion before the value promise lands.
- *"Ahhhhh… This is the blueprint to becoming a millionaire and I'm going to walk you through the levels."*

### Nonverbal hook category
Sounds and visuals that capture attention without (or before) words.

- **Sound pattern-breaks** — at the cocktail party, both a dropped tray of dishes and the *cling-cling-cling* of a knife on a champagne flute orient the room. One signals embarrassing disaster, the other signals important news. Both work; both create the "what happens next?" reflex.
- **Visual pattern-breaks** — Hormozi's "catching a banana" ad opener is the worked example. Unexpected motion or imagery in the first frame holds attention long enough for the verbal hook to land.
- **Combined verbal + nonverbal** — when the platform allows (video, especially short-form), the strongest hooks pair a nonverbal pattern-break with a verbal call out + value promise. The visual buys the seconds the words need.

## Strategic Implications

- **Hook-writing is combinatorial, not creative.** With 8 verbal formats × N call-outs (audience labels) × M value promises, even a small palette generates dozens of testable variants for any single asset. The "I'm out of hook ideas" problem is almost always a process problem, not an inspiration problem.
- **Each format triggers attention through a different mechanism.** Labels trigger identity. Questions trigger curiosity / answer-seeking. Conditionals trigger self-recognition ("that's me"). Commands trigger compliance reflex. Statements trigger credibility-checking. Lists trigger completion drive. Narratives trigger story-pull. Exclamations trigger emotional contagion. A diversified hook portfolio attacks attention through multiple channels — a portfolio of one format saturates fast.
- **Translate one winner into all 8 formats to find the best vehicle.** Hormozi's checklist demonstrates this directly: the same chiropractor pitch is rewritten across conditional, label, question, command, statement, list, and story formats. The underlying value promise is identical; the surface form is what gets tested. This is the cheapest variant-generation trick available.
- **Nonverbal beats verbal in feed-scroll formats.** On platforms where the user is scrolling at speed (IG Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts), the visual/audio pattern-break in frame one is what stops the scroll. The verbal hook then converts the stop into a watch.
- **Format-fit varies by channel.** Labels and conditionals do disproportionately well in ads (audience-targeting baked into the call out). Narratives dominate IG and YouTube long-form. Provocative statements dominate Twitter. Email subject lines lean on questions, statements, and conditionals. Use the [[Hormozi-Hook-Library|Hook Library]] to confirm which formats Hormozi's actual top performers favor by channel.

## Practical Use

**When writing hooks for a new asset:**
1. Define the asset's audience (the call out target) and value promise (what the audience gets by consuming) in one sentence each.
2. Open the palette. Write at least one variant in each of the 8 verbal formats. Don't optimize at this stage — produce volume.
3. If the asset is video or has a visual surface, pair the strongest 2–3 verbal variants with a nonverbal pattern-break (motion, prop, sound) for the first frame.
4. Test using the recording ratios in [[Hormozi-Hook-70-20-10-Process|Hormozi — Hook 70-20-10 Process]] (10:1 for ads; 3–4 for long content; split-test for email; reuse winning Twitter posts as Shorts hooks).

**When iterating on a known winner:**
1. Take the winning hook. Identify which format it lives in.
2. Translate it into the other 7 formats to generate "winner-adjacent" variants.
3. Push these into the 20% emerging bucket of the [[Hormozi-Hook-70-20-10-Process|70-20-10 process]].

**Worked single-pitch example (from Hormozi's checklist, p. 22–23 body):**
| Format | Variant for "chiropractor leads" |

| Conditional | If you're a chiropractor, this video will get you more leads. |
| Label | Chiropractors, I have a gift for you. |
| Question | Would you rather struggle to get patients for the rest of your life or fix it in 6 weeks? |
| Command | Watch this if you want to get more patients. |
| Statement | The top 1% of chiropractor clinics follow these rules… |
| List / Steps | 11 ways Chiropractors get more patients without paid ads. |
| Narrative | All of a sudden, my phone starts ringing off the hook… |
