# Hormozi — Ad CTA

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|GOATed Ads — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The CTA layer of the [[Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]]. Defines how to construct the 1–3 CTAs per recording session, what makes a CTA "sound" rather than clever, and how to test CTA variants without wasting cycles. Ties the ad's last 3–5 seconds to the offer-side enhancement levers canonical in `01-100M-Offers/`.

## Core Idea
> "Clear > Clever."

The CTA does not need to be inventive. It needs to be **explicit, specific, and demonstrated**. After the hook lands and the meat fulfills the promise, the prospect has high motivation but a tiny window to act. The CTA's job is to spend that window correctly: tell them what to do, and **show** what happens when they do it.

> "A sound CTA hasn't ever broken a campaign, but no CTA has."

CTAs are the lowest-attention layer of the assembly process (~0% of prep time, ~5 minutes of recording for several variants). Their leverage is real but bounded — once one works, stick with it.

## Framework / Structure

### Rule 1 — Clear > Clever
Spell out the action. "Click this button." "Call this number." "Reply with YES." "Go to this website." "Scan this QR code." Most ads still skip this; the audience can only do what you tell them to do.

### Rule 2 — Show + Tell (not just tell)
Demonstrate what the next step looks like. Show the button being clicked. Show the form being filled out. Show the page they'll land on. Walk through the next steps and click submit on camera. The result: when the prospect clicks and gets exactly what was previewed, they follow through more often. **Congruence between the ad and the post-click experience is the lever.** This is "obvious and basic, but almost no one does it."

### Rule 3 — The 5-element CTA structure
A complete CTA shows or tells:

1. **What to do** — "Take advantage of this great offer by..."
2. **How to do it** — "...tapping the button on the bottom of your screen..."
3. **When to do it** — "...before it expires..."
4. **What they get for doing it** — "...and you'll get $1,000 of free stuff."
5. **What happens next** *(optional)* — "Delivered straight to your inbox."

Element #5 is more important for lead magnets and multi-step sales processes than for direct-to-purchase CTAs.

### Rule 4 — Make 1–3 CTAs per session
- 1 CTA is enough to ship.
- 2–3 enables a clean A/B test.
- More than 3 wastes the recording day; CTA variance is not the campaign's largest lever.

### Rule 5 — Minimum-viable CTA test
- Run **3 identical hook + meat combinations** to the **same audience**, varying only the CTA.
- Example (a 14-day free trial offer):
  1. "Start free on the next page."
  2. "Grab your 14-day free trial on the next page."
  3. "Get started on the next page free."
- Once you have one CTA that follows the structure and works, **stick with it.** Do not optimize past sound.

### Rule 6 — Use offer-level enhancement levers on the CTA
The CTA is one of the surfaces where the enhancement levers from `01-100M-Offers/` apply. They get more people to act:

- [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Urgency|Urgency]] — "...before it expires." Deadline language.
- [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Scarcity|Scarcity]] — "X spots left." Supply cap language.
- [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Guarantees|Guarantees]] — risk-reversal language attached to the action.
- [[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Bonuses|Bonuses]] — "$1,000 of free stuff" in element #4.

These are not new mechanics for the CTA — they are the same offer-level levers, applied to the moment of action. Hormozi's framing: they "apply here, and everywhere else you tell your audience to do something."

## Strategic Implications

- **Most CTA failure is omission, not design.** Operators who do not explicitly tell prospects what to do see significantly fewer prospects do it. The fix is not cleverness; it is *putting the instruction in the ad at all*.
- **Show + tell is asymmetric leverage.** The cost is one extra clip — the button being clicked, the page loading, the form being filled. The benefit is post-click follow-through, which compounds across every winning ad. Most operators skip it; this is one of the cheapest creative wins available.
- **CTA optimization caps fast.** A "sound" CTA performs near the ceiling of what CTAs alone can do for a campaign. Time spent optimizing past sound is time not spent expanding hooks — and hooks have far higher leverage. The 80% / 20% / ~0% prep ratio (see [[Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]]) reflects this asymmetry.
- **The enhancement levers belong on every action moment, not just the offer page.** Urgency, scarcity, guarantees, and bonuses applied to the ad CTA increase action rates upstream of the landing page. They are not redundant with offer-page versions — they compound.
- **Element #5 is for funnels with friction.** Direct-to-purchase CTAs survive without "what happens next." Multi-step funnels (lead magnet → email sequence → sales call → close) need element #5 to keep the prospect oriented. Skipping it on those funnels increases drop-off at every step.
- **Consistency between ad CTA and post-click reality is a trust signal.** "Click this button to get [X]" → button click → exactly [X] appears = the prospect has been told the truth once already and is more likely to trust the next instruction. Mismatch = the prospect's trust is partially burned before the offer page even loads.

## Practical Use

Construct the 1–3 CTAs for the next recording session like this:

1. **Pick the action.** Buy / book / subscribe / start trial / download / call / reply / scan. One action per CTA.
2. **Draft the 5 elements.**
   - What to do → name the action.
   - How to do it → name the mechanism (tap, click, scan, reply, call).
   - When to do it → urgency hook (deadline, limit, "today only" — only if true).
   - What they get → the deliverable. Quantify if possible.
   - What happens next → for lead magnet / multi-step only. Skip for direct-purchase.
3. **Decide on enhancement levers.** Layer urgency / scarcity / guarantees / bonuses where they are honest. If the deadline is real, include it. If the spot count is real, include it. **Do not fake any lever** — see Notes / Limits.
4. **Record show + tell.** Film the prospect's next 5 seconds: cursor on button, page loading, form fields visible, submit click. Voice-over the 5 elements over the demo.
5. **Produce 1–3 variants** if you intend to test. Vary only the CTA wording; keep show + tell consistent.
6. **Test by triplet.** Same hook, same meat, same audience, 3 CTAs. Pick the winner. Stick with it.
7. **Reuse.** Once a CTA wins, attach it to all future hook × meat combinations until performance decays. Replace late, not eagerly — CTA is the slowest-rotating layer of the assembly.

## Notes / Limits

- **Fake urgency / scarcity destroys trust faster than it earns clicks.** "Only 3 spots left" repeated weekly with no enforcement teaches the audience the ad is lying. Use levers that are operationally true. The canonical lever notes in `01-100M-Offers/` cover the honesty rule in detail.
- **CTA cannot rescue a weak hook + meat.** Prospects who don't watch past the hook never see the CTA. Optimizing CTA before the hook converts is solving the wrong problem.
- **Element #4 (what they get) must match the offer.** Inflated "$1,000 of free stuff" claims that don't show up post-click create the worst kind of mismatch — high click-through, zero conversion, burned trust. Element #4 is a promise; the offer page must keep it.
- **One CTA per ad.** Stacking multiple actions in a single CTA dilutes intent. If the funnel needs both a primary and a fallback, run two ad variants, not two CTAs in one ad.
- **CTA quality is bounded.** "Stick with what works" is not laziness — it is the recognition that CTA leverage caps. The operator's marginal hour is better spent on a new hook than on a cleverer CTA.
