# Hormozi — Ad Assembly Process

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|GOATed Ads — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Umbrella mechanism of the *GOATed Ads* playbook. Defines how to mass-produce winning ads as an assembly line of three interchangeable parts (hooks, meats, CTAs) recorded on a fixed weekly cadence. Converts ad creation from a per-asset craft into a combinatorial output system that produces 150–750 ads per recording session, surfaces winners via volume, and scales past the ad-quality wall described in [[Hormozi-Ad-Awareness-Continuum|Ad Awareness Continuum]].

## Core Idea
> "I wasn't really creating ads at all. I was assembling them."

A winning ad is the product of three independent components combined combinatorially:

**ad = hook × meat × CTA**

Build each component in batches in its own session, then combine. This is the operational answer to the wall every paid-ads operator hits: cost-per-acquisition rises not because the market is saturated, but because the **creative is saturated**. The fix is volume × quality of the creative pipeline, not new audiences or higher bids.

The thesis Hormozi forces on the portfolio operator who was making "five ads a month, sometimes a few more": go from 5 to 600 ads per month by chunking production. Not by working harder per ad — by changing the unit of production.

## Framework / Structure

### The combinatorial math
- **50 hooks** × **3–5 meats** × **1–3 CTAs** = **150 to 750 ads per weekly recording session**.
- Hook variation rotates fastest (most viewers see the first 1–3 seconds; hooks burn out fast).
- Meat rotates slower (fewer viewers reach the body; the same meat survives more impressions).
- CTAs rotate slowest (a sound CTA is rarely the campaign-breaker; once one works, stick with it).

### Prep ratio (per recording session)
- **80%** of prep time → hooks. If the hook fails, nothing else matters.
- **20%** of prep time → meats.
- **~0%** of prep time → CTAs (a few minutes; they take the least effort).

### Time cost of recording
- Hooks: ~1–2 hours of recording.
- Meats: ~1 hour for 3–5 meats.
- CTAs: ~5 minutes for a few CTAs.
- The rest of the work — and the leverage — is in **prep**, not in shooting. "Six-pack-abs are made in the kitchen, not in the gym."

### Cadence
- **One dedicated day per week** for advertising. Hormozi uses Friday in the source.
- Weekly output target: 150–750 finished ads, all combinations of that week's prepared parts.
- Rationale for weekly: hook burnout is fast; the bottleneck is fresh top-of-funnel creative.

### The three components (canonical notes)
- **Hooks** → see [[Hormozi-Hook-By-Awareness|Hook By Awareness]] — the 5 sourcing places + the 5 awareness-mapped hook styles.
- **Meats** → see [[Hormozi-Ad-Meat-Formats|Ad Meat Formats]] — the 5 reusable formats.
- **CTAs** → see [[Hormozi-Ad-CTA|Ad CTA]] — Clear > Clever, show + tell, 5-element CTA structure.

### Defense side benefit
A combinatorial pipeline produces hundreds of variants of the same underlying winner. Competitors scraping ad libraries cannot identify which exact ad performs because the top performer is buried in a cloud of near-identical variants. **Volume is offense and defense at once.**

## Strategic Implications

- **The unit of production is the part, not the ad.** Operators who treat ads as singular creative artifacts cap themselves at single-digit-ads-per-month and burn out the few hooks they have. Treating hook/meat/CTA as separately-batched factors raises output by 10–100×.
- **More winners come from more attempts, not from more cleverness.** Winners surface from volume. Fewer than ~50 hooks per session does not generate enough variance to find the next winner; this is why "five ads a month" was the bottleneck for the portfolio company in the source.
- **Reuse > novelty.** Once you have a winner, the work is reusing its hook, its meat, or its CTA across new combinations — not inventing the next thing from scratch. New customers enter the market every day; the same ad is "the first time they see it."
- **The 80% on hooks is a forcing function, not a preference.** It forces the operator to write expansion hooks for cold audiences whenever existing hooks cap, which is the only way to convert progressively colder audiences profitably (see [[Hormozi-Ad-Awareness-Continuum|Awareness Continuum]]).
- **Camouflage is structural.** A weekly drop of 150–750 variants makes the top performer invisible to competitors auditing your ad library. This is a side-effect, but a useful one — the same volume that finds winners also hides them.
- **This sits on $100M Leads, not next to it.** Hormozi explicitly says GOATed Ads is *on top of* the paid-ads chapters in *$100M Leads*, not instead of them. The targeting / channel / bidding mechanics are upstream; this playbook is the creative-production layer.
- **Kaleidoscope is downstream.** Hormozi teases a separate playbook (*Kaleidoscope Ads*) as the mechanism that takes a confirmed winner to maximum scale. That mechanism is **not** in this book. Build the winner pipeline first; the kaleidoscope step requires a winner to feed it.

## Practical Use

Operator weekly checklist (synthesized from the playbook + the cheat sheet on p. 26):

**Step 1 — Pick your awareness target.** Decide which level(s) of [[Hormozi-Ad-Awareness-Continuum|Ad Awareness Continuum]] you are writing for this week. Most ads should bias broader than your last winner.

**Step 2 — Write 50 hooks** (80% of prep time). Sources, in priority order (see [[Hormozi-Hook-By-Awareness|Hook By Awareness]]):
1. Your past winning hooks.
2. Your own high-performing organic content (longs, shorts, tweets, emails).
3. Other people's high-performing ads.
4. Other people's high-performing organic content.
5. Platform ad libraries (last resort — too noisy).

Spread the 50 across awareness buckets; bias broader than your existing winners if you've capped current spend.

**Step 3 — Write 3–5 meats** (20% of prep time). Pick from the five formats: Demonstration, Testimonial, Education, Story, Faceless. See [[Hormozi-Ad-Meat-Formats|Ad Meat Formats]]. Mix and match.

**Step 4 — Write 1–3 CTAs** (~0% of prep time). Show + tell. Cover what / how / when / what-they-get / what-happens-next. See [[Hormozi-Ad-CTA|Ad CTA]].

**Step 5 — Record.** Hooks first (1–2 hrs), meats next (~1 hr), CTAs last (~5 min). Combine combinatorially in post — every hook attaches to every meat attaches to every CTA.

**Step 6 — Ship the batch.** 150–750 ads.

**Step 7 — Find next week's winner.** Repeat. When a new top performer emerges, treat *its* hook as a candidate source for next week's 50 hooks (Source #1).

**Step 8 — Double down.** When ads wildly outperform the rest, reuse those hooks across more combinations rather than chasing novelty.

## Notes / Limits

- **Volume without quality is noise.** The 150–750 number is an output target, not a goal. The hook-sourcing discipline (writing from past winners + scanning high-performing content) is what keeps the variance productive instead of random.
- **Recording time is the smallest line item.** If "I don't have time to record" is the bottleneck, the diagnosis is wrong — the bottleneck is the prep document. Hormozi is explicit: 90% of advertising work is prep.
- **Weekly is the cadence floor.** Less frequent than weekly and the hook library staleness compounds. More frequent than weekly typically does not justify the recording overhead unless the operator already has multiple presenters / formats running in parallel.
- **First-time advertisers skip Source #1.** "Look at past winners" presumes you have past winners. New advertisers lean on Sources #2–#5.
- **This pipeline does not replace the offer or the channel.** A bad offer with a great hook converts attention into nothing. The Assembly Process scales the *creative*; the offer must already be sound (see `01-100M-Offers/`).
