# Hormozi — Ad Awareness Continuum

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|GOATed Ads — Book Home]]

## Purpose
The audience model behind GOATed Ads. Adapts Eugene Schwartz's 5-level awareness pyramid into a continuous quality-vs-audience-size curve and uses it to explain the central scaling thesis: ads cap because of **creative quality**, not market saturation. Sets the targeting frame the [[Hormozi-Hook-By-Awareness|Hook By Awareness]] note operationalizes.

## Core Idea
> "You haven't saturated [the platform]. You've gotten all the low hanging fruit. You've hit a wall with your ad quality, not with the market. The better your ads, the bigger the audience they'll convert."

Audiences exist on a spectrum from **warmest + smallest** (already know your product, only need the deal) to **coldest + largest** (don't know they have a problem). Ads start by converting the warm, small slice profitably. As spend scales, you reach colder slices that require progressively better creative to convert at the same CAC.

The wall is not the market. The wall is the operator's current creative capability. **Better ads — and only better ads — buy access to colder, larger audiences.**

## Framework / Structure

### Schwartz's 5 levels of awareness (bottom = warmest, top = coldest)

| Level | Audience state | Hook style typically required |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Most Aware (warmest, smallest) | Knows the product, only needs the deal | Offer-driven |
| 2. Product-Aware | Knows what you sell, unsure it's right for them | Proof-driven |
| 3. Solution-Aware | Knows the result they want, doesn't know your product can deliver it | Promise-driven |
| 4. Problem-Aware | Senses a problem, doesn't know solutions exist | Pain-driven |
| 5. Completely Unaware (coldest, largest) | Doesn't know they have a problem | Curiosity-driven |

Hook-style operationalization lives in [[Hormozi-Hook-By-Awareness|Hook By Awareness]].

### Hormozi's continuous adaptation
Hormozi rejects the discrete-bucket reading of Schwartz: "Labels are clean. Reality is messy. I've never met these people." He uses a **continuous curve**:

- Vertical axis: audience size (low warm → high cold).
- Horizontal axis: creative quality required to convert profitably.
- A given ad converts profitably up to some "altitude" on the curve. Below that altitude, it works. Above, CAC blows up.
- Improving creative quality (stronger hook, better offer, better person delivering) raises the altitude — letting the same offer convert a larger, colder slice.

### The scaling rule
- **The larger the audience reached, the more varied and exceptional the ads must be.**
- Below cap: CAC is sane, repeating winners works.
- At cap: CAC rises sharply at +5–8k/day in spend (Hormozi's example: a portfolio company hit this on Facebook).
- Above cap: only new, broader, higher-quality creative restores profitable scale.

### Wide-vs-narrow hooks across the continuum
- **Most-Aware hooks** (offer-driven) reach a small slice precisely. Do not over-rely on them at scale — they cap fastest.
- **Cold-end hooks** (curiosity, pain, meme-like) reach far larger slices. The wider the hook, the more eyeballs it earns.
- **Pro tip:** memes (or audience-specific subculture memes) reach the widest slice possible. They work like "a moth to a flame."
- **Default bias:** if 90% of your hooks land in Most-Aware, redistribute. When in doubt, go broader. You still catch the warm slice and earn cold attention too.

### Edge case — pre-existing brand awareness collapses the curve
A national brand (e.g., Subway's "$5 Footlong") can run an offer-driven hook to a cold national audience because hundreds of millions in prior brand spend already paid for product- and brand-awareness. Without that prior investment, an offer-driven hook to a cold audience is "the closest thing to burning money on fire."

Implication: an unbranded operator must lead with proof / promise / pain / curiosity hooks, not offers, when reaching out to colder slices.

## Strategic Implications

- **The wall is diagnostic, not terminal.** When CAC blows up at scale, the question is "is the creative quality bottlenecked?" not "is the market saturated?" Almost always the answer is the former.
- **Audience expansion happens through hook expansion, not new channels.** The same channel still works; what changes is *which hook style* the channel must carry. Switching channels before exhausting hook-style range solves the wrong problem.
- **Quality unlocks size.** The Old Spice example: Hormozi was not an Old Spice buyer — the hook was good enough to convert him *and* "70% of the market." That's the structural pattern of an ad without a cap. Aim for that.
- **Creative quality has three load-bearing components:** the hook, the offer, and the person delivering it. Improving any one moves the altitude. Improving all three is how a single ad becomes uncapped.
- **Awareness level dictates hook style, not the other way around.** The mistake is writing a curiosity-driven hook for a Most-Aware audience (wastes their warmth) or an offer-driven hook for a Completely-Unaware audience (asks for a decision they're not ready to make). Match the hook to the slice.
- **Brand spend collapses the curve.** Without prior brand investment, the operator cannot run offer-only hooks at scale. With enough prior brand investment, they can. Treat prior brand-awareness investment as a multiplier on every future ad's reach.

## Practical Use

When CAC rises and existing creative caps:

1. **Diagnose, don't replace.** Confirm the wall is creative-quality, not channel/offer/audience-targeting. Symptom: ads still convert profitably for a small slice but CAC explodes when you scale spend.
2. **Audit hook distribution.** Pull the last batch of 50 hooks. Tag each by awareness level. If >70% sit in Most-Aware or Product-Aware, you are saturating the warm slice.
3. **Write expansion hooks.** Add Solution-Aware (promise), Problem-Aware (pain), and Completely-Unaware (curiosity) hooks until the distribution is broader. Use the awareness-mapped hook styles in [[Hormozi-Hook-By-Awareness|Hook By Awareness]].
4. **Test memes / widest hooks.** A meme hook for the broadest audience (or a subculture-specific meme for a narrower one) buys the most cheap attention and breaks ad blindness.
5. **Hold offer and CTA constant.** Isolate the variable. Same meat, same CTA, new awareness-mapped hooks. If CAC restores, the diagnosis was correct.
6. **If still capped:** review the offer ([[../01-100M-Offers/Hormozi-Grand-Slam-Offer|Grand Slam Offer]]) and the person delivering the ad — the other two components of creative quality.

## Notes / Limits

- **The pyramid is a thinking tool, not a taxonomy.** Hormozi is explicit: real audiences sit on a continuum, not in clean buckets. Use the levels to vary hook style, not to gate which prospect sees which ad.
- **Wider hooks have a tradeoff.** A meme-like hook may pull cold audiences but convert at a lower rate per impression than a precisely-targeted Most-Aware offer. The arithmetic still favors them at scale because the impression count is so much larger.
- **Quality cannot fully replace targeting.** If the channel's targeting is broken (wrong country, wrong age, wrong language), no hook quality saves it. This note assumes targeting is sound — see *$100M Leads* paid-ads chapters for the upstream layer.
- **Brand spend is a real prerequisite for offer-only hooks at scale.** Operators without it should not assume Most-Aware hooks alone will scale.
