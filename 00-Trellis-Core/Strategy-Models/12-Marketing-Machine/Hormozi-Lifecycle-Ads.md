# Hormozi — Lifecycle Ads

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Marketing Machine — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Node 1 of the Marketing Machine. Defines a system for harvesting *moments* from the recorded customer journey — sales calls, support calls, ascension/upsell calls, milestone calls, and physical-delivery reactions — and stitching them into "before / during / after" ads that show, rather than tell, a customer's transformation across time.

## Core Idea
> *"This allowed us to show rather than tell a client's entire journey through time with us. So rather than recall their hard times, you can actually see the person at their hard time and at their victorious after moment."*

A Lifecycle Ad is a temporally composed ad: the *same* customer, captured at three separate stages, edited together. It is the most labor-intensive node in the Marketing Machine, but also the most original-feeling output, because no scripted testimonial can reproduce real footage of the same person at rock-bottom and the same person at victory.

## Framework / Structure

### The three temporal moments
| Moment | Source call type | What to capture |
|---|---|---|
| **Before** | Initial sales call | The pain in the prospect's own words — what they hate doing, what they cannot do, the metric that hurts |
| **During** | 1–2 support / coaching calls | The struggle in motion — confusion, partial wins, the work being done |
| **After** | Upsell, milestone, ascension, or testimonial call | The victory — the metric, the new posture, the language of someone who made it |

The same customer's name must thread all three clips. Hormozi's instruction: marketing searches the calendar by client name, cross-references to the recorded calls, and pulls the three clips. Without the calendar-name discipline, the matching breaks.

### Local-business variant: "Upon Delivery" reaction ads
For businesses with a physical delivery moment, the lifecycle compresses into a single high-leverage shot: the reaction at first sight of the result. Hormozi's examples:
- The customer opening their eyes to a remodeled kitchen.
- The customer stepping on the scale after X days.
- The customer seeing their teeth after veneers.
- The recruiting customer hearing "you got the job."

The reaction itself is the **hook** — first 3–5 seconds of the ad. See [[../07-Hooks/Hormozi-Hook-Definition|Hook Definition]] for the canonical hook definition. From the same source, Hormozi recommends two output formats:
1. **Single-customer ad** — one full reaction edited as a 30–60s ad with context.
2. **Highlight reel** — reactions only, stacked. Hormozi's note: *these crush*.

### Compliance preconditions
The lifecycle node only runs if the legal foundation is in place. Hormozi spells out the gating items:
- All customer contracts include language authorizing recording for compliance, training, *and advertising* purposes.
- Even with the contract, the operator individually asks each featured customer if they are comfortable being used in ads. If they decline, the operator honors it. There are always more willing customers.

## Strategic Implications

- **Lifecycle ads are the highest-fidelity proof a business can show.** A scripted testimonial is one slice; a lifecycle ad is a longitudinal record. Skeptical prospects discount edited single-shot testimonials more than they discount the same person before and after. The format buys trust the others cannot.
- **The lifecycle node is the most demanding to install — and the last to install for a reason.** It requires recorded calls (with consent), call-name discipline across departments, marketing-side searchable storage, and editing horsepower. Hormozi's recommended install order ([[Hormozi-Marketing-Machine-Cadence|Cadence]]) puts Lifecycle ninth out of nine for this reason — high payoff, high friction.
- **"Upon Delivery" gives local businesses an unfair advantage.** Virtual businesses have to manufacture moments; physical-delivery businesses already have them every day. Most local operators leave 100% of these moments uncaptured. The single discipline of pointing a phone at the reaction shot turns ordinary delivery into ad inventory.
- **Storage is the hidden lever.** Auto-delete on call-recording platforms is the single most common reason this node fails to produce. Hormozi names the fix explicitly: turn off auto-delete, buy more storage. Cheap insurance against losing the only footage that exists of a customer at their rock-bottom moment.
- **The reaction is the hook.** The lifecycle ad's opening 3–5 seconds is, by construction, a real reaction. That is the canonical hook unit. See [[../07-Hooks/Hormozi-Hook-Types-Palette|Hook Types Palette]] for how to select which reaction line to lead with when stitching multiple options.

## Practical Use

Lifecycle install checklist (lifted from p. 8, ordered by department):

1. **Legal:** add compliance-and-permission language to all customer contracts. Authorize use of recordings for *advertising* (not just training and compliance).
2. **IT:** record all customer-facing calls — sales, onboarding, support, ascension, milestone calls — and turn off auto-delete. Buy extra storage.
3. **All departments:** create calendar events for every customer call. Put the client's name in the event title. Without this, marketing cannot find the clips later.
4. **Sales:** send the happy-customer call list to marketing every week.
5. **Marketing search:** look up the calendar by client name. Cross-reference to recorded calls. Match names to dates.
6. **Marketing edit (per customer):**
   - Clip the **before** moment from the initial sales call.
   - Clip a **during** moment from 1–2 support calls.
   - Clip the **after** moment from an upsell, milestone, or testimonial call.
7. **Marketing label:** create a friendly, searchable labeling system for these clips so any team member can pull them on demand.
8. **Marketing run:** edit per the canonical [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]] and ship to ad accounts.

For "Upon Delivery" local-business operators, the lifecycle compresses to:

1. Pre-stage the camera at the delivery moment (kitchen reveal, scale, mirror, "you got the job" call).
2. Capture the reaction with audio. One take.
3. Cut the reaction as a 3–5 sec hook.
4. Edit a single-customer ad and add the customer's reactions to the rolling highlight-reel ad.

## Notes / Limits

- **Auto-delete is the silent killer.** Most call-recording platforms purge after 30–90 days by default. The lifecycle node depends on being able to pull a sales call from 6+ months ago when the customer hits their milestone. Storage policy must extend at minimum the length of the customer's typical journey.
- **Calendar discipline drives the whole node.** If sales does not put the client's name in calendar event titles, marketing cannot find the calls. This is a small, free, one-meeting fix that operators routinely fail to make.
- **One declined customer is not a problem.** Hormozi's framing: there are "plenty more successful clients" who are happy to share. Build the workflow assuming a healthy decline rate; do not pressure customers who say no.
- **Quality of "during" footage is the bottleneck.** Sales-call footage and milestone-call footage tend to be expressive. Mid-journey support calls are often dry — you may need to lean on a coaching session or check-in instead. Adapt without making the content fake.
- **"Upon Delivery" requires pre-staging.** The reaction shot is one-take. If the camera is not already running when the customer opens their eyes / steps on the scale / sees the result, the moment is gone. Build the trigger into the delivery SOP.
- **Storage is not the bottleneck people think it is.** Cloud storage cost for a year of calls is trivial vs. the value of a single working lifecycle ad. Do not let IT optimization decisions strand the rest of the system.
