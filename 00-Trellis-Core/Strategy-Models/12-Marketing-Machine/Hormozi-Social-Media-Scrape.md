# Hormozi — Social Media Scrape

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Marketing Machine — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Node 2 of the Marketing Machine. Defines a system for harvesting customer-posted and operator-posted social-media content — images, videos, stories, tagged posts — and converting the organically-validated winners into paid creative. The node's value rests on a single empirical claim: **organic performance is a leading indicator of ad performance.**

## Core Idea
> *"Good organic performance correlates highly with good ad and outbound performance."*

The Social Media Scrape inverts the usual creative pipeline. Instead of producing ad creative and hoping it travels, the operator lets the customer base (and their own organic feed) produce candidates, sees what already worked at zero ad spend, then pays to amplify the winners. The node's mechanism: a tagging convention, a calendar-driven scrape rhythm, and a discipline of separating organic winners from the noise.

## Framework / Structure

### The two channels of inbound content
The scrape pulls from two streams in parallel.

**Customer stream** — content posted by customers about the business. Activated by giving the community a hashtag or a profile to tag.

**Operator/founder stream** — the founder's or brand's own existing posts that already performed organically. These are reusable as paid creative even though they were never produced as ads.

### The hashtag/tag convention
Every Marketing Machine deployment needs at least one designated tag the customer base can use. Hormozi's examples (verbatim from p. 9):

- **Gym Launch:** `#gymlord`, `#gymlaunch`, `#gymowner`
- **Prestige Labs:** `#poweredbyprestige`
- **Hormozi personally:** `#neverskipdessert`, `#acquisition`, `#100moffers`, `#100mleads` (and any post that simply tags the profile)

A profile tag is the minimum viable substitute if no hashtag exists. Without a tag or hashtag, the scrape has nothing reliable to query against.

### The scrape rhythm
The biggest operational risk is the 24-hour content (Instagram / Snapchat / Facebook stories). Hormozi calls this out specifically: **stories disappear within 24 hours**. The scrape calendar must be daily for ephemeral platforms and at least weekly for permanent posts.

### Studs vs. duds
Hormozi's discipline: separate the high performers from the average ones into distinct folders. The studs folder is the working pool the marketing team draws from for ads. The duds folder still gets saved (compliance, archive, future re-evaluation) but is not in active rotation.

The signal for "stud": the post's organic performance materially exceeded a typical post on the same account. Ranking can be by likes, shares, or saves depending on platform — Hormozi does not prescribe a specific metric, only the discipline of separating.

### Reuse logic
Hormozi's instruction is explicit: **use the best-performing organic images and videos as creative with already-performing ad copy**. The lever is creative-side substitution. Pair organic winners with proven copy; do not re-write copy for every new asset. This compresses the iteration loop.

## Strategic Implications

- **Organic is the cheapest A/B testing lab the business has.** Every customer post is an unpaid creative test. The operator who scrapes systematically is reading the results of an experiment they did not have to fund. Operators who do not scrape are running paid tests that organic already answered.
- **Reusing operator-stream posts collapses production cost.** A founder who has been posting for years has hundreds of organic-validated assets. Most have never been seen by a cold audience. Hormozi's note that "thousands of ads we made at Gym Launch" included repurposed organic-stream content captures this — the founder's existing feed is a back-catalog of ad inventory.
- **Tagging convention is the load-bearing piece.** Without it, the customer stream is a search problem the marketing team cannot solve. With it, scraping becomes a 30-minute weekly task. The single operational decision of choosing and publishing a hashtag is what makes the rest of the node viable.
- **Stories are time-bombed assets.** A 24-hour disappearance means a daily capture rhythm — or the asset is gone. Most operators discover this only after losing a flagship customer story. Build the daily reminder before launching the hashtag.
- **The stud/dud split is a quality filter, not a culling.** Saving the duds matters: the same asset that underperforms organically can occasionally over-perform paid (different audience, different surface). The discipline is to *prioritize* studs, not to delete duds.
- **Ad copy reuse is the other half of the lever.** Hormozi explicitly couples scraped creative with already-performing ad copy. The ad pipeline becomes: pull stud → drop into proven copy → run. This works because the variable being tested (visual) is being changed against a control (copy). Marketing Machine pairs naturally with [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]] for this reason.

## Practical Use

Social Media Scrape install checklist (synthesized from p. 9 + tear-out p. 28):

1. **Marketing — name the tag.** Pick one hashtag and one profile-tag convention for the brand. Publish it everywhere customers can see it (welcome emails, community pinned post, signature blocks, fulfillment communications).
2. **Marketing — set the scrape calendar.**
   - Daily: Instagram stories, Snapchat, Facebook stories, ephemeral platforms.
   - Weekly: permanent posts, tagged feed posts, mentions.
3. **Marketing — store the captures.** One folder structure. Use searchable filenames with the customer name, date, platform, and a short tag describing what the content shows.
4. **Marketing — run studs vs. duds.** Within the storage tree, separate posts that materially outperformed the account's typical organic baseline from those that did not. Studs is the active ad pool; duds is the archive.
5. **Marketing — also save your own stuff.** Search the operator's and founder's own feeds for posts that performed organically and save them. They are usable as creative without any new production.
6. **Marketing — pair stud creative with proven ad copy.** Drop the stud asset into existing winning ad-copy templates. Ship to test. See [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]] for the assembly logic.
7. **Marketing — capture everything you find from yourself, tags, and customers (images and videos).** This is the tear-out's tear-down: the weekly checklist line is "Social Media Saves: save images and stories on all platforms for yourself; search & save images and stories from your customers & their tags."

## Notes / Limits

- **Stories die fast.** Daily capture is not optional for ephemeral content — it is the entire mechanism. A weekly cadence loses six out of every seven story posts.
- **Tag fatigue is real.** Customers will not tag forever. Refresh the call-to-tag periodically: highlight customers who got featured, run small recognition for tagged posts, keep the loop visible.
- **Permission still applies.** Public posts do not automatically equal advertising rights. Even a tagged post should trigger a one-line "are you OK if we use this in our ads?" before paid amplification. Same compliance posture as Lifecycle.
- **Platform algorithm noise.** A post that organically crushed may have benefited from algorithmic timing rather than creative quality. Cross-check by running it as paid against a small budget before scaling — the same way [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]] tests new creative.
- **Brand-voice drift.** Repurposed customer content is in the customer's voice, not the brand's. That is the point — but if the customer's voice is wildly off-brand, the asset belongs in the duds archive even if engagement was strong.
