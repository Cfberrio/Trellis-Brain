# Hormozi — Testimonial Competition

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Marketing Machine — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Node 6 of the Marketing Machine. Defines a 1–2× yearly contest where customers submit raw iPhone-style testimonials and the winner is chosen by **measured ad performance over a 30-day window**. The mechanism produces a single large batch of customer-shot testimonial creative — the highest install-effort-to-output ratio in the entire Marketing Machine. Recommended as the **first node to install** for an operator starting from scratch.

## Core Idea
> *"You run a raw iPhone-style testimonial competition. People compete for the best one. And we measure the best by…drum roll… its performance as an ad. The one with the most conversions over 30 days wins."*

The Competition's structural innovation: the prize is awarded based on the asset's *external performance as an ad*, not on the operator's internal taste. This both (a) removes founder bias from selection, and (b) creates a customer-side incentive to make a video that genuinely converts — which is the only metric that matters anyway. The operator runs all submissions as paid creative for 30 days, picks the highest-converting, and announces the winner.

## Framework / Structure

### The cadence rule
Hormozi's explicit limit: *"people tire of them, so you can't do them very often, like once or twice a year max."* The competition is a high-impact, low-frequency event — running it more often dilutes participation and devalues the prize.

### The prize
A "crazy" award. Hormozi's example: **6 months of free service / product.** The prize must be:
- High enough perceived value to motivate submission effort.
- Tied to the operator's own offer (so the prize is also retention).
- Branded for visibility — the announcement of the winner becomes its own marketing moment.

### The submission requirements
Every submission must include:
- A **results template fill-in** (the operator designs this — typically the customer's metric snapshot before/after).
- A **video file** (raw, iPhone-style preferred — *"the raw-er the better"*).
- A community post tagging the community manager.
- The raw video file emailed to a dedicated address (e.g., `proof@yourco.com`).

Provided to the entrants:
- The previous winner's testimonial (or the operator's best testimonial to date) as the model.
- The [[Hormozi-6-Point-Testimonial-Script|6-point testimonial script]] as the structural template.

### The launch sequence
1. **Set the announcement date and the submission deadline.** Make both calendar-public so the team has commitment dates.
2. **Announce via every internal channel** — email list, group messages, community posts. The competition needs full surface area.
3. **Run a countdown** to the deadline.
4. **Personally invite the top 50 customers** and follow up with them individually. The top 50 (by results, by community engagement, by tenure) are the most likely to submit and the most likely to submit *quality*.
5. **Ask them to record in the moment.** Hormozi's note: *"asking them to do it the moment gets great results. (Like right now, it takes 5min just take out your phone so you don't have to think about it)."* Latency kills participation.

### The selection mechanism
This is the structural innovation, and the part most operators miss.

1. **Cut off submissions** at the deadline.
2. **Format every submission as an ad** and run it. Not a poll, not a vote — every submission becomes paid creative.
3. **Run the ads for 30 days.**
4. **Measure conversions** per submission across the 30-day window.
5. **The submission with the most conversions wins.**
6. **Announce the winner publicly.** This becomes the next marketing moment.

### Set the calendar reminder
Hormozi's specific operational note: set a calendar reminder thirty days in the future to remind the operator to count which ads did best and announce the winner. Without the reminder, the announcement slips, the loop doesn't close, and trust in the next year's competition erodes.

## Strategic Implications

- **The competition is the fastest path to customer-generated ad inventory.** A single 30-day run produces dozens of submissions, each functioning as ad creative. For an operator with no UGC pipeline, this single mechanism populates the ad account in weeks.
- **Measuring by ad performance removes the operator's own bias.** Operators consistently pick the testimonial they *like* — usually the most articulate, the most polished, the most metric-rich. Cold prospects often respond to different signals (energy, rawness, relatability). Letting the cold-traffic algorithm pick the winner aligns the contest with the actual job to be done.
- **"Raw-er the better" is a structural choice, not a stylistic one.** Polished testimonials feel like ads and get scrolled past. iPhone-style raw video reads as authentic. The competition's submission rules enforce the format that performs.
- **Customer-side incentive is real.** A customer who knows their video will be judged on conversions has an incentive to *make a video that converts*. This is fundamentally different from a customer asked to "leave a testimonial" — the latter optimizes for politeness, the former optimizes for performance.
- **Personal outreach to the top 50 is the participation lever.** Mass announcements alone produce 5–15 submissions. Adding personal invitations to the top 50 and asking them to record *in the moment* produces dozens. The top-50 list also tends to produce the highest-quality submissions, because they have the strongest results to show.
- **Annual cadence is the right cadence.** Hormozi's own ratio — 1 competition + 2 events per year = a big content drop every ~12 weeks. Quarterly competitions feel like spam; annual feels like a real event. The cadence shapes the prize value and the customer's anticipation.
- **The "format every submission as an ad" rule is the secret.** Most operators treat a competition as a contest with one winner. The Marketing Machine treats it as a creative-test sprint where the winner is just the lead asset — every submission feeds the ad account either way.

## Practical Use

Pre-launch (2–4 weeks out):
1. **Pick the prize.** 6 months free, a high-value bonus, a tier upgrade — something the customer base will pursue.
2. **Set the dates.** Announcement date, submission deadline, 30-day measurement window, winner-announce date.
3. **Build the submission template.** Results form + raw video upload instructions.
4. **Pre-write the announcement copy** for every channel.
5. **Compile the top-50 list** for personal outreach.
6. **Identify the reference asset** — last year's winner or the operator's best testimonial — and link it in the announcement.

Launch (announcement → deadline):
7. **Announce simultaneously across email, community, group messages, social.**
8. **Run the countdown** through the deadline.
9. **Personal-invite the top 50** with the in-the-moment ask.
10. **Repeat reminders weekly** through the submission window.

Post-deadline (30-day measurement):
11. **Cut off submissions.**
12. **Format every submission as an ad** per the canonical [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]].
13. **Run all submissions in paid** with consistent budget per asset.
14. **Set the 30-day reminder.**
15. **At day 30, measure conversions per submission, identify the winner, and announce publicly.**
16. **Continue running the strongest submissions** as evergreen creative beyond the 30-day window.

## Notes / Limits

- **Cadence discipline matters.** Running this more than 2× a year breaks the mechanism. The prize loses scarcity value, customers get fatigue, and the announcement stops being an event.
- **Equal ad budget per submission is non-negotiable.** A fair selection requires every submission to get the same paid spend over the same 30 days. Inconsistent budgeting biases the result and makes the winner-announcement feel rigged.
- **The 30-day window is the floor, not the ceiling.** Some submissions need longer to find their audience. Use 30 days for the contest decision; keep running the strong ones for months afterward.
- **In-the-moment ask is the high-yield request.** "Take out your phone right now, it's 5 minutes" produces submissions; "you should submit something this week" produces nothing. Latency kills.
- **Prize must be deliverable.** A 6-months-free prize that the operator would resent honoring (because the customer is the wrong fit) creates back-end pain. Pre-screen the eligible pool to prevent prize-induced regret.
- **Prepare for low submission counts the first year.** First-year competitions in any business produce fewer submissions than later years — there is no precedent, no past winner to model against. Use the operator's best testimonial as the reference and lean harder on top-50 personal invites.
- **Public winner announcement is itself ad inventory.** The "we ran a competition, here's the winner, here is what they did" video is its own ad. Don't treat the announcement as an internal event.
