# Hormozi — Event Capture Playbook

## Parent
- [[00-Book-Home|Marketing Machine — Book Home]]

## Purpose
Node 3 of the Marketing Machine. Defines the production playbook for converting in-person events — owned events, conferences, awards dinners, summits — into three distinct ad formats: stage ads, professional testimonials, and man-in-the-street reels. The node turns the cost of running events from a P&L line into a creative-supply mechanism.

## Core Idea
> *"Since the events cost a fortune, and I didn't sell anything, that media was the only way I could get returns! So I had to get pretty good at squeezing every last drop of marketing power from every event."*

Events are the single highest-density Marketing Machine source: a curated audience, controlled environment, captive subjects, and a finite production window. The playbook treats every event as a documentary shoot whose primary deliverable is creative inventory — independent of any sale that does or doesn't happen at the event itself. Hormozi's recommendation for any operator not already running events: do at least 1–2 per year, even if you sell nothing on stage.

## Framework / Structure

### The three event-ad formats

**Format 1 — Stage Ads.** Photo and short-clip content captured *of the operator on stage*, with the audience visible behind. Visual proof of size, scope, and credibility. Typically a still + a short clip, used as proof in subsequent ads ("here is the size of the room").

**Format 2 — Professional Testimonials.** Long-form, broadcast-quality testimonials recorded in a separate side room during the event, off the main floor. The flagship asset of the entire node. Uses [[Hormozi-6-Point-Testimonial-Script|the 6-point script]].

**Format 3 — Man in the Street.** Quick soundbite interviews captured on the event floor during breaks. One question, fast answers from many people, edited into rapid-fire highlight reels. Uses 10 pre-prepared questions (or the same 6-point script in rapid-fire form).

### Stage Ads — production rules
Hormozi's checklist (verbatim from p. 13):
- Get a picture while on stage with people celebrating behind. Visual proof of size and scope.
- Get multiple selfie images and a video if possible. **One take only — make it count.**
- Time it: end of day one, or before lunch on day two. Energy is highest, room is at peak engagement.

### Professional Testimonials — production rules

**Rooms and crew:**
- Two separate rooms: one for the event itself, one for testimonial recording. If two rooms aren't possible, take 2–3 extra hotel rooms set up for capture.
- At least one extra camera team. Sometimes two. **The testimonial team is the more important team — assign the better crew there.**
- Two camera operators per testimonial room. One runner to bring clients in and out.

**Equipment discipline:**
- Multiple mics, multiple cameras.
- **TEST. TEST. TEST.** Hormozi flags this as the single largest catastrophic-failure risk. *"The worst thing that happens is you lose audio or video from every testimonial for an entire event. You will want to kill everyone."*

**Subject selection and scheduling:**
- Pick the customers you want testimonials from *ahead of time*. Confirm in writing.
- Tell each person their testimonial will not occur during a big keynote and that they will receive notes for the few minutes they miss.
- Print a hit list on a clipboard. Check off names as captured.
- Announce at the event opening that the team will be doing testimonials. Introduce the handler so when shoulder-tapped during the event, the customer knows what is happening.

**Handler incentive:**
- Give the **customer** a bonus for leaving a testimonial.
- Give the **handler** an extra **$50 for every additional person captured beyond the hit list**. The handler often grabs adjacent people while collecting hit-list subjects. This is high-yield free expansion.

**Capture timing:**
- Avoid big keynotes.
- Maintain a "second list" for pushback customers — capture during morning, lunches, breaks, end of day.

**The script:** all professional testimonials run the [[Hormozi-6-Point-Testimonial-Script|6-point script]]. Two production-side details Hormozi specifies (p. 16):
- The hook is the most important part of the testimonial. Be explicit. Have the subject try a few different hooks to see what works better before they get into the story.
- Have the subject **say their name at the beginning of the video**. The camera team often does not know who the person is — saying the name on tape solves the matching problem in post.

**Output:** save, upload, add music, run.

### Man in the Street — production rules

**Crew:** if running this in addition to professional testimonials, hire a **second two-man camera team** specifically for it.

**Question discipline:**
- Prepare **10 core questions** ahead of time.
- Goal: 10+ responses per question.
- Each capture takes seconds. Output is a fast-hitting highlight reel of many people answering the *same* question.

**Hormozi's example questions (p. 16):**
- "How many new customers have you gained since you started?"
- "What were you making before X vs now?"
- "How much extra income has X added?"
- "How much weight have you lost since you started here?"
- "How much has your credit score improved?"
- "What was your old credit score and what is it now? From what to what?"

**Two output modes:**
1. **One question, many people.** Mass repetition of the same outcome statement. ("I lost 27 pounds." "I lost 42 pounds." "I lost 18 pounds." …)
2. **6-point rapid-fire.** Same person, all six questions in <2 minutes, weave the same characters across the testimonial reel. Combines the volume of MITS with the depth of a pro testimonial.

**Camera-team enablement:**
- Print the questions for the camera team.
- Below each question, leave a tally space — they mark off responses as they get them.
- Discipline: ask only one question until the target count is hit, then move to the next.

## Strategic Implications

- **Events that don't sell still pay back through media.** Hormozi explicitly didn't sell at Gym Launch events. The ROI was purely the media inventory captured. Operators who treat events as sales channels alone are missing the larger payoff. *"I just provided value. But, I did document as much of each event as I could so that I could use that media in my advertisements."*
- **The testimonial room is the event's flagship asset.** More important than the keynotes, the breakout sessions, or even the on-stage announcements. The room is the only place that produces permanent inventory; the rest is one-night-only. Resource it accordingly: better crew, better gear, better lighting.
- **Equipment failure is the highest-cost mistake in this node.** A single bad audio capture across an event wipes out a year of testimonial inventory. The TEST-TEST-TEST discipline is not paranoia — it is the only insurance against zero output.
- **The handler-bonus economy is a free volume multiplier.** $50 per extra capture is a rounding error against the ad value of a single testimonial that performs. Hormozi consistently uses "pay for surfacing" mechanics across the Marketing Machine ([[Hormozi-Communications-Scrape|Communications Scrape]] gift cards, this handler bonus, [[Hormozi-Bonus-And-Award-Mechanism|Bonus Unlocks]]).
- **Hook-first testimonial structure.** Most testimonials default to chronological ("before, then, then, then"). Hormozi puts the hook first — explicitly testing multiple hooks per subject. This is the same logic from the canonical [[../07-Hooks/Hormozi-Hook-Definition|Hook Definition]]: the first 3–5 seconds determine whether the rest of the asset is seen at all.
- **Man-in-the-street is the volume hedge.** Pro testimonials are deep and few. MITS is shallow and many. Together they cover both *credibility* (full story arc) and *consensus* (many independent voices saying the same thing). Running only one mode leaves half the proof palette on the table.
- **The 6-point rapid-fire variant is underrated.** The same script that drives 10–15 minute pro testimonials, run rapid-fire, produces a "mondo testimonial" that has the depth of a long testimonial and the pacing of a highlight reel. This is one of the highest-leverage edit choices in the playbook.

## Practical Use

Pre-event setup (1–3 weeks out):
1. Reserve the second room (testimonial room) or 2–3 extra hotel rooms.
2. Hire crew: 1× event camera team + 1× testimonial camera team (2 operators) + 1× handler/runner. Add a 2nd camera team if running MITS.
3. Pick the testimonial subject list. Confirm in writing. Prepare the on-stage announcement.
4. Define the 10 MITS questions. Print question sheets with tally spaces for the camera team.
5. Test all gear at the venue *before* day one. Multiple mics, multiple cameras, lighting, sound levels.

Day-of execution:
6. Open day one with the announcement: testimonials are happening, here is the handler, here is what to expect.
7. Capture stage ads at the right energy moment (end day 1 or pre-lunch day 2). One take.
8. Run the testimonial room continuously. Avoid keynote conflicts. Bump pushback customers to the secondary-time list.
9. Run MITS during breaks. One question per session until the count hits 10+.
10. Have every subject say their name at the start of the recording.
11. Capture multiple hooks per testimonial subject before going into the story.

Post-event (within 1 week):
12. Back up everything to redundant storage. *Redundant.*
13. Hand off to editing per the canonical [[../08-GOATed-Ads/Hormozi-Ad-Assembly-Process|Ad Assembly Process]].
14. Build outputs: full-length pro testimonials, mondo 6-point rapid-fire reels, single-question MITS reels (one per question), stage-photo proof clips.
15. Pay handler bonuses for off-list captures.

## Notes / Limits

- **No equipment redundancy = no testimonials.** Single-mic, single-camera setups will lose footage. Hormozi is explicit and unflinching about this — it is the one mistake that wipes out the entire node for an event.
- **Testimonial subjects must hear the plan.** Surprise tap-outs during a keynote create resentment. The pre-event written confirmation + on-stage announcement removes friction.
- **Hit list must include reserves.** Some confirmed subjects will no-show, push back, or not be in the right state to record. Build the list at 1.5× the target capture count.
- **Avoid over-coaching.** The hook-test is fine; the body of the answer should be the customer's own language. Over-scripting kills the authenticity that makes events the highest-trust source in the Marketing Machine.
- **MITS without target counts is noise.** The discipline is "ask only that question until you get the number of answers you want." A team that asks all 10 questions to one person and moves on produces no usable reels.
- **Stage-ad timing matters more than people think.** A pre-keynote shot has empty seats; a post-lunch shot has a half-empty room. End of day one or pre-lunch day two are the only reliable windows.
- **Cost frame.** Hormozi notes the events cost a fortune. The node's economics work because the *creative output* is what justifies the spend, not the on-event sales. Operators should budget testimonial production as a line item, not a stretch.
